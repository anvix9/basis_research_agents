## Recurring Patterns

- **Gap between international legal norms and enforcement capacity**
  Appearances: Post-WWII legal architecture (1945-1950), Geneva Conventions Additional Protocols (1977), Rome Statute implementation (1998-present), Gaza conflict (2008-present)
  Why: International law remains state-centric while seeking to constrain state behavior; enforcement depends on voluntary state cooperation precisely when states have strongest incentives not to cooperate; no sovereign enforcement authority in international system
- **Selective application of international law based on geopolitical power**
  Appearances: Nuremberg vs Tokyo trials (1945-1948), ICTY/ICTR vs Rwanda/Yugoslavia vs other conflicts (1993-present), ICC cases (2002-present), Gaza vs other conflicts (2008-present)
  Why: Powerful states and their allies protected from accountability while weak states and non-state actors prosecuted; international institutions lack independence from states that fund and legitimize them
- **Debate over whether educational/cultural destruction constitutes genocide or separate crime**
  Appearances: Raphael Lemkin's original genocide concept (1944), Genocide Convention negotiations (1948), Cultural destruction debates (1990s-2000s), Scholasticide theorization (2014-present)
  Why: Tension between physical destruction of group and destruction of group's cultural/intellectual existence; Genocide Convention's final text excluded cultural genocide; question resurfaces when educational systems systematically targeted
- **Colonial legal structures persisting after formal decolonization**
  Appearances: British Mandate law into Israeli law (1948-present), Continuation of Mandate-era land laws (1948-present), Legal status of Palestinian territories (1967-present), ICC jurisdiction debates over Palestine (2015-present)
  Why: Successor states selectively retain colonial legal frameworks that serve their interests; international law recognizes state sovereignty over territory without examining legitimacy of territorial acquisition; legal continuity serves power maintenance
- **Civilians and civilian infrastructure as strategic targets in asymmetric warfare**
  Appearances: WWII strategic bombing debates, Vietnam War, Yugoslav Wars (1990s), Iraq War (2003-2011), Gaza conflicts (2008-2025)
  Why: Asymmetric power dynamics incentivize targeting civilian morale and infrastructure when conventional military victory impossible; dominant powers rationalize civilian targeting through doctrines of 'military necessity' and 'human shields'; IHL protections systematically undermined in practice
- **Documentation and evidence collection by NGOs and civil society preceding legal action**
  Appearances: Holocaust documentation, Yugoslav Wars documentation (1990s), Rwanda genocide documentation, Gaza conflicts documentation (2008-present)
  Why: States and international institutions lack mechanisms for real-time evidence collection during conflicts; NGOs fill gap but face credibility challenges and accusations of bias; legal proceedings depend on NGO evidence but courts must assess reliability

## Methods Evolution

International humanitarian law methodology evolved from post-WWII state-centric treaty frameworks toward increasingly sophisticated multi-level governance involving international courts, NGOs, and academic documentation. Early period (1945-1977) focused on treaty negotiation and norm articulation through intergovernmental processes. Humanitarian Law Expansion phase (1977-1993) saw development of Additional Protocols addressing asymmetric conflicts and occupation, incorporating lessons from decolonization wars. International Criminal Justice Institutionalization (1993-2002) introduced judicial institutions (ICTY, ICTR, ICC) with prosecutorial capacity, shifting from purely normative frameworks to enforcement mechanisms. This required development of evidentiary standards, interpretive methodologies, and jurisdictional doctrines. Post-2002 period saw maturation of ICC jurisprudence, integration of NGO documentation methodologies into legal processes, and development of digital evidence collection tools. Educational Infrastructure as Military Target phase (2006-2014) saw emergence of specialized legal analysis addressing specific categories of civilian infrastructure, moving beyond general civilian protection principles. Scholasticide Theorization phase (2014-present) integrated post-colonial theory, indigenous rights frameworks, and decolonization analysis into international humanitarian law scholarship, challenging state-centric assumptions. Methods increasingly combine legal analysis, empirical documentation, satellite imagery, digital forensics, survivor testimony, and theoretical frameworks from multiple disciplines. Contemporary methodology treats international law as embedded in power relations rather than neutral arbiter, examining how legal categories themselves reflect and reproduce colonial structures.
