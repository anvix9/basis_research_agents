## Overview

The proposals address a research landscape marked by profound philosophical fragmentation and untested assumptions about intelligence, consciousness, and AI's relationship to human existence. Current debates operate in isolated disciplinary silos: philosophers argue about intrinsic versus derived intentionality without empirical tests, neuroscientists map consciousness substrates without engaging conceptual frameworks, AI researchers optimize rational agents without questioning whether rationality exhausts intelligence, and critical theorists examine collective consciousness while ignoring computational models entirely. Meanwhile, non-Western epistemologies—Taoist attunement, Ubuntu relationality, Indigenous ecological knowledge—remain completely absent from AI evaluation criteria, rendering all current assessments potentially parochial rather than universal.

The proposals fall into four categories: (1) methodological frameworks to bridge disciplinary divides and test untestable debates, (2) alternative intelligence paradigms that challenge Western computational assumptions, (3) empirical investigations of consciousness components that sidestep the deadlocked hard problem, and (4) normative frameworks for human flourishing that current capability-focused AI research ignores. Several proposals deliberately revive dismissed approaches—Thorndike's mechanistic learning, embodiment requirements, developmental trajectories—whose vindication or falsification by modern AI would reshape foundational assumptions. The highest-promise proposals either create empirical adjudication methods for philosophical deadlocks (turning metaphysics into testable hypotheses) or expose hidden assumptions in dominant paradigms (revealing that "intelligence = rationality" or "consciousness is unitary" are unvalidated axioms rather than established facts).
