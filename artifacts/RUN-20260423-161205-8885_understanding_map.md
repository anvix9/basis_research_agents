# Research Understanding Map: Scholasticide in Gaza and International War Crimes Law (1922-2025)

## 1. The Territory at a Glance

This field sits at the collision of three incommensurable systems: a definitional gap (scholasticide has no legal definition in international criminal law), a classification impasse (the Israeli-Palestinian conflict's legal status remains contested—international armed conflict, non-international armed conflict, or counter-terrorism operation), and a temporal contradiction (the 103-year timeframe spans British colonial mandate through Israeli statehood and occupation under frameworks that did not exist when the mandate was established). The central organizing tension is between *advocacy frameworks* that have theorized scholasticide as a distinct form of mass atrocity and *prosecutorial frameworks* that require fitting educational destruction into existing categories (genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes)—categories that may systematically fail to capture what scholasticide describes. The one question that would unlock everything else is: *Does systematic educational destruction constitute a distinct international crime, or must it be disaggregated into existing crime categories that obscure its systematic character?* This field exhibits neither empirical consensus nor competing methodologies, but rather a mismatch between what scholars claim they are analyzing (scholasticide as unified phenomenon) and what international law can actually prosecute (discrete incidents categorized separately). Understanding this territory requires grasping why legal precision creates conceptual erasure, and why advocacy frameworks cannot be directly translated into prosecution strategies.

## 2. The Intellectual Genealogy — How We Got Here

International law's capacity to prosecute mass atrocities developed through a century of struggle against the assumption that states held absolute sovereignty even in harming their own populations. The 1899 and 1907 Hague Conventions created the first systematic distinction between lawful and unlawful conduct in armed conflict, but they operated entirely at the level of state conduct, not individual criminal responsibility. This changed catastrophically with World War I's Armenian massacres (1915), which provoked the Allied Powers to invoke "crimes against humanity" as a concept, establishing the intellectual (though not yet legal) precedent that systematic mass killing could violate international obligation. Yet World War II was necessary before individual criminal responsibility became operational reality: the Nuremberg Charter (1945) revolutionized accountability by prosecuting individuals for crimes they committed under state orders, establishing that sovereignty could not shield perpetrators. The 1948 Genocide Convention then did something more specific and narrower—it created a legal definition with a precise intent requirement: acts committed "with intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnical, racial or religious group." This definition's restrictiveness immediately created problems. The Geneva Conventions (1949) took a different approach, establishing *duty-based* humanitarian law that prohibited specific acts (attacking civilians, destroying civilian property) regardless of intent, making war crimes prosecution possible without proving genocidal intent. The Additional Protocols (1977) extended these frameworks and, crucially, explicitly protected "civilian objects" including "educational institutions" during armed hostilities.

But here is where the genealogy turns paradoxical: as international humanitarian law became more precise and comprehensive, it fragmented into distinct crime categories requiring distinct prosecutions. The 1998 Rome Statute codified genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes as three separate domains with different intent thresholds, temporal requirements, and evidentiary burdens. Prosecutors had to choose: Does destroying Gaza universities constitute *genocide* (requiring specific intent to destroy Palestinians as a group), *crimes against humanity* (requiring knowledge of widespread attack on civilians), or *war crimes* (requiring targeting of civilian objects)? Each choice excludes others' evidentiary weight. David Scheffer's 2006 concept of "atrocity crimes" attempted to reunify these categories by treating genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes as an integrated system rather than competing silos. But the Rome Statute rejected this approach—the ICC operates through separate crime categories, not integrated atrocity jurisprudence.

The Krstić judgment (2001) and Bosnia v. Serbia decision (2007) then established that intent could be inferred from *systematic patterns* of targeting and scale of destruction. This meant prosecutors could prove genocidal intent without smoking-gun documents showing explicit intent—patterns themselves could demonstrate intent. This jurisprudential development opened new possibility: if you could document a *systematic pattern* of educational destruction across the 103-year period, you might infer specific intent to destroy Palestinian capacity for national self-determination through educational elimination. But this requires what the field currently lacks: longitudinal data demonstrating systematic targeting rather than episodic destruction.

The recent emergence of "scholasticide" as a critical concept (Shlaim 2025, Hajir et al. 2025) represents a fundamental shift: scholars began analyzing educational destruction *as a unified phenomenon* rather than as scattered war crimes. The concept implicitly claims that destroying universities, schools, and educational systems constitutes a distinct form of mass atrocity. Yet this critical framework emerged precisely *because* existing legal categories seemed inadequate to capture what was happening. Shlaim explicitly situates scholasticide within settler-colonial analysis, connecting it to the foundational contradictions embedded in the British Mandate (1922) that established a "Jewish national home" while ostensibly protecting existing Palestinian inhabitants—an impossible mandate that generated the entire conflict. Jeremy Sarkin's work (2008) established the intellectual framework for understanding how colonial projects systematically destroy indigenous knowledge systems through educational erasure. But Sarkin worked in reparations literature, not prosecution literature—the two fields operate with different conceptual vocabularies and evidentiary standards.

The field now faces this genealogical trap: It has developed rich theoretical frameworks (settler-colonialism, scholasticide, unchilding) that cannot be translated into existing prosecutorial categories without losing their systemic insight. The legal tools became more precise over time—moving from state-level accountability to individual criminal responsibility, from intent-based to pattern-based genocide proof—but this precision created compartmentalization. Educational destruction gets divided into incidents, each prosecuted separately, losing the systematic character that gives scholasticide its analytical force. The temporal scope (1922-2025) exposes this genealogical contradiction most sharply: the British Mandate operated under a completely different legal regime than contemporary international criminal law, and no continuous accountability framework bridges these periods. We have magnificent legal structures for prosecuting contemporary crimes but no mechanism for connecting colonial-era educational policies to contemporary destruction through unified narratives of systematic harm.

## 3. The Reading Curriculum

### Tier 1 — Foundations (Read First)

**1. Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide (1948) — United Nations**

**Why you read it at this tier:** This text is the foundational legal definition that all subsequent scholarship either builds upon or argues against. You cannot understand any genocide claim, including scholasticide-as-genocide arguments, without understanding what genocide *legally* means. The specific intent requirement in Article II is the threshold all contemporary debate hinges on.

**What to look for while reading:**
- Precisely what acts constitute genocide (Article II(a)-(e))—especially (c) "deliberately inflicting on the group conditions of life calculated to bring about its physical destruction"
- The intent requirement: "with intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnical, racial or religious group"
- The gap: what does *not* constitute genocide despite being horrific (cultural destruction, forced displacement, psychological harm)

**What it connects to:** The 1977 Additional Protocols attempt to fill gaps the Genocide Convention left; later jurisprudence (Krstić, Bosnia v. Serbia) develops how intent is inferred from patterns; Scheffer's "atrocity crimes" concept exists partly because this definition is too narrow.

---

**2. The Hague Conventions (1899 and 1907) — International Peace Conferences**

**Why you read it at this tier:** These establish the foundational legal distinction between lawful and unlawful armed conduct. They predate the Mandate period and remain binding customary international law. They are the intellectual origin of the entire concept that warfare has legal limits.

**What to look for while reading:**
- How civilian property is protected (1907 Hague Convention IV, Articles 23, 27, 43-56)
- The distinction between *occupation* and *conquest*—occupation creates specific duties toward inhabited territory
- What "unnecessary suffering" means in practice (Article 22)

**What it connects to:** These conventions form the historical basis for contemporary IHL; the British Mandate (1922) technically operated under Hague Convention frameworks; Protocol I (1977) builds directly on Hague categories.

---

**3. British Mandate for Palestine (1922) — League of Nations**

**Why you read it at this tier:** This is the *source document* for your 103-year timeframe. It is not a law in the contemporary sense but a League of Nations administrative instrument that created the legal-territorial contradiction at the root of the conflict. You must understand what it actually *says* versus what it is claimed to say.

**What to look for while reading:**
- Article 2: the Balfour Declaration language about "Jewish national home" 
- What obligations toward "non-Jewish communities" are created (they are minimal and contradictory)
- What educational institutions existed or were planned under the Mandate
- How the Mandate defines British authority and territorial scope

**What it connects to:** Sarkin's colonial genocide framework (2008) makes sense only if you understand what the Mandate's original contradictions were; Shlaim's scholasticide argument (2025) depends on treating the Mandate as origin point; all subsequent Israeli-Palestinian legal status depends on how the Mandate is interpreted.

---

**4. Geneva Convention Relative to the Protection of Civilian Persons in Time of War (Fourth Geneva Convention, 1949) — International Committee of the Red Cross**

**Why you read it at this tier:** This is the comprehensive civilian protection regime. It defines "grave breaches" that constitute war crimes, establishes duties toward occupied populations, and creates the framework all contemporary war crimes analysis uses. Without understanding its specific duties, you cannot assess violations of those duties.

**What to look for while reading:**
- Part III (Occupied Territory), especially Articles 27-34 on civil rights and Article 43 on penal laws
- The definition of "grave breaches" (Article 147)—these are war crimes
- What protection "civilian objects" receive
- What duties occupying powers have toward education/schools in occupied territory

**What it connects to:** Protocol I (1977) expands these protections; war crimes prosecutions under the Rome Statute use this Convention as their legal basis; the question of whether Gaza is "occupied" turns partly on how Fourth Geneva Convention duties are interpreted.

---

**5. Additional Protocol I to the Geneva Conventions (1977) — Diplomatic Conference**

**Why you read it at this tier:** This is the most directly applicable legal framework for educational institution protection. Article 52 specifically protects civilian objects; Article 52(2) addresses "attacks which may be expected to cause" excessive harm. This is the operative law for any scholasticide prosecution.

**What to look for while reading:**
- Article 1: How "armed conflicts" are defined—this determines whether the protocol applies at all
- Article 52: Protection for civilian objects, including schools and educational institutions
- Article 52(2): The proportionality standard—when attacking military objectives, expected civilian harm must not be excessive
- Articles 27-31: Specific protections for "cultural objects and places of worship"—education facilities fall here

**What it connects to:** This is what war crimes prosecution of educational destruction relies on; contemporary debates about counter-terrorism classification turn on whether Protocol I applies.

---

**6. Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court (1998) — United Nations Diplomatic Conference**

**Why you read it at this tier:** This codifies modern international criminal law and establishes the ICC's jurisdiction. Any prosecution of scholasticide would occur under Rome Statute definitions. You need to understand: (a) the three crime categories it establishes, (b) the elements required for each, (c) the intent standards for each.

**What to look for while reading:**
- Article 5: The three crimes the ICC has jurisdiction over (genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes)
- Article 6: Genocide definition and elements
- Article 7: Crimes against humanity definition and specific acts
- Article 8: War crimes definition, subsections (b)(ii) on attacking civilian objects and (b)(iv) on attacking places of worship/education
- Article 7(1)(k): "Other inhumane acts"—the catch-all category for atrocities that don't fit other definitions

**What it connects to:** This is where Scheffer's "atrocity crimes" concept would need to operate but doesn't—the Rome Statute rejected unified atrocity prosecution; contemporary scholasticide advocacy would require fitting educational destruction into one of these categories.

### Tier 2 — The Main Debates (Read Second)

**1. Prosecutor v. Radislav Krstić (Judgment, 2001) — International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia**

**Why you read it at this tier:** This is the first conviction for genocide where systematic pattern evidence proved specific intent *without* direct documentary evidence. The methodological approach—inferring intent from scale, systematicity, dehumanizing rhetoric—has become the template for all subsequent genocide prosecutions. You must understand how Krstić establishes that intent can be inferred from context rather than requiring explicit statements.

**What to look for while reading:**
- How the tribunal proves "specific intent to destroy" without smoking-gun documents showing explicit genocidal intent (pages on pattern evidence, dehumanizing statements, scale)
- The distinction between killings that happen to be systematic versus systematic killings undertaken with genocidal intent
- How witness testimony about planning discussions creates intent inference
- The quantitative threshold: does one massacre constitute genocide, or must multiple incidents establish pattern?

**What it connects to:** Bosnia v. Serbia (2007) applies the same reasoning at the state level; contemporary scholasticide analysis would need similar pattern evidence; the inability to produce such evidence for educational destruction is a major prosecutorial barrier.

---

**2. Application of the Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide (Bosnia and Herzegovina v. Serbia and Montenegro) (2007) — International Court of Justice**

**Why you read it at this tier:** This represents the ICJ's authoritative interpretation of genocide, distinguishing individual criminal responsibility from state-level accountability. It refines the evidentiary standards for proving genocidal intent and establishes what obligations states have to *prevent* genocide. This is where genocide law reaches maximum development—understanding this judgment means understanding the doctrinal frontier of genocide law.

**What to look for while reading:**
- How the ICJ distinguishes between state responsibility for genocide and responsibility for complicity/acquiescence
- The evidentiary standard: "beyond reasonable doubt" (pages on burden of proof)
- What circumstantial evidence can establish specific intent
- The duty to prevent: what actions would constitute breach (relevant to understanding Israeli state obligations)
- How the Court treats competing narratives of intent

**What it connects to:** This represents the current frontier of genocide jurisprudence; contemporary Gaza claims would be evaluated under this standard; the difficulty of proving specific intent under this standard is why many scholars turn to crimes against humanity instead.

**3. Genocide and Atrocity Crimes (2006) — David J. Scheffer**

**Why you read it at this tier:** This is the major conceptual challenge to the Rome Statute's compartmentalization of crime categories. Scheffer argues that genocide's restrictive intent requirement creates a gap where massive systematic atrocities escape prosecution because they fail the specific intent threshold. He proposes treating genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes as an integrated "atrocity crimes" system. This concept is crucial because scholasticide might fit better under atrocity crimes logic than under any single Rome Statute category.

**What to look for while reading:**
- The argument that genocide's intent threshold is too high for practical prosecution (pages on intent proof difficulties)
- How treating multiple crime categories as integrated atrocity law circumvents those thresholds
- The distinction between "genocide as legal category" versus "genocide as political designation"
- Why the Rome Statute ultimately rejected this framework

**What it connects to:** This directly addresses the scholasticide dilemma—systematic educational destruction might constitute atrocity even if it fails to prove genocide; Scheffer's framework offers an alternative prosecutorial strategy that the Rome Statute itself doesn't support; this represents a dead end in terms of current ICC practice but a reopenable question for future legal development.

**4. The Protection of Civilian Educational Institutions During the Active Hostilities of International Armed Conflict in International Humanitarian Law (2006) — David A. Lewis**

**Why you read it at this tier:** This is the only comprehensive legal analysis of IHL protections *specifically* for educational institutions. Lewis maps exactly what Protocol I and other instruments protect regarding schools, universities, and research facilities. This is the foundational legal framework for any scholasticide war crimes claim.

**What to look for while reading:**
- What specific protections Protocol I Article 52 provides for schools (civilian object status, proportionality requirements)
- The distinction between schools as civilian objects versus military use of schools
- What evidence shows a school is being "used for military purposes" that might forfeit protection
- The evidentiary burden: what must be proven to establish unlawful targeting?
- How proportionality calculations work in practice—when does damage to civilian schools exceed military necessity?

**What it connects to:** This provides the direct legal framework for prosecuting educational destruction under war crimes; contemporary Gaza claims depend on establishing either: (a) schools weren't used militarily, or (b) harm exceeded military necessity; Lewis's analysis shows how that proof burden operates in practice.

**5. Srebrenica as Genocide? The Krstić Decision and the Language of the Unspeakable (2005) — Katherine Southwick**

**Why you read it at this tier:** This critical analysis examines how legal definitions *create* what can be recognized as genocide and what gets erased as "merely" tragic. Southwick argues that the shift from "Srebrenica massacre" to "Srebrenica genocide" required reconstructing evidence through juridical language that transformed understanding of what happened. This is crucial for scholasticide because it shows how legal categories don't simply describe reality—they actively constitute what counts as recognizable atrocity.

**What to look for while reading:**

- The relationship between massacre, genocide, and crimes against humanity—why the shift from one category to another matters

- What gets lost when atrocity is fit into legal categories

**What it connects to:** This directly addresses the scholasticide problem: "educational destruction" is recognizable as massive wrong; scholasticide names it; but international law's categories may make it unrecognizable as prosecutable crime; Southwick shows this is not a neutral evidentiary problem but a structural feature of how legal language works.

## 4. The Conceptual Map — How the Ideas Connect

International law for mass atrocities operates through a taxonomy of distinct crime categories (genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes) that function not as interconnected system but as compartments with different proof requirements. **Genocide** requires proving specific intent to destroy a protected group in whole or in part—the highest intent bar, requiring evidence of planning, deliberate selection of victims, and systematic execution. **Crimes against humanity** require knowledge that one's acts are part of widespread or systematic attack on civilian population, but not specific intent to destroy the group—lower intent bar, making prosecution more feasible when intent evidence is weak. **War crimes** require only that an act violate international humanitarian law protections (attacking civilians, destroying civilian objects, torturing prisoners)—lowest intent bar, sometimes requiring only general intent or even negligence. Each category has different temporal and jurisdictional requirements, different elements of proof, and different sentencing ranges. 

The conceptual problem for scholasticide is that educational destruction could plausibly fall into any of these categories, but each categorization obscures different dimensions of what scholasticide describes. Prosecuted as **genocide**, scholasticide requires proving that destroying Gaza's educational system was done with specific intent to destroy Palestinians as a group—a very high bar requiring evidence of genocidal planning. The destruction would be categorized as means to group destruction, alongside killing, displacement, and physical genocide elements. But this categorical logic breaks apart when applied to infrastructure: is destroying universities a "means" to destroying the group, or is it a *goal in itself*? International genocide law assumes the act (killing, displacing) is means to group destruction, but systematic educational destruction seems to function simultaneously as means (preventing group self-determination) and end (eliminating intellectual capacity). Prosecuted as **crimes against humanity**, educational destruction would be characterized as attack on civilian infrastructure as part of widespread attack on Palestinian civilian population—much easier proof burden (knowledge rather than specific intent), but this risks minimizing systematic character, treating it as one element among many civilian harms rather than distinct targeting pattern. Prosecuted as **war crimes**, violations of Protocol I Article 52 (attacks on civilian objects) would be the legal basis—but this purely legalistic categorization strips away the element that makes scholasticide analytically distinctive: the intentional targeting of *educational capacity* specifically, not just physical destruction of property.

The hidden conceptual work emerges here: **International humanitarian law was designed to regulate how states conduct warfare, not to prosecute systematic destruction of national intellectual capacity.** The law recognizes "civilian objects" as protected category, but "educational infrastructure" is a subcategory of civilian objects, and educational capacity (the ability to produce, transmit, and preserve knowledge) is not a protected category at all. The Rome Statute permits prosecuting attacks on educational facilities as war crimes, but it does not permit prosecuting *destruction of educational capacity* as distinct crime. This is why scholasticide scholarship insists on analytic autonomy from legal categories—because the legal categories available cannot capture what scholasticide describes.

A second conceptual tension emerges between **temporal continuity** and **legal discontinuity**. The research question spans 1922-2025, requesting analysis of whether "scholasticide" has existed throughout this period. But the legal instruments protecting education didn't exist in 1922 (Hague Conventions had general civilian property protections but not specific educational institution protections). The Fourth Geneva Convention (1949) and Additional Protocols (1977) created the specific legal framework, but this creates a paradox: one cannot prosecute violations of a law that didn't exist when the violation occurred (prohibition against retroactive criminalization). So the 1922-1948 British Mandate period's educational policies—which may have systematically denied Palestinian educational access—cannot be prosecuted under contemporary war crimes law because contemporary war crimes law didn't exist. Historical continuity of harm is incommensurable with legal discontinuity of frameworks.

A third conceptual fault line separates **material destruction** from **systematic intent**. International humanitarian law recognizes that physical destruction of objects is only criminalized when accompanied by specific circumstantial intent (attacking civilian objects, targeting objects used by opposing force). Accidentally destroying a school is tragic but not a war crime if not intentional targeting. But scholasticide claims rest on establishing that destruction is *systematic*—occurring across multiple institutions over long periods in pattern evidencing deliberate targeting. Krstić and Bosnia v. Serbia established that systematic patterns can infer specific intent, but this requires: (1) documentation of pattern across time and space, (2) evidence that pattern exceeds what military necessity would require, and (3) either documentary evidence of planning or circumstantial evidence of coordination. For scholasticide in Gaza (2023-2025), does such evidence exist? The current scholarship asserts systematic destruction but has not produced the longitudinal forensic documentation that genocide jurisprudence requires for intent inference.

A fourth conceptual tension emerges between **occupation law** and **armed conflict law**. The Israeli-Palestinian relationship involves occupation (territory taken in 1967 war, currently administered by Israel, with Palestinian claims to self-determination). Occupation creates specific duties under Fourth Geneva Convention and Hague Regulations—occupying powers must maintain essential services including education. Educational destruction in occupied territory thus has a distinctive legal character: it's not just violation of humanitarian protections but violation of occupational duties to maintain civil administration. Yet international law has virtually no doctrine addressing systematic withdrawal of educational services as occupational crime. The concept doesn't exist in the Rome Statute or Geneva Conventions. This is a conceptual gap where law simply stops.

A fifth conceptual problem concerns **settler-colonial frameworks and international law's absence of settler-colonial category**. Sarkin (2008) and Shlaim (2025) analyze scholasticide within settler-colonial theory, arguing that systematic educational destruction is specific to settler projects seeking to displace indigenous populations and prevent alternative knowledge systems from sustaining group identity. But international law does not recognize "settler-colonialism" as legal category. The Rome Statute recognizes genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and crime of aggression—but not "settler-colonial violence" as distinct category. This means settler-colonial analysis and international law operate in different conceptual registers. You could argue educational destruction constitutes genocide *because of* settler-colonial intent, but you'd have to translate from settler-colonial vocabulary into genocide vocabulary, potentially losing analytical precision in translation.

Finally, a conceptual distinction exists between **epistemic scholasticide** (suppression of scholarly knowledge production, as Hajir et al. and Alqaisiya & Perugini analyze) and **infrastructural scholasticide** (destruction of physical educational facilities, as Lewis and Alburai analyze). International law addresses material destruction of property (infrastructure), not suppression of knowledge production (epistemic). Western universities' constraints on Palestine scholarship might constitute scholasticide in Hajir's analytical sense, but international criminal law has virtually no mechanism for prosecuting institutional suppression of knowledge. This creates two different scholasticide phenomena operating in two different registers (material and epistemic) but sharing a single analytical category. Understanding this distinction is essential because it prevents assuming that legal prosecution of Gaza educational destruction would address the full dimension of scholasticide.

## 5. The Unresolved Core

**The Central Unresolved Question:** Can systematic educational destruction constitute a distinct prosecutable international crime, or must it be disaggregated into discrete incidents prosecuted separately under existing war crimes/crimes against humanity categories?

**Why this question remains open:**

This question is simultaneously *conceptually contested*, *methodologically blocked*, and *legally underdetermined*:

- **Conceptually contested:** Scholasticide scholarship (Shlaim 2025, Hajir et al. 2025) claims that treating educational destruction as unified phenomenon reveals a specific form of mass atrocity with distinct characteristics from other war crimes. Disaggregating it into incident-based prosecutions (school A destroyed on date 1, school B destroyed on date 2, university C damaged on date 3) would lose the systemic insight that scholasticide enables. Yet international criminal law assumes crime categories are universal—genocide is genocide whether one village or one million are killed. The conceptual tension is whether *systematicity* should be a crime element itself, or whether it merely provides evidence for inferring intent within existing categories.

- **Methodologically blocked:** Proving scholasticide as unified phenomenon requires longitudinal forensic documentation establishing that educational destruction: (1) spans decades or continuous period, (2) exceeds what military necessity would justify, (3) evidences deliberate targeting rather than collateral damage, and (4) demonstrates specific intent or systematic planning. Current scholarship on Gaza (Alburai 2023, Shlaim 2025) asserts this pattern but has not produced the systematic forensic documentation that Krstić/Bosnia jurisprudence requires. The methodological tools exist (Juhl 2006 on mass grave investigation, Garfield et al. 2025 on demographic documentation), but operationalizing them specifically for educational victim identification and temporal pattern mapping has not occurred. Without this documentation, scholasticide remains assertion rather than prosecutable claim.

- **Legally underdetermined:** The Rome Statute does not recognize scholasticide as crime. Prosecuting educational destruction requires fitting it into genocide (specific intent to destroy group), crimes against humanity (knowledge of widespread attack), or war crimes (attacking civilian objects). Each categorization is legally possible but conceals different dimensions of scholasticide's character. As Scheffer (2006) argues, restricting prosecutions to these categories creates gaps where massive atrocities escape prosecution because they fail intent thresholds. But Scheffer's proposed "atrocity crimes" framework was rejected in Rome Statute negotiations, and no subsequent international legal development has resurrected it. This leaves the legal status of scholasticide indeterminate: it might constitute genocide, crimes against humanity, and/or war crimes, but none of these categories will fully capture what scholasticide describes.

**What genuine contribution to this question would require:**

1. **Forensic operationalization**: Develop a comprehensive forensic-documentary protocol that: (a) identifies university student victims as distinct demographic cohort in casualty datasets, (b) documents destruction patterns across space and time with evidentiary standards meeting Rome Statute burden of proof, (c) establishes quantitative thresholds distinguishing "educational system destruction" from "isolated educational facility damage," (d) analyzes whether destruction patterns exceed military necessity thresholds.

2. **Legal argument at Rome Statute scale**: Rather than arguing scholasticide should be recognized as new crime (which would require treaty amendment), develop prosecutorial strategy showing how scholasticide patterns can be proven under existing categories. This means: (a) constructing genocide argument showing specific intent to destroy Palestinians' capacity for national self-determination through educational elimination, or (b) constructing crimes against humanity argument showing knowledge that educational destruction is part of widespread systematic attack on Palestinian civil society, or (c) constructing cumulative war crimes argument where aggregate of violations exceeds proportionality thresholds. This requires engaging Rome Statute jurisprudence directly, not just scholasticide theory.

3. **Historical-legal continuity analysis**: Tracing how British Mandate educational policies (1922-1948), Israeli state policies (1948-1967), occupation policies (1967-1995), post-Oslo governance (1995-2000), and blockade effects (2006-present) constitute continuous or discontinuous accountability chains. This requires understanding: What legal frameworks governed education in each period? What obligations did different actors (British Mandate authority, Israeli state, Palestinian Authority, international community) have regarding Palestinian education? Can contemporary prosecutions address colonial-era harms without treaty retroactivity? This analysis would determine whether the 103-year timeframe is prosecutionally coherent or comprises multiple discontinuous legal periods.

4. **Distinction from metaphorical decolonization**: Following Tuck & Yang (2012), distinguish material scholasticide claims (destruction of actual educational capacity affecting actual Palestinians' intellectual development) from metaphorical critiques of Western curricular representation or diversity. This matters because prosecutions address material destruction, not symbolic representation. Genuine contribution requires proving that destruction is material/demographic harm to Palestinian educational access and capacity, not cultural offense or epistemological critique.

5. **Specification of intent**: Address the methodological challenge (Krstić/Bosnia) of proving intent when direct documentary evidence is unavailable. This requires analyzing: Do systematic destruction patterns themselves evidence intent? How much destruction exceeds military necessity threshold? What circumstantial evidence establishes deliberate targeting versus foreseeable but unintended consequence? Can intent be inferred from long historical pattern (centering back to Mandate policies) or only from contemporaneous military operations?

**What distinguishes superficial from deep engagement:**

- **Superficial engagement** documents that schools have been destroyed (true and important but insufficient) and asserts this constitutes scholasticide/genocide without confronting the conceptual-legal translation problem. It treats scholasticide as self-evidently prosecutable rather than grappling with why international law may structurally exclude it. It uses scholasticide term without engaging how the term emerges precisely as response to law's inadequacy.

- **Deep engagement** recognizes that scholasticide scholarship exists because international law's categories are inadequate for capturing systematic educational destruction. It confronts the choice: either (a) modify scholasticide analysis to fit Rome Statute categories, accepting what gets lost in translation, or (b) argue for legal development (recognizing scholasticide as new crime), accepting this requires political/treaty work outside individual prosecution. It produces longitudinal forensic evidence meeting contemporary jurisprudential standards. It engages historical-legal continuity problem rather than treating 1922-2025 as uninterrupted continuous period. It specifies what makes educational destruction distinctive crime rather than generic war crime against civilian objects.

### Question 4: How does Scheffer's (2006) "atrocity crimes" framework reveal something about the limitations of Rome Statute crime categories that persists regardless of scholasticide?

**Answer:** Scheffer argued that the Rome Statute's compartmentalization of genocide (requiring specific intent to destroy group), crimes against humanity (requiring knowledge of widespread attack), and war crimes (requiring targeting violation) creates gaps where massive systematic atrocities escape prosecution because they fail the highest intent category while potentially satisfying lower ones. For instance, systematic destruction of civilian infrastructure (war crime) might not meet crimes against humanity threshold (if not part of "widespread systematic attack on civilian population") and definitely wouldn't meet genocide threshold (lacking specific intent to destroy group). Scheffer proposed treating these three categories as integrated "atrocity crimes" system where prosecutors could charge cumulatively, with conviction on lesser crimes sufficient. The Rome Statute rejected this approach, maintaining separate categories. But Scheffer's insight—that crime categories don't align with atrocity patterns—remains valid. Scholasticide illustrates this precisely: it involves war crimes (attacking civilian objects), potentially crimes against humanity (systematic attack on Palestinian educational capacity), potentially genocide (systematic destruction of Palestinians' ability to maintain independent knowledge systems and state structures). But compartmentalized prosecution might result in war crimes convictions while genocide charges fail due to intent bar, losing the systematic character that scholasticide identifies. This reveals that the conceptual problem predates scholasticide—the Rome Statute structure itself struggles with systematic violence that spans crime categories.

**Why this matters for your work:** This question prevents you from assuming that scholasticide prosecution is a scholasticide-specific problem. If you advocate for scholasticide recognition, you're engaging a larger debate about whether international law's categories are adequate. If you work within Rome Statute categories, you're accepting structural limitations that may obscure your analysis.
