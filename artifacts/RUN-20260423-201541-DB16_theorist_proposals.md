## Overview

The proposal landscape addresses a complete void in cardiovascular pathophysiology research: the intersection of connective tissue disorders and intercellular electrical communication. Current understanding of Marfan syndrome, Loeys-Dietz syndrome, and related acropathies focuses almost exclusively on structural matrix defects while entirely ignoring the electrical coupling machinery that coordinates cellular function in cardiac and vascular tissues. This represents not merely a gap but a paradigmatic blind spot created by disciplinary boundaries between structural biology and electrophysiology.

The proposals span from foundational empirical characterization (establishing whether connexin alterations exist at all) to mechanistic pathway investigation (linking matrix defects to electrical communication disruption) to temporal dynamics (determining causality versus consequence). They deliberately bridge methodological divides by integrating structural and functional approaches, challenge implicit assumptions about independence of matrix and electrical systems, and exploit the natural experiment provided by syndromes with distinct molecular etiologies but convergent cardiovascular phenotypes. Several proposals target specific mechanistic hypotheses—TGF-β regulation of connexins, mechanotransduction pathways, cytoskeletal intermediaries—that could explain how primary genetic defects in matrix components translate into electrical dysfunction. The most transformative proposals recognize that discovering connexin alterations without mechanistic grounding or temporal context would provide incomplete understanding, necessitating integrated experimental designs that simultaneously assess structure, function, mechanism, and timeline.
