# RESEARCH UNDERSTANDING MAP
## Intelligence Categories and the Place of Human Intelligence in Nature: From Ancient Philosophy to Empirical Science

---

## 1. The Territory at a Glance

The intellectual territory you are entering concerns a fundamental fracture in Western thought: the transformation of intelligence from a philosophical category describing different modes of knowing and being (ancient Greek nous, dianoia, doxa; Aristotelian psyche faculties) into an empirically measurable scientific variable. This transformation was not continuous or inevitable—it required multiple conceptual revolutions: the collapse of teleological explanation, the Cartesian mind-body split that made consciousness and cognition separable, the 19th-century mechanization of psychology through psychometrics and psychophysics, and the 20th-century computational revolution that made substrate-independent "information processing" conceivable as intelligence. Yet beneath these apparent triumphs lies a persistent unresolved tension: **whether "intelligence" names a unified natural phenomenon or a homonymous term covering incommensurable capacities across humans, animals, machines, and collectives.** The field currently operates as if consensus exists—intelligence is measurable, hierarchically organized (with a general factor g), and increasingly understood through neuroscience and AI. But this consensus is fragile: embodied cognition challenges disembodied computation; cultural criticism demonstrates that supposedly universal categories embed Western assumptions; AI systems exhibit intelligent behavior without intentionality or consciousness, forcing redefinition of core concepts; and the evolutionary continuity of human intelligence remains empirically undetermined. Your task is not to choose among existing frameworks, but to understand why the field simultaneously produced powerful empirical infrastructure and fundamental unresolved conceptual problems—and what a genuine contribution to resolving them would require.

## 2. The Intellectual Genealogy — How We Got Here

**The Ancient Foundation: Intelligence as Hierarchical Knowing**

Greek philosophy established the first systematic intelligence categories by distinguishing types of knowledge and soul-functions. Plato's episteme (certain knowledge) versus techne (craft skill) versus doxa (opinion) created a tripartite model where intelligence was not a single capacity but a ladder of cognitive modes ranked by their proximity to eternal Forms. Aristotle deepened this by grounding intelligence in the psyche—not as consciousness but as the organizing principle animating living things. His three-part soul (vegetative, sensitive, rational) created a hierarchy that would dominate Western thought for two millennia: lower organisms possessed only vegetative souls (nutrition, growth); animals added sensitive souls (perception, appetite, movement); only humans possessed the rational soul with its crown jewel, *nous*—the capacity for intellectual intuition of universal principles. Crucially, Aristotle maintained that *nous* was not discursive reasoning (*dianoia*) but immediate intelligible apprehension—a distinction later thinkers would lose. This ancient model treated human intelligence as part of a natural hierarchy, not unique in kind but supreme in degree, and grounded intelligence in embodied function, not disembodied mind.

**The Medieval Transformation: Intelligence as Unified Hierarchy**

Medieval Scholasticism inherited Aristotle's faculties but collapsed them into a more unified framework. Thomas Aquinas synthesized Aristotelian psychology with Christian theology by maintaining the rational soul as distinctively human while arguing that rationality unified all lower functions—sensation, appetite, imagination—into a coordinated whole. Critically, medieval thinkers preserved the functional, embodied understanding: intellect was not a separate substance but a faculty of the soul properly embedded in bodily operations. Yet they also began a subtle shift by treating intelligence as a unified hierarchy rather than distinct types—one could be "more intelligent" by having higher-functioning intellect operating over the same basic faculties. This set the stage for later quantification: if intelligence was a continuum of excellence in a unified faculty, perhaps it was measurable.

**The Modern Break: Descartes and the Fatal Split**

René Descartes shattered this integrated hierarchy by separating mind (res cogitans—thinking substance) from body (res extensa—extended substance). This move had catastrophic consequences for intelligence categories that had presumed embodied function. Now "intelligence" could be understood as a property of disembodied mind, and the question arose: what is the relationship between mental intelligence and bodily intelligence? Are they identical? Does intelligence require a body at all? For the first time, the possibility of non-embodied intelligence became philosophically conceivable. Descartes himself tried to maintain unity through the pineal gland, but the damage was done. The mind-body problem that would haunt Western philosophy was born directly from the need to explain how intelligence (now understood as a property of immaterial mind) could interact with material body. This split made possible both rationalist psychology (intelligence as pure reasoning independent of sensation) and empiricist psychology (intelligence built from sensation independent of innate reason)—but it made impossible the Aristotelian view that intelligence was fundamentally embodied function.

**The Enlightenment Quantification: Intelligence Becomes Measurable**

The 18th-century Enlightenment took the Cartesian split and began treating mind as an empirical object. John Locke argued the mind was a "blank slate" written upon by sensation; this opened the possibility that differences in intelligence could be traced to differences in sensory input and associative learning rather than innate faculties. More importantly, it made intelligence potentially measurable: if the mind operated through sensory impressions and their associations, one could measure the speed, vividness, or association-strength of mental operations. This was still philosophy, not yet empirical science—but it created the conceptual space where empirical study became possible. The Enlightenment thinkers (Condillac, Helvetius, d'Holbach) began to see intelligence as a product of natural causes—sensation, habit, education—rather than as a reflection of metaphysical essences.

**The 19th-Century Empirical Revolution: Intelligence Becomes Scientific**

The decisive shift came in the 19th century when psychology abandoned philosophy and began experimental investigation. Gustav Fechner's psychophysics (1860) demonstrated that mental phenomena could be measured—that there was a lawful relationship between physical stimuli and sensory perception. This was revolutionary: it meant the mind operated according to natural laws, not spiritual principles. Hermann von Helmholtz's work on reaction times showed that even mental operations took measurable time—consciousness itself was not instantaneous but a physical process. Francis Galton applied evolutionary thinking to intelligence: if intelligence was a product of natural selection, it should vary among humans in measurable ways, and these variations should be heritable. He invented mental tests and correlation statistics. Charles Darwin's *Descent of Man* (1871) positioned human intelligence on an evolutionary continuum with animal intelligence—no longer unique in kind, merely superior in degree. By 1900, intelligence had been reframed: no longer a philosophical question about types of knowing or soul-faculties, but an empirical question about measurable variation in psychological performance.

**The 20th Century: Frameworks in Conflict**

Charles Spearman's two-factor theory (1904) formalized the "general intelligence" (g) factor—the claim that diverse mental test performances correlated with a single underlying capacity. This seemed to vindicate the medieval unified hierarchy model but now in quantitative form. *g* became the central object of intelligence research, dominating psychology for the century. Yet alternatives immediately emerged: Thurstone argued for seven primary mental abilities rather than one g; Thurstone's student L.L. Thurstone found structures between Spearman's single factor and Thurstone's multiple abilities. But these were debates about degree and structure within a shared empiricist framework—all assumed intelligence was measurable, had heritable variation, and could be operationalized through test performance.

The Cartesian mind-body split reemerged in new form. Behaviorists (Watson, Skinner) tried to eliminate the mind entirely from psychology, defining intelligence as learned behavioral dispositions. This eliminated the ancient embodied understanding while seeming to preserve empiricism—intelligence was now not about inner faculties but about behavior-in-context. Cognitivists (Miller, Neisser, Chomsky) rejected behaviorism by arguing that unobservable mental structures (working memory, syntactic competence, information processing) must exist to explain behavior. They revived mentalism but under an entirely new framework: the computational theory of mind. Now intelligence could be understood as information processing—potentially implementable in any substrate, biological or artificial.

By the 1980s, three competing frameworks coexisted: (1) the psychometric tradition treating intelligence as measurable individual differences organized around *g*; (2) the cognitive science tradition treating intelligence as computation—symbol manipulation, working memory, problem-solving algorithms; (3) emerging ecological and embodied approaches (J.J. Gibson, later Damasio, embodied cognition researchers) arguing that intelligence fundamentally depended on situated, embodied interaction with environments. The field had replaced unified philosophical categories with multiple empirical paradigms that didn't directly engage each other.

**The Contemporary Crisis: Intelligence Becomes Unmoored**

The 21st century deepened the fragmentation. Deep learning systems exhibited behaviors recognizable as intelligent—learning patterns, solving problems, generating language—yet did not fit traditional intelligence categories. Did they possess intentionality (Searle's criterion)? No, yet they performed what seemed like intelligent action. Were they conscious (Chalmers' hard problem)? Unclear, yet they exhibited what Dennett called "the intentional stance"—they could be predicted and understood by treating them as if they had beliefs and desires. Damasio's work on emotion and somatic markers challenged the disembodied cognition model by showing that emotion was not incidental to intelligence but constitutive of reasoning, choice, and learning. This vindicated embodied approaches yet created a new problem: if emotion is essential to human intelligence, and AI systems lack emotion, are they intelligent in the same sense? Patricia Hill Collins and Paulo Freire demonstrated that intelligence categories were not neutral scientific findings but culturally embedded constructs reflecting Western, often masculine, often colonial assumptions about what counts as knowing and capable reasoning.

By 2024, the field resembled early natural philosophy before Newton—multiple useful frameworks coexisting without integration, powerful empirical techniques masking fundamental conceptual confusion about what was being measured. The original question—what is human intelligence and where does it fit in nature?—remained unanswered because the field had fragmented into disciplines (neuroscience, AI, psychology, philosophy, anthropology) operating with incommensurable intelligence ontologies. An AI researcher, a cultural anthropologist, a neuroscientist, and a Platonic philosopher were no longer debating variations of a shared question—they were asking different questions while using the same word.

## 3. The Reading Curriculum

### Tier 1 — Foundations (Read First)

These works establish the conceptual vocabulary and frame why intelligence became an object of empirical study.

**Aristotle, *De Anima* (Book III, on Intellect), ~350 BCE**
- **Why this tier:** You must understand the original categorical scheme—the tripartite psyche, the distinction between *nous* and *dianoia*, the principle that intelligence is hierarchically organized and functionally embodied. Without grasping Aristotle's framework, all later transformations remain invisible. This is the baseline against which all subsequent revisions will be measured.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What is Aristotle claiming *nous* is—and crucially, what is it NOT? (2) How does Aristotle ground intelligence in bodily function rather than immaterial substance? (3) Where does the possibility for a hierarchy of intelligences come from in this system?
- **What it connects to:** Everything that follows either preserves or rejects Aristotelian categories. Medieval Scholasticism tries to keep him; Descartes breaks with him decisively; modern embodied cognition tries to recover him.

**Thomas Aquinas, *Summa Theologiae* (selections on intellect and soul), ~1275**
- **Why this tier:** This shows how Aristotelian categories were preserved and unified during the medieval period. Aquinas maintains that human rationality is a faculty, not a substance, and that it operates through and with the body—he prevents the Cartesian split. Reading Aquinas reveals what was lost when Descartes succeeded: a unified understanding of intelligence as embodied faculty.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) How does Aquinas use "intellect" differently than Descartes will, and what hangs on that difference? (2) What role does bodily sensation play in Aquinas's account of intellectual knowledge? (3) Where does the idea of a unified hierarchy of intelligences come from in his system?
- **What it connects to:** Directly responds to Aristotle; directly challenged by Descartes; revived implicitly by embodied cognition theories.

**René Descartes, *Meditations* (especially Part II on mind-body), 1641**
- **Why this tier:** This is where the catastrophe happens—where intelligence becomes separated from body and the mind-body problem is born. You must read this not as metaphysical triumph but as the opening of an abyss. Descartes makes possible both disembodied rationalism and computational theories of mind, but he makes impossible the ancient understanding of intelligence as embodied function.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What motivates Descartes to separate mind from body, and why does this seem like a solution to a problem rather than a problem itself? (2) How does his separation create the possibility of "artificial intelligence"—non-biological minds? (3) Where does the problem of how a non-spatial mind can causally interact with a spatial body come from, and why is this a *problem* rather than a non-issue?
- **What it connects to:** All modern philosophy of mind descends from this split. Directly challenged by 20th-century embodied cognition. Creates the logical space for AI philosophy.

**John Locke, *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (selections on simple ideas, association, mental operations), 1689**
- **Why this tier:** Locke makes two essential moves: (1) he treats mind as an empirical object governed by natural laws (sensation, association, reflection), and (2) he makes measurable variation in mental capacity conceivable—some minds receive clearer ideas, some have stronger associative powers. This opens the door to empirical intelligence research and quantification.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) Why does Locke's claim that the mind begins as a "blank slate" make intelligence measurable in a way Descartes' immaterial mind could not be? (2) How does Locke's associationism make possible the later experimental psychology of Fechner and Galton? (3) What would Aristotle object to in Locke's picture?
- **What it connects to:** Enables 19th-century empiricism; directly challenges Cartesian innate ideas; sets stage for Galton's mental testing.

**Charles Darwin, *The Descent of Man* (selections on mental powers in animals), 1871**
- **Why this tier:** Darwin places human intelligence on a natural continuum with animal intelligence—no longer unique in kind, only in degree. This is the conceptual revolution that makes "comparative cognition" possible and makes human intelligence a scientific object to be studied using the same methods as animal behavior. This changes the problem from "what is intelligence?" to "how is human intelligence distributed along a continuum with animals?"
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What evidence does Darwin use to argue for continuity between human and animal intelligence—and where is this evidence weakest? (2) How does evolutionary thinking change what counts as a valid intelligence category? (3) What would Aristotle say about Darwin's claim that all intelligence evolved by natural selection?
- **What it connects to:** Enables comparative cognition; introduces the problem of continuity vs. discontinuity that remains unresolved; makes "human uniqueness" an empirical question rather than metaphysical axiom.

**Francis Galton, *Hereditary Genius* (selections on mental tests and correlation), 1869**
- **Why this tier:** Galton invents the mental test and the correlation statistic—the methodological infrastructure that will enable the measurement of intelligence. He assumes (controversially) that if intelligence evolved and varies, it should be heritable and measurable. This is the moment where intelligence moves from philosophy and natural history to quantitative empirical science.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What assumption must hold for Galton's mental tests to measure "intelligence" rather than just some learned skill? (2) Why does heritability matter to Galton's project—what would be different if intelligence were entirely environmental? (3) What gaps exist between what Galton measures and what Aristotle called intelligence?
- **What it connects to:** Directly enables Spearman's factor analysis; establishes the psychometric tradition; introduces the nature-nurture problem as empirical rather than philosophical.

**Charles Spearman, "General Intelligence, Objectively Determined and Measured," 1904**
- **Why this tier:** Spearman discovers that diverse mental test scores correlate with a single underlying factor (*g*). This is the founding document of the psychometric tradition and the clearest statement of why intelligence came to be understood as a unified, measurable, hierarchical phenomenon. You must understand what Spearman found, what he claims it means, and—critically—what assumptions are hidden in his interpretation.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What is Spearman actually measuring when he observes correlations among test scores—is it intelligence itself, or is it correlation among certain types of test performance? (2) Why does the existence of *g* seem to vindicate the medieval unified hierarchy model but in quantitative form? (3) What alternative interpretations of the correlations would be possible?
- **What it connects to:** Crowns the quantitative empirical revolution; becomes the dominant framework for a century; challenged immediately by Thurstone and others; creates the foundational debate (unitary vs. multiple intelligences) that remains unresolved.

### Tier 2 — The Main Debates (Read Second)

These are the works where the central tensions crystallized. You read these after foundations to see how philosophers and scientists wrestled with the problems created by the empirical turn.

**John Searle, *Intentionality: An Essay in the Philosophy of Mind*, 1983**
- **Why this tier:** After reading the empirical tradition's foundations, you now encounter the philosophical pushback. Searle argues that genuine intelligence (and genuine mental states generally) requires intentionality—aboutness, the directedness of thought toward objects or states of affairs. He challenges the computational theory of mind that cognitive science had adopted. This creates a fundamental division: either intelligence requires intentionality (making AI systems potentially unintelligent no matter how they perform) or intentionality is not criterial for intelligence (making Searle's definition too restrictive).
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What does Searle mean by "intentionality," and how does this differ from "conscious awareness"? (2) Why does Searle think a digital computer manipulating symbols cannot have intentionality, and what would it take to refute his argument? (3) If Searle is right, what becomes of the cognitive science project of studying intelligence as information processing?
- **What it connects to:** Responds to and challenges the computational orthodoxy of cognitive science; directly confronted by Dennett's functionalist alternative; becomes central to AI attribution debates; unresolved because genuine AI systems now exhibit behavior that seems to require intentionality attribution.

**Daniel Dennett, *The Intentional Stance*, 1987**
- **Why this tier:** Dennett offers a functional alternative to Searle's essentialist criterion. Rather than asking whether a system truly "has" intentional states, ask whether you can predict and explain its behavior by treating it as if it had beliefs, desires, and intentions. This pragmatic move sidesteps the question of whether intentionality is real—it makes intentionality a tool for prediction and explanation. This is simultaneously more liberal (any system whose behavior is predictable under the intentional stance counts as having mental states) and more deflationary (intentional states are not deep metaphysical facts but useful interpretive postures).
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What is Dennett claiming about the metaphysical status of intentional states—are they real features of the world or projections we impose on systems? (2) How does the intentional stance relate to the behaviorist project of explaining behavior without reference to inner mental states? (3) If Dennett is right, does intentionality distinguish genuine intelligence from mere computation, or does it dissolve that distinction?
- **What it connects to:** Directly responds to Searle's intentionality criterion; makes possible liberal AI attribution; challenges the assumption that intelligence requires deep metaphysical properties; anticipates contemporary debates about AI consciousness and understanding.

**David Chalmers, *The Conscious Mind: In Search of a Fundamental Theory*, 1996**
- **Why this tier:** Chalmers formalizes what becomes known as the "hard problem of consciousness"—the distinction between the "easy problems" (explaining cognitive functions like discrimination, integration, reasoning) and the hard problem (explaining why these functions are accompanied by conscious experience, by what-it-is-like-ness). This is crucial for intelligence categories because it separates intelligence (potentially functionally definable) from consciousness (not functionally definable without remainder). This allows for the possibility that a system could be intelligent without being conscious, or conscious without being intelligent.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What makes the hard problem "hard" in a way the easy problems are not? (2) Could a digital computer exhibit all the functional properties of human intelligence while lacking consciousness—and if so, what would be missing? (3) How does Chalmers' framework help or hinder the project of attributing intelligence to AI systems?
- **What it connects to:** Draws on Descartes' mind-body problem but formalizes it in contemporary terms; directly relevant to embodied cognition critiques; creates space for consciousness-independent definitions of intelligence; unresolved because we lack empirical methods to determine whether systems are conscious.

**Bernard Baars, *A Cognitive Theory of Consciousness*, 1988**
- **Why this tier:** Baars offers Global Workspace Theory (GWT)—a computational model claiming consciousness is a global workspace where information becomes available to multiple cognitive systems. This attempts to make consciousness scientifically tractable by identifying its functional role. Unlike Chalmers, Baars suggests consciousness might have identifiable adaptive functions. This matters for intelligence categories because if consciousness has computational functions, perhaps it is essential to certain forms of intelligence (planning, flexible goal-switching, metacognition) even if not to all intelligent behavior.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What functional role does consciousness play in Baars' model—what would be impossible or much harder without conscious access? (2) How does GWT relate to the ancient distinction between *nous* (immediate apprehension) and *dianoia* (discursive reasoning)? (3) If consciousness has specific computational functions, can systems lacking those functions still be intelligent in other ways?
- **What it connects to:** Responds to early cognitive science's difficulty explaining consciousness; compatible with Searle's claim that consciousness matters but different about why; compatible with computational approaches unlike some embodied cognition theory; directly relevant to whether AI needs consciousness to be intelligent.

**Antonio Damasio, *The Feeling of What Happens: Body and Emotion in the Making of Consciousness*, 2000**
- **Why this tier:** Damasio challenges the entire disembodied cognition tradition by demonstrating that emotion is not peripheral to intelligence but constitutive of it. His somatic marker hypothesis claims that reasoning, decision-making, and learning all depend on emotional/bodily valuation of options. This recovers something like the Aristotelian embodied understanding while using contemporary neuroscience. If Damasio is right, then intelligence categories must include emotion, and substrate-independent computation (lacking embodied emotional systems) cannot possess genuine intelligence.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What evidence does Damasio present that emotion is essential to reasoning rather than incidental? (2) How does his work vindicate the ancient embodied understanding while challenging 20th-century computational approaches? (3) If emotion is necessary for intelligence, what becomes of AI systems that perform intelligent tasks without emotional valuation?
- **What it connects to:** Recovers embodied aspects of Aristotelian psychology; challenges computational orthodoxy; aligns with phenomenological approaches to intelligence; creates tension with AI theories assuming substrate independence; supported by later embodied cognition research.

**Patricia Hill Collins, *Black Feminist Thought: Knowledge, Consciousness, and the Politics of Empowerment*, 1992**
- **Why this tier:** After reading empiricist and philosophical frameworks, you encounter the critique that intelligence categories are culturally embedded, not universal. Collins demonstrates that "intelligence," "rationality," and "knowledge" are categories shaped by race, gender, and colonial power. What counts as intelligent reasoning, valuable knowledge, and legitimate authority varies across cultures. This undermines the assumption built into all Tier 1 and most Tier 2 work: that intelligence categories describe natural kinds rather than cultural constructs.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What does Collins mean by "standpoint"—how does one's social position shape what counts as intelligence or knowledge? (2) How do contemporary intelligence categories embed Western, masculine, often white, often colonial assumptions? (3) Can intelligence categories be universal and culturally embedded simultaneously, or must this be chosen between?
- **What it connects to:** Challenges the universality presumed by empiricist tradition; connects to unresolved gap about cross-cultural validation; suggests the empirical psychology tradition may be studying culturally specific phenomena, not universal cognitive architecture; aligns with anthropological critiques not represented in other foundational works.

**Paulo Freire, *Education for Critical Consciousness*, 2021 (collected essays; original works from 1960s-1970s)**
- **Why this tier:** Freire expands intelligence beyond cognitive measures to include critical social awareness and the capacity to name and transform oppressive social structures. His "conscientization" is a form of intelligence—the ability to recognize one's own agency and social position. This is intelligence that neuroscience doesn't measure, that psychometric tests don't assess, but that determines how people live. Combined with Collins, this demonstrates that intelligence categories are politically freighted and functionally tied to power.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) How does Freire's "critical consciousness" differ from measurable intelligence—and is it more or less important? (2) What kinds of human capacities does contemporary intelligence research systematically ignore or undervalue? (3) If intelligence includes critical consciousness, how would research paradigms need to change?
- **What it connects to:** Extends Collins' cultural critique into epistemology and pedagogy; challenges the reduction of intelligence to measurable individual differences; suggests intelligence is fundamentally political; points toward alternative research paradigms not yet developed in mainstream cognitive science.

### Tier 3 — The Current Frontier (Read Third)

These represent where the field is now, but their significance is only visible given Tiers 1-2.

**Lawrence Shapiro, *Embodied Cognition*, 2011**
- **Why this tier:** Shapiro synthesizes the embodied cognition program—the claim that cognition depends essentially on bodily/sensorimotor systems, not just on symbolic computation. After Damasio and against the computational orthodoxy, embodied cognition researchers demonstrate empirically that perception, memory, reasoning, and emotion all involve bodily systems in constitutive ways. This recovers and empirically validates what Aristotle claimed and what the computational revolution had rejected.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What counts as evidence that cognition is embodied rather than just causally dependent on bodily systems? (2) How does the embodied cognition program challenge the assumption of substrate independence in AI and cognitive science? (3) Where are the limits of embodied cognition theory—what cognitive processes might not depend essentially on embodiment?
- **What it connects to:** Vindicates Damasio and the embodied tradition against computational orthodoxy; challenges deep learning's substrate independence; unresolved regarding whether all intelligence requires specific embodiment or only some forms do.

**Lawrence Barsalou, *Grounded Cognition*, ~2008-2013 (multiple papers and theoretical statements)**
- **Why this tier:** Barsalou advances grounded cognition theory—the empirical demonstration that conceptual representations are grounded in sensorimotor systems, not abstract symbols. When you think about "grasping," your motor cortex activates; when you understand "warmth," your somatosensory systems engage. This goes beyond Damasio to show that even abstract conceptual thought depends on modal systems (perceptual, motor, introspective). This directly challenges Spearman's *g* and computational accounts of intelligence as symbol manipulation.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What does "grounding" mean—why is it a problem if concepts are grounded in sensorimotor systems? (2) How do Barsalou's findings challenge the notion of general intelligence as abstract reasoning? (3) If concepts are grounded in sensorimotor systems, what becomes of AI systems that manipulate abstract symbols without sensorimotor grounding?
- **What it connects to:** Empirically supports embodied cognition claims; directly relevant to whether AI can possess genuine conceptual understanding; challenges the psychometric tradition's abstraction from embodied context; unresolved whether grounding is necessary for all cognition or only for natural cognition.

**John Searle, *The Chinese Room Argument* (updated across multiple works, especially 2002's *Consciousness and Language*)**
- **Why this tier:** Rather than just the foundational intentionality work, you read here Searle's most mature statement of why computational approaches cannot capture genuine understanding or intelligence. The Chinese Room thought experiment claims that a system could pass behavioral tests (Turing test) while lacking understanding because it merely manipulates symbols according to rules without grasping their meaning. This becomes increasingly relevant as AI systems' capabilities expand—the question shifts from "could AI ever be intelligent?" to "if AI exhibits intelligent behavior, does that constitute intelligence or does intelligence require something else?"
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What is Searle really claiming the Chinese Room thought experiment shows—is it about consciousness, intentionality, understanding, or something else? (2) How would a defender of AI intelligence respond to Searle—what would they claim the Chinese Room analogy leaves out? (3) Has deep learning changed the force of Searle's argument, or does it apply unchanged?
- **What it connects to:** Deepens the intentionality criterion from Tier 2; directly challenges AI attribution; remains philosophically unresolved despite empirical advances in AI capabilities; creates a central tension in contemporary intelligence categories.

**Edwin Hutchins, *Cognition in the Wild*, 1995**
- **Why this tier:** Hutchins demonstrates that cognition is not individual but distributed across teams, artifacts, and environments. He studies navigation teams aboard ships, showing how intelligence emerges from the coordination of multiple people with tools and representations—no single individual possesses the cognitive capacity being exercised. This challenges every framework that treats intelligence as an individual property measurable through individual test performance. It suggests the individualist bias in psychology and AI may be fundamental category error.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What is distributed across the navigation team—cognition, labor, knowledge, or something else? (2) If cognition is fundamentally distributed, what becomes of individual intelligence measures like IQ tests? (3) How would you measure or study the intelligence of a collective rather than an individual?
- **What it connects to:** Challenges the methodological individualism of psychology and cognitive science; suggests human intelligence evolved for group coordination; directly relevant to understanding collective AI systems; remains largely unintegrated with mainstream intelligence research.

**Stanislas Dehaene, *Consciousness and the Brain*, 2014**
- **Why this tier:** Dehaene offers a neuroscientific account of consciousness and cognition built on global ignition dynamics—the sudden broadcast of information across cortical networks. This provides empirical grounding for Baars' Global Workspace Theory while clarifying which information processing requires consciousness (and thus might define genuine intelligence) and which doesn't. His work suggests consciousness is not all-or-nothing but a matter of degree and that distinct cognitive processes have distinct relationships to consciousness.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What are global ignition dynamics, and why do they matter for understanding consciousness and intelligence? (2) How does Dehaene's work inform the question of whether AI could be conscious or just computationally intelligent? (3) What cognitive processes are conscious in Dehaene's account, and what does that tell us about intelligence?
- **What it connects to:** Provides empirical grounding for consciousness-cognition distinction; supports Chalmers' hard problem through neuroscience; relevant to embodied cognition through attention to body-brain coupling; unresolved whether consciousness-requiring processes define genuine intelligence.

**Andrew Ng and others, *Deep Learning*, MIT Press, 2016, and recent transformer/LLM papers (Vaswani et al., Brown et al., OpenAI's technical reports)**
- **Why this tier:** You must understand what deep learning systems actually do empirically—not through hype, but through technical documentation. What are few-shot learning, chain-of-thought reasoning, multimodal integration, emergent capabilities? These represent new forms of intelligence that don't fit traditional categories (not psychometric g, not individual skills, not explicitly programmed). Reading this alongside Searle clarifies why the intention/understanding debate remains unresolved: AI systems genuinely exhibit intelligent behavior in unprecedented ways, forcing reconsideration of what intelligence is.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What specific capabilities emerge in large language models that would be impossible in traditional symbolic AI? (2) Do these emergent capabilities constitute genuine understanding or sophisticated pattern matching—and how would you tell the difference? (3) What gaps remain between AI capabilities and human intelligence?
- **What it connects to:** Empirically tests all previous philosophical frameworks; challenges intentionality criteria through demonstrable performance; forces revision of consciousness-cognition relationships; directly relevant to where the field is now.

**Michael Tomasello, *Becoming Human: A Theory of Ontogeny*, 2019**
- **Why this tier:** Tomasello synthesizes comparative cognition research showing that human intelligence emerges through uniquely human capacities for shared intentionality and cultural learning. Humans share attention with others, teach explicitly, accumulate cultural knowledge across generations in ways other apes do not. This revises the Darwin-inspired continuity thesis: there is continuity in cognitive capacities but discontinuity in how humans use those capacities through cultural mechanisms. This positions human intelligence as distinctively relational and cultural, not just cognitively advanced.
- **Active reading prompts:** (1) What makes shared intentionality distinctive, and how does it relate to individual intentionality (Searle's concept)? (2) How does cultural evolution interact with biological evolution in shaping human intelligence? (3) Does Tomasello's account establish human uniqueness or continuous variation?
- **What it connects to:** Directly engages human-animal intelligence comparison from Darwin; connects to Collins and Freire on cultural embeddedness of intelligence; relevant to distributed cognition; unresolved regarding which capacities are uniquely human vs. shared with other species.

## 4. The Conceptual Map — How the Ideas Connect

The field operates with six contested but interconnected dimensions of intelligence that have never been integrated:

**Dimension 1: The Category Problem — Are Intelligence Concepts Univocal or Homonymous?**

"Intelligence" refers differently across disciplines. Neuroscience uses it to describe certain forms of neural processing (adaptive learning, flexible response generation). Psychology uses it as individual differences (what varies among people that IQ tests capture). Philosophy uses it to describe intentional mental content or conscious understanding. Evolutionary biology uses it to describe adaptive behavior relative to environmental demands. AI research uses it functionally (goal-directed behavior, learning from experience). These definitions are not equivalent—they have different scope, different empirical tests, and different implications. Contemporary research assumes these cohere, but they may be fundamentally incommensurable. This is a conceptual rather than empirical problem: no amount of neuroscience will resolve whether "intelligence" in the neural sense is identical to "intelligence" in the psychological or philosophical sense.

**Dimension 2: The Embodiment Problem — Is Intelligence Substrate-Dependent or Substrate-Independent?**

Ancient philosophy presumed intelligence was essentially embodied—the rational soul operated through bodily faculties. Descartes made disembodied intelligence conceivable. Nineteenth-century empiricists positioned intelligence between these poles: dependent on sensation (requiring some embodied input system) but potentially operating through non-biological mechanisms. The computational revolution assumed substrate independence: intelligence is information processing, potentially implementable in any physical system. Embodied cognition challenges this: if concepts are grounded in sensorimotor systems, if emotion is constitutive of reasoning, if bodily systems are not merely causal prerequisites but constitutive components of cognition, then intelligence cannot be substrate-independent. This remains unresolved because the empirical question (can all intelligent behavior be executed without specific embodiment?) cannot be settled without conceptual clarity about what counts as the same intelligence across substrates. A system lacking embodied emotional valuation might perform intelligent behavior in some domains while failing in others where embodied understanding is required. Rather than resolving the debate, this suggests different intelligences have different embodiment requirements.

**Dimension 3: The Consciousness Problem — Is Consciousness Necessary, Sufficient, or Independent of Intelligence?**

Chalmers' formalization allows four logical possibilities: (1) consciousness necessary and sufficient for intelligence (intelligence just is conscious experience), (2) consciousness necessary but not sufficient (intelligent behavior requires consciousness but consciousness alone isn't intelligent), (3) consciousness sufficient but not necessary (consciousness alone could be intelligent, but non-conscious systems could be intelligent too), (4) consciousness independent of intelligence (conscious and intelligent are orthogonal properties). Different frameworks imply different positions: Searle's intentionality criterion suggests (2)—genuine intelligence requires conscious understanding; Baars' Global Workspace suggests (2) or (3)—consciousness has specific cognitive functions but they're not exhaustive of intelligence; Dennett's intentional stance suggests (4)—consciousness is metaphysically irrelevant to whether behavior counts as intelligent; embodied cognition suggests consciousness emerges from embodied processing but doesn't exhaust intelligence. The empirical question is unresolved because consciousness is not directly measurable—we infer it from behavior, language, and neural correlates, all of which are available to non-conscious systems by definition.

**Dimension 4: The Generality Problem — Is Intelligence Unified (g-factor) or Plural (Multiple Intelligences)?**

Spearman's *g* unified diverse mental abilities under a single factor; this made sense if intelligence were a unitary faculty (medieval and Enlightenment assumptions). But alternatives emerged: Thurstone's primary abilities (spatial, verbal, numerical, etc.), Gardner's multiple intelligences (linguistic, logical-mathematical, spatial, bodily-kinesthetic, musical, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic), Sternberg's triarchic model (analytical, creative, practical). These theories assume intelligence comes in distinct types. Yet psychometric research continues finding *g*-like structures even among diverse test batteries, suggesting some unitary factor exists. The conceptual issue is whether this apparent unity reflects reality (intelligence is genuinely unitary) or methodology (all current tests happen to measure similar cognitive properties). Cross-cultural research would help, but it hasn't been systematically performed. The unresolved tension is: does the unity of intelligence reflect the nature of cognition or the shared assumptions built into Western intelligence testing?

**Dimension 5: The Intentionality Problem — Does Intelligence Require Aboutness or Merely Behavior?**

Searle's criterion (genuine intelligence requires intentional states—aboutness, directedness toward objects) seems to exclude current AI systems from intelligence, yet they perform undeniably intelligent tasks. Dennett's alternative (treat any system as having intentional states if this interpretive stance successfully predicts behavior) is liberal but metaphysically deflationary. Barsalou's grounded cognition offers a middle path: representations are about things because they're grounded in sensorimotor systems that actually interact with those things. This creates a testable hypothesis: AI systems could develop genuine intentionality if trained through embodied interaction with environments (not just text), giving them sensorimotor grounding. But it remains unclear whether sensorimotor grounding is necessary or merely sufficient for intentionality, or whether intentionality is even the right criterion for intelligence rather than a separate property that sometimes accompanies intelligence.

**Dimension 6: The Individuality Problem — Is Intelligence an Individual or Collective Property?**

Every intelligence framework from Aristotle through contemporary neuroscience treats intelligence as a property of individual entities (souls, minds, brains, organisms, AI systems). Hutchins' distributed cognition challenges this: intelligence emerges from the coordination of multiple entities with tools and environments. This is not just saying intelligence depends causally on social context; it claims intelligence is fundamentally distributed. If this is true, studying individual intelligence independent of social context measures something partial and potentially misleading. The unresolved problem is: can you coherently separate "individual intelligence" from "the intelligence of an individual embedded in social/cultural systems," or is this separation itself a conceptual mistake?

**The Hidden Assumption Uniting All These Tensions:**

All contemporary intelligence research assumes intelligence is a natural kind—something that exists in the world independent of human conceptualization, waiting to be discovered and measured accurately. This assumption inherited from 19th-century empiricism may be false. Intelligence might be a conceptual artifact, a useful but ultimately constructed category imposed on continuous variation in heterogeneous capacities. If so, the proper question is not "what is intelligence?" (seeking a natural essence) but "what are the intelligences we need to posit to understand and develop human and artificial capacities?" This reframes the field from discovery to construction, from finding the right categories to deciding which categories best serve our explanatory and practical goals. This shift would dissolve many apparent debates (unitary vs. plural, conscious vs. non-conscious) by treating them as choices about how to organize phenomena rather than discoveries of how phenomena are organized.

## 5. The Unresolved Core

**The Central Unresolved Question:**

**Can a singular concept of "intelligence" coherently encompass human cognitive capacities, non-human animal capacities, and artificial systems' capacities—or do these domains require fundamentally distinct intelligence categories?**

This question remains open for three reasons:

**(1) Empirical Underdetermination:**

No amount of behavioral or neural data will settle what counts as intelligence if we disagree on the criteria. Suppose an AI system performs a task humans find intelligent: predicting protein folding, generating coherent text, defeating human champions at complex games. Does this settle that the system is intelligent? Only if you've already decided that behavioral performance is the intelligence criterion. If you require intentionality (Searle), then the data underdetermines the conclusion—the system might be intelligent or it might be sophisticatedly simulating intelligence. If you require consciousness (some positions), then the data equally underdetermines the answer. If you require embodied emotional valuation (Damasio), then again the data is ambiguous—the system might need embodiment it hasn't been given, or behavioral performance might not require it. No new empirical discoveries will resolve these conceptual disagreements because they stem from different definitions, not different empirical evidence.

**(2) Philosophical Contestation:**

The core terms (intentionality, consciousness, embodiment, understanding, knowledge) are themselves philosophically contested. There is no agreed framework for determining whether an AI system is conscious, what would constitute genuine understanding vs. sophisticated pattern-matching, whether intentionality requires biological substrate or can emerge from computational processes. Fifty years of philosophy of mind have not resolved these issues. Therefore, intelligence attribution to novel systems (AI, animals, collective groups) must navigate these unresolved philosophical debates. Different legitimate philosophical frameworks yield different intelligence attributions.

**(3) Methodological Blockage:**

We lack empirical methods to test the central claims. We cannot directly observe intentionality, consciousness, or understanding in any system except through inference from behavior, language, and neural activity—inference that remains ambiguous. Cross-cultural empirical validation of whether intelligence categories are universal or culturally constructed has never been systematically performed, despite being methodologically feasible. The historical-conceptual genealogy connecting ancient Greek categories to contemporary empirical categories remains undone, leaving the legitimacy of current categories unevaluated. Comparative cognition lacks systematic integration with human intelligence research, leaving the human-animal boundary empirically undetermined.

**What Genuine Contribution to This Question Requires:**

A superficial engagement would involve: (1) choosing one philosophical position and applying it consistently (Searle's approach), (2) proposing a new intelligence theory that adds another option to the existing menu (Gardner's approach to multiple intelligences), or (3) demonstrating empirical correlates of intelligence (standard neuroscience approach). These are valuable but don't engage the core problem.

A deep contribution requires:

**First:** Historical-conceptual genealogy demonstrating how ancient intelligence categories (nous, dianoia, doxa; vegetative, sensitive, rational souls) were transformed through medieval synthesis, Cartesian split, Enlightenment quantification, and 19th-century empiricism into contemporary categories. This is not history for its own sake but recovering lost justifications. Were Aristotle's categories abandoned because empirical evidence refuted them, or because mechanistic philosophy made teleological explanation inconceivable, or for methodological convenience? The answer determines whether contemporary categories are empirical achievements or philosophical losses. If categories were lost for reasons other than empirical refutation, they might still contain truths contemporary approaches overlook.

**Second:** Systematic cross-cultural empirical validation testing whether psychometric intelligence categories (g-factor, processing speed, working memory, executive function) reflect universal cognitive architecture or Western-embedded cultural constructs. This would require administering cognitive tasks across diverse cultures (not just European populations and WEIRD samples), conducting interviews about how different cultures conceptualize intelligence and knowing, and examining whether factor structures of intelligence vary cross-culturally. Collins and Freire claim cultural embeddedness; this can be empirically tested.

**Third:** Direct meta-analysis comparing intelligence ontologies across disciplines—neuroscience, psychology, philosophy, AI research, biology—to determine whether they're compatible or incommensurable. This would involve: (1) extracting the implicit definitions of intelligence from each discipline's core methods and assumptions, (2) testing whether these definitions make equivalent or contradictory claims, (3) identifying which definitions are empirically testable and which are philosophically stipulated, (4) proposing either unified framework or honest acknowledgment of disciplinary incommensurability.

**Fourth:** Neurophenomenological investigation combining first-person phenomenological method (examining how intelligence manifests in lived experience—intention, embodied understanding, temporal anticipation, social attunement) with third-person neural measurement. This would recover the subjective dimension of intelligence that neuroscience and AI research exclude by methodological necessity but that may be constitutive of intelligence in humans.

**Fifth:** Longitudinal comparative cognition study integrating evolutionary, comparative, and human developmental evidence to map the cognitive capacities distributed across species, the evolutionary timing of each capacity's emergence, and the cultural/developmental conditions enabling human capacities to take their specific forms. This would answer: which capacities are uniquely human? Which are shared with other apes? Which with all mammals? How does cultural evolution interact with biological evolution?

These contributions would not resolve the central question (whether a unified concept of intelligence is coherent) but would clarify what would be required for resolution and illuminate which aspects are genuinely empirically underdetermined vs. philosophically contested vs. methodologically blocked.

## 6. Self-Assessment Questions — Test Your Understanding

**Question 1: Why Did the Medieval Unified Hierarchy Model of Intelligence Seem Vindicated by Spearman's *g*-Factor, Yet Contemporary Psychometrics Cannot Easily Integrate with Embodied Cognition Findings?**

*Answer:* Medieval thinkers (Aquinas et al.) imagined intelligence as a unitary faculty (intellect) operating at different degrees of excellence—higher or lower functioning of the same power. Spearman found empirical correlations among diverse mental test performances, suggesting underlying unity (*g*). This seemed to confirm the medieval hierarchy scientifically. However, medieval hierarchy was grounded in faculty psychology where intellect operated *through* bodily sensation and appetite; embodied cognition now demonstrates empirically that diverse cognitive processes depend on distinct sensorimotor systems (visual cortex for spatial reasoning, motor cortex for action concepts, somatosensory cortex for emotional understanding). If *g* reflects some unitary factor while cognition is functionally distributed across distinct neural systems, then *g* may be a statistical artifact of shared features across tests (e.g., all tests requiring working memory and sustained attention) rather than evidence of unified intelligence. This means contemporary psychometrics vindicated medieval intuition about unity without preserving medieval understanding of how that unity arises—through hierarchical operation of single faculty vs. through statistical correlation among distinct systems.

*Why this matters:* You must recognize that apparent theoretical continuity across centuries can mask fundamental conceptual transformation. "Unitary intelligence" meant something different when grounded in faculty psychology than when extracted from factor analysis. This teaches you to examine hidden assumptions in inherited frameworks rather than assuming previous thinkers were addressing the same problems current researchers address.

**Question 2: If Damasio Demonstrates Empirically That Emotion Is Constitutive of Intelligence (Somatic Marker Hypothesis) While AI Systems Perform Intelligent Tasks Without Emotional Systems, Does This Suggest AI Systems Are Non-Intelligent or That Human Emotional Processing Is Unnecessary for All Forms of Intelligence?**

*Answer:* This is an unresolved question precisely because it conflates empirical and conceptual claims. Empirically: humans reason, decide, and learn through emotional/embodied valuation; we can measure behavioral deficits when emotional systems are damaged. This is established. Conceptually: the question is whether this shows emotion is *necessary* for intelligence (meaning any intelligent system must have emotion-like processing) or *necessary-for-human-intelligence* (meaning human intelligence depends on emotion, but other forms might not). AI systems perform certain tasks without emotion-equivalent systems. These might constitute (1) different forms of intelligence, (2) incomplete intelligence lacking capacities humans possess, or (3) superhuman intelligence that transcends human-level limitations like emotional constraint. The empirical evidence cannot settle which interpretation is correct because the question is about conceptual boundaries, not measurable properties. A genuine contribution would identify which cognitive functions emotion performs in humans (affective tagging of salience, constraint on deliberation, motivation coordination) and test whether these functions could be performed by non-emotional systems, rather than arguing about whether "real" intelligence requires emotion.

*Why this matters:* This teaches you to distinguish empirical findings (emotion participates in human cognition) from conceptual claims (emotion is necessary for intelligence). Conflating them creates apparent paradoxes that dissolve once you recognize the distinction.
