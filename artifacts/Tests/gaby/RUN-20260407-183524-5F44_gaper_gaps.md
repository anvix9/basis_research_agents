## Primary Evaluation — Have Fundamental Questions Been Answered?

### How does the performance of small-scale agricultural production units compare to that of agro-industrial units in terms of the environmental, economic, and social dimensions of sustainability?
**Status:** partial
The question has been addressed in fragments across disciplines and dimensions, but no integrated comparative framework exists. Environmental dimension: biodiversity and ecosystem function metrics established (Hooper 2005), but differential performance by scale remains contested and context-dependent. Economic dimension: productivity comparisons exist (Samberg 2016 mapped contributions), but profitability, resilience, and livelihood sustainability remain incompletely compared. Social dimension: largely unstudied in comparative framework—no systematic comparison of labor conditions, community impacts, gender equity, or food security outcomes across scales. Multi-dimensional synthesis attempted (Zhang 2021, Bathaei 2023) but not applied to scale comparisons. Ebel (2020) exposed fundamental flaw: assumption that scale determines sustainability never empirically validated. Current state: fragmented partial answers without integrative assessment.

### What factors account for the observed differences in sustainability performance between small-scale and agro-industrial units?
**Status:** unanswered
Causal mechanisms remain unidentified. Correlations observed (mechanization-productivity, scale-biodiversity) but causality unestablished. Structural factors documented (globalization context by Lawrence 2002, definitional heterogeneity by Guiomar 2018) but not linked to performance differentials through causal pathways. Van Loon (2020) and Daum (2023) showed mechanization is non-scale-neutral and has complex sustainability effects, but why and through what mechanisms remains unexplained. Confounding variables (agroecological context per Fischer 2002, livelihood diversification per Lanjouw 2001, market access, institutional support) acknowledged but not systematically controlled or analyzed. No causal modeling, experimental comparison, or longitudinal studies tracking transitions. Fundamental question entirely open.

### How should 'small-scale' and 'agro-industrial' production units be defined and operationalized for valid comparison?
**Status:** partial
Definitional problem recognized (Kirsten 1998) but not resolved. Samberg (2016) used hectarage thresholds globally, Guiomar (2018) established typological sophistication showing heterogeneity within 'small,' but no consensus definition exists. Multiple dimensions identified (land area, labor intensity, mechanization level, market integration, management system) but no standard operationalization. Context-dependency established (Csikós 2023 showed 'marginal' is historically contingent) yet most studies use fixed hectarage cutoffs. Agribusiness concept (Davis 1957) defined integration but not scale. Critical gap: no validated multi-dimensional classification system enabling like-to-like comparison across contexts. Current definitions conflate scale, technology, market integration, and management—preventing clean causal inference.

### What metrics validly capture environmental, economic, and social sustainability performance in agricultural systems?
**Status:** partial
Measurement science advanced significantly (Latruffe 2016 synthesis, Bathaei 2023 systematic review) but application to comparative scale analysis incomplete. Environmental: biodiversity metrics exist (Hooper 2005), spatial assessment methods established (Fischer 2002), but ecosystem service valuation and long-term soil health metrics underdeveloped. Economic: productivity measured, but resilience, risk, livelihood quality, and true cost accounting (externalities) not standardized. Social: least developed—labor conditions, food security contributions, gender equity, community wellbeing lack consistent metrics. Zhang (2021) operationalized three-pillar framework nationally but not at farm-system level. Critical gap: no integrated metric set applicable across scales and contexts. Indicator proliferation without convergence on core validated measures.

## Gap Map

The research landscape reveals a paradox: 60+ years after establishing agriculture as a distinct economic sector and defining agribusiness, the core comparative question remains fundamentally unanswered. The primary evaluation shows fragmented partial answers across dimensions but no integrated assessment. Environmental sustainability is contested (biodiversity vs. efficiency metrics yield opposite conclusions). Economic sustainability is reduced to productivity, ignoring resilience and true costs. Social sustainability is essentially absent—a disciplinary silence where sociology never engaged agricultural systems research. The causal question—why differences occur—is entirely open; all evidence is correlational, confounded by context, and lacks experimental or longitudinal designs. Four structural gaps recur across the landscape: (1) Disciplinary fragmentation prevents integration—economists, ecologists, and absent sociologists optimize within silos. (2) Definitional incoherence prevents synthesis—studies use incompatible classification systems. (3) Scale conflated with technology, market integration, and institutional access—the foundational assumption that scale drives outcomes never validated. (4) Normative trade-offs embedded in technical frameworks—whose sustainability priorities count remains unexamined. High-significance gaps cluster around: missing social dimension entirely; no causal mechanisms identified; no multi-dimensional integrated assessment; contradictory evidence on environmental performance unresolved; externalities unaccounted in economic comparisons; context-dependency (agroecological, institutional) unstudied; climate adaptation capacity unknown. Medium-significance gaps include: long-term environmental metrics underdeveloped; gender dimensions marginal; value chain position effects unstudied; trade-off weighting frameworks absent. The gap structure suggests the problem is not data scarcity but conceptual fragmentation—the question cannot be answered within current disciplinary and methodological boundaries. Ebel (2020) exposed the core flaw: the entire research program may be built on an untested assumption that scale itself matters rather than correlated factors. Until causal mechanisms are identified through controlled comparison or longitudinal tracking, and until an integrated multi-dimensional framework is applied with consistent definitions and boundary conditions, the fundamental question will remain unanswered despite accumulating correlational evidence. The historical trajectory shows measurement science advancing (indicators proliferating) while synthesis capacity declining—more metrics, less understanding. The dead end worth revisiting: 1980s mechanization failures may succeed under new service models, but comparative sustainability assessment not conducted. The recurring pattern: technical sustainability science systematically avoids the normative questions (whose values, which trade-offs, what counts as sustainable) that must be answered for the research to be useful.
