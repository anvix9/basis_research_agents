# RESEARCH UNDERSTANDING MAP: Small-Scale vs. Agro-Industrial Agricultural Production and Sustainability

## 1. The Territory at a Glance

The comparative sustainability of agricultural production systems organizes itself around a deceptively simple question that dissolves into three impossible empirical problems: you cannot measure what you cannot define, you cannot compare what you measure differently, and you cannot establish causation without isolating mechanisms from their institutional containers. The field's central intellectual tension is this: **scale appears to be a causal variable but may merely be a proxy for management intensity, technology access, market integration, or institutional arrangement**—and decades of research have assumed causality without validating it. The underlying question that would unlock everything else is not "which system is more sustainable?" but rather "what are the mechanisms through which production scale correlates with sustainability outcomes, and are those mechanisms inherent to scale or contingent on institutional and ecological contexts?" This is fundamentally a field divided between empirical researchers generating partial measurements (productivity, biodiversity, labor conditions—each in isolation) and theoretical researchers pointing out that sustainability is a multi-dimensional property requiring simultaneous assessment, but lacking the methodological infrastructure to execute integrated comparisons. The result is a literature that fragments into disciplinary silos—agricultural economists optimizing for productivity, ecologists measuring biodiversity, sociologists documenting labor conditions—each producing findings that appear contradictory at system level because they measure different properties at different scales using incommensurable metrics.

## 2. The Intellectual Genealogy — How We Got Here

The comparative analysis of agricultural production systems emerged from a specific historical necessity in the 1950s-60s: postcolonial development states needed to understand whether their inherited smallholder agriculture could be modernized to feed growing populations, or whether large-scale mechanized production was structurally necessary. This produced the first generation of frameworks—Oluwasanmi's (1961) conceptualization of agriculture as an economic sector with distinct development implications, and Davis & Goldberg's (1957) invention of "agribusiness" as an intellectual category that treated agriculture not as isolated farming but as an integrated system spanning inputs, production, processing, and distribution. These frameworks made comparison possible by establishing a common analytical language, but they were simultaneously *performance-oriented frameworks*: they asked "which system produces more food per unit of input?" This productionist bias embedded itself in the comparative tradition.

The 1960s-70s brought policy-driven research—Mackie & Chrisler (1961) on accelerating Indian grain production, the USDA's institutionalization of cross-national agricultural data—that created the empirical infrastructure for scale comparison but inherited the productivity assumption. Researchers documented that mechanization increased yields, that larger farms achieved economies of scale, that agribusiness integration increased market integration. These appeared to be simple facts, but they rested on a hidden methodological decision: sustainability was being measured as productivity alone.

The 1990s-2000s brought the first conceptual challenges. Lanjouw's (2001) work on rural non-farm sectors and Lawrence & Almas's (2002) analysis of globalization's effects on local livelihoods complicated the picture by showing that farm productivity metrics alone could not explain household viability—small-scale farmers persisted not because their farms were efficient but because rural livelihood systems were diversified, with farm income providing only partial household income. Simultaneously, Kirsten et al.'s (1998) definitional work exposed a methodological crisis: there was no agreed-upon way to classify farms as "small" or "large," meaning studies were comparing non-equivalent categories. If you defined scale by hectarage, small farms in resource-rich regions outperformed large farms in marginal areas. If you defined it by mechanization level, you created a completely different classification. If you defined it by market integration, you got yet another division.

The 2000s-2010s brought ecological methods into agriculture: Fischer & van Velthuizen (2002) established the agro-ecological zones methodology enabling context-specific assessment, and Hooper & Chapin (2005) synthesized evidence linking biodiversity to ecosystem functioning, making it possible to measure environmental performance beyond simple input-output ratios. This enabled the first genuine sustainability comparisons, but it also revealed a troubling empirical pattern: small farms showed higher biodiversity per hectare but lower productivity per hectare, while large farms showed the inverse—and it was unclear whether this was an inherent trade-off or a result of management practices, market pressures, or available technology.

The 2010s-2020s saw the field fragment into partial solutions. Latruffe et al. (2016) and Bathaei et al. (2023) synthesized decades of sustainability indicator research, establishing that the field had developed sophisticated tools for measuring individual dimensions (soil health, labor conditions, water use efficiency) but no integrated frameworks for simultaneous multi-dimensional assessment. Samberg et al. (2016) provided the first global empirical mapping of farm sizes and smallholder production contributions, establishing that small farms actually produced a larger share of global food than policy discourse acknowledged—but this factual correction did not resolve the sustainability question. Guiomar et al. (2018) showed that "small farms" were not a homogeneous category; they ranged from subsistence operations to market-oriented enterprises, with radically different sustainability profiles.

The critical turning point came with Ebel's (2020) frontal challenge to the agroecology movement's foundational assumption: that small farms are inherently sustainable. Ebel demonstrated that scale itself has no necessary relationship to sustainability outcomes—small farms *can* be highly sustainable or deeply destructive depending on management practices, ecological context, technology adoption, and market pressures. This forced the field to confront its unvalidated assumption: scale had been treated as causally determinative when it might be only correlational. Simultaneously, Van Loon et al. (2020) and Daum (2023) documented that mechanization—long assumed scale-dependent—was actually decoupling from scale, with context-appropriate mechanization services being developed for smallholders, while some large farms remained labor-intensive. This revealed that technology adoption could not simply be predicted from farm size.

The field now stands at an inflection point. Zhang et al. (2021) showed that nations prioritize different sustainability dimensions (some optimizing environmental indicators, others social, others economic) and that this revealed choice, not natural law. The implication is unavoidable: sustainability outcomes are not determined by scale or technology but by explicit prioritization and institutional design. The field moved from asking "which system is more sustainable?" to asking "sustainable along which dimensions, prioritized by whom, through what mechanisms?"—but it has not yet developed the integrated assessment methods to answer this more sophisticated question.

## 3. The Reading Curriculum

### TIER 1 — Foundations (Read First)

**The Role of Agriculture in Economic Development — H. A. Oluwasanmi (1961)**

*Why you read it at this tier:* This work established agriculture as an analyzable economic sector rather than a residual traditional activity, creating the conceptual foundation for all subsequent comparative work. Without this framing, "agricultural production systems" is not yet a coherent analytical object.

*What to look for while reading:*
- How does Oluwasanmi define the boundary between "agriculture" and "economic development"? What stays within agriculture and what gets treated as external?
- What assumptions does he make about production scale and productivity? Where does he suggest scale matters and why?
- Notice what he treats as comparable and what he treats as incommensurable—this reveals the hidden frame.

*What it connects to:* Directly anticipates Davis & Goldberg's agribusiness framing; implicitly sets up the productivity-bias that subsequent literature inherits.

---

**Agribusiness: A New Concept — John H. Davis and Ray A. Goldberg (1957)**

*Why you read it at this tier:* This invented the intellectual category "agribusiness" and established the integrated value-chain framework that enables analysis of agro-industrial systems as distinct from traditional farming. It is foundational because it answers: what makes agro-industrial production a coherent system rather than just "bigger farms"?

*What to look for while reading:*
- How do Davis & Goldberg define "agribusiness"? What is included in the system boundaries and what is excluded?
- Notice the specific organizational forms they privilege—vertical integration, input-output coordination. Are these properties of scale itself or properties of a particular institutional arrangement?
- What does their framework make visible and what does it render invisible? (Hint: labor relations, ecological effects, and market power asymmetries are notably absent.)

*What it connects to:* Establishes the organizational logic that subsequent mechanization and technology transfer work assumes; implicitly defines "industrial" agriculture in terms of integration rather than scale.

---

**DEFINING SMALL-SCALE FARMERS IN THE SOUTH AFRICAN CONTEXT — J. Kirsten et al. (1998)**

*Why you read it at this tier:* This work exposes the methodological crisis underlying all comparative research: there is no agreed-upon definition of "small-scale" farming. This is not a trivial problem. It means every comparison in the literature is potentially comparing non-equivalent categories. You must read this before any comparative work, or you will inherit invisible assumptions about what categories you are comparing.

*What to look for while reading:*
- What different classification schemes does Kirsten identify? (Hectarage, labor, mechanization level, market integration, income levels—trace how each produces different groupings.)
- Why does definitional choice matter? Work through a specific example: how would a 5-hectare mechanized farm producing for export markets be classified under different schemes?
- What does this suggest about studies claiming to compare "small" and "large" farms? Are they comparing the same thing across studies?

*What it connects to:* This is the methodological foundation for understanding why Samberg et al. (2016), Guiomar et al. (2018), and Ebel (2020) all begin by establishing their own classification systems rather than using inherited categories.

---

**Global Agro-ecological Assessment for Agriculture in the 21st Century: Methodology and Results — G. Fischer and H.T. van Velthuizen (2002)**

*Why you read it at this tier:* This established the spatial and ecological framework that enables context-specific comparison. You cannot compare small-scale and agro-industrial systems without controlling for agroecological context (climate, soil, water availability, topography). This work created the methodology for doing so.

*What to look for while reading:*
- What is the Agro-Ecological Zones (AEZ) methodology? How does it operationalize "context"?
- Why is agroecological context essential to meaningful comparison? (Hint: mechanization makes sense in flat, uniform terrain but not in mountainous regions; water-intensive crops require different scales in wet vs. arid zones.)
- What does this framework make impossible to hide? (You can no longer claim productivity differences without specifying the ecological context in which they occurred.)

*What it connects to:* Enables the context-contingent mechanism theories that emerge in 2020s work; provides the spatial infrastructure for understanding why scale-sustainability relationships are not universal but context-dependent.

---

### TIER 2 — The Main Debates (Read Second)

**EFFECTS OF BIODIVERSITY ON ECOSYSTEM FUNCTIONING: A CONSENSUS OF CURRENT KNOWLEDGE — David U. Hooper and F. Stuart Chapin (2005)**

*Why you read it at this tier:* This synthesizes ecological evidence that biodiversity is not decorative but functional—higher biodiversity produces greater stability, resilience, and productivity of ecosystem services. For agriculture, this means environmental sustainability can be measured and compared empirically. This is where the field moved from philosophical environmental concerns to measurable metrics.

*What to look for while reading:*
- What specific ecosystem functions does biodiversity support? (Trace the mechanisms: soil stability, nutrient cycling, pest regulation, pollination.)
- Critically: does higher biodiversity *within agricultural systems* produce the same functions as higher biodiversity in natural systems? Where might they differ?
- What are the measured relationships between scale and biodiversity? (You'll find evidence but it's correlational, not causal—this is important.)

*What it connects to:* Sets up the empirical basis for claims that small farms have higher environmental sustainability; simultaneously raises the question whether this is inherent to small scale or to management practices within small farms (which remains unanswered).

---

**The rural non-farm sector: issues and evidence from developing countries — Jean O. Lanjouw (2001)**

*Why you read it at this tier:* This work fundamentally challenged the assumption that agricultural sustainability could be assessed at the farm level alone. It showed that rural households in developing countries derive income from multiple sources—farms, wage labor, services, small enterprise—and that farm-level productivity metrics tell you nothing about household-level viability or food security. This is where the field discovered that sustainability at the farm scale might be entirely different from sustainability at the household scale.

*What to look for while reading:*
- What proportion of rural household income comes from non-farm sources? How does this vary by region and over time?
- Why does this matter for sustainability assessment? (If a household's resilience depends on income diversification, you cannot assess resilience by measuring farm productivity alone.)
- What does this suggest about comparing small farms (which are typically embedded in diversified livelihoods) with large agro-industrial operations (which tend to be specialized)? Are you comparing equivalent economic units?

*What it connects to:* Anticipates the critique that farm-level sustainability metrics are incomplete; enables understanding why small farms persist despite apparently lower productivity (they are not primarily productivity-dependent). Essential context for understanding livelihood sustainability dimension.

---

**Globalisation, Localisation and Sustainable Livelihoods — Geoffrey Lawrence and Reidar Almas (2002)**

*Why you read it at this tier:* This work situates agricultural production systems within global structural dynamics—globalization pressures toward consolidation and specialization, countervailing localization pressures. It establishes that scale and sustainability outcomes are not properties of production systems themselves but emerge from the interaction between production systems and global economic structures.

*What to look for while reading:*
- How do global supply chains and market structures shape farm-scale viability? (Trace specific examples: commodity prices, input costs, technology availability.)
- What does "localization" mean in this framework? Is it a property of farms or a property of markets and institutions?
- How might the same production scale be viable or unviable depending on whether it is integrated into global value chains or serving local markets? What does this imply about causal mechanisms?

*What it connects to:* Establishes that scale effects cannot be separated from institutional and economic context; enables understanding that observed scale-sustainability correlations may reflect institutional biases rather than inherent properties of scale.

---

**Measurement of sustainability in agriculture: a review of indicators — L. Latruffe et al. (2016)**

*Why you read it at this tier:* This is the state-of-the-art synthesis of two decades of sustainability indicator research. It documents what we can measure, what we measure inconsistently across studies, what we cannot measure, and what we measure but do not understand. This is essential because it maps the infrastructure of the field—what tools exist and what gaps remain.

*What to look for while reading:*
- How are environmental, economic, and social dimensions operationalized in existing studies? What metrics are standard, what are contested?
- Critically: are environmental and economic indicators well-developed compared to social indicators? (You will find yes—and this bias shapes research findings.)
- What does the review reveal about the absence of integrated multi-dimensional assessment? Why has the field measured dimensions separately rather than simultaneously?

*What it connects to:* Directly enables understanding why Bathaei et al. (2023) can conduct a systematic review but cannot yet reach consensus on integration; shows you what the measurement science can and cannot currently deliver.

---

**Subnational distribution of average farm size and smallholder contributions to global food production — Leah H. Samberg et al. (2016)**

*Why you read it at this tier:* This provided the first global-scale empirical mapping of farm sizes and quantified what proportion of global food comes from smallholders. This is where empirical reality confronted policy discourse: smallholder farms were not marginal but central to global food security. This does not directly answer sustainability questions but establishes the empirical stakes.

*What to look for while reading:*
- What proportion of global agricultural land is in small-scale farms? What proportion of global food comes from smallholders?
- How does the small-scale share vary by region, crop, and food type? Where are small farms dominant and where are they marginal?
- Critically: does this data tell you anything about sustainability? (It does not—you can now say "most global food comes from small farms" but not whether that production is sustainable. This gap is important.)

*What it connects to:* Establishes why sustainability comparison matters—small farms are not a declining residual category but a persistent majority. Makes inefficiency arguments empirically untenable.

---

**Are Small Farms Sustainable by Nature?—Review of an Ongoing Misunderstanding in Agroecology — Roland Ebel (2020)**

*Why you read it at this tier:* This is the critical turning point where the field confronts its foundational unvalidated assumption. Ebel demonstrates that the agroecology movement has assumed small farms are inherently sustainable without establishing the causal mechanisms. The review reveals that some small farms are highly sustainable, others deeply destructive; sustainability correlates with management practices, technology adoption, and market engagement, not with scale per se.

*What to look for while reading:*
- What is the specific evidence Ebel cites that scale does NOT determine sustainability? (Trace the examples: sustainable small farms using intensive inputs, unsustainable small farms degrading soils, vice versa for large farms.)
- What does Ebel argue is actually driving sustainability outcomes if not scale? (Management practices, technology, market pressures, institutional access—factors that are scale-independent.)
- Why has the field persisted in treating scale as causally determinative despite this evidence? (What ideological or disciplinary commitments explain this bias?)

*What it connects to:* This work forces you to revise all previous reading. Go back through Hooper & Chapin, Lanjouw, Lawrence & Almas with the understanding that you cannot attribute observed correlations to scale itself without establishing mechanisms.

---

### TIER 3 — The Current Frontier (Read Third)

**Typology and distribution of small farms in Europe: Towards a better picture — N. Guiomar et al. (2018)**

*Why you read it at this tier:* This establishes empirically that "small farms" are not a homogeneous category—they range from subsistence operations to market-oriented enterprises, each with different sustainability profiles. This directly operationalizes the theoretical insight from Ebel (2020) by showing that heterogeneity within size categories exceeds differences between size categories.

*What to look for while reading:*
- What typology does Guiomar develop? How are small farms differentiated within the "small" category?
- For each farm type, what sustainability profile emerges? (Do subsistence farms differ from commercialized small farms in environmental or economic sustainability?)
- What does this typology suggest about the inadequacy of simple size classifications? How would comparison studies need to change?

*What it connects to:* Directly addresses the definitional problem from Kirsten (1998) by proposing a typological solution rather than monolithic size categories. Enables more precise comparative analysis.

---

**Scaling agricultural mechanization services in smallholder farming systems: Case studies from sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and Latin America — J. Van Loon et al. (2020)**

*Why you read it at this tier:* This documents the failure of 1980s-90s technology transfer (transferring large-farm mechanization to small farms) and the emergence of context-appropriate mechanization services (shared equipment, hire services, appropriate-scale technologies). This is where the field moved from treating technology as scale-dependent to treating technology as decoupled from scale with appropriate institutional arrangements.

*What to look for while reading:*
- What were the specific failures of technology transfer? Why did mechanization designed for large farms not work for smallholders?
- What institutional innovations enabled mechanization to work at small scale? (Cooperatives, equipment hire services, custom hire arrangements—trace how these change the scale-technology relationship.)
- Critically: if mechanization works at small scale with appropriate services, what does this suggest about previous conclusions that small farms cannot mechanize? (Were conclusions about scale or about specific institutional arrangements?)

*What it connects to:* Directly challenges the assumption that scale and technology are linked; enables understanding that observed correlations between scale and mechanization reflect institutional bias rather than technological necessity.

---

**Quantitative assessment of agricultural sustainability reveals divergent priorities among nations — Xin Zhang et al. (2021)**

*Why you read it at this tier:* This operationalizes three-pillar sustainability assessment at national scale with independent metrics for environmental, social, and economic dimensions. It demonstrates that nations make explicit choices about which sustainability dimensions to prioritize, and that sustainability outcomes reflect these choices, not natural laws.

*What to look for while reading:*
- How are environmental, social, and economic sustainability measured independently? What metrics constitute each pillar?
- What variation do nations show in sustainability profiles? (Do high environmental sustainability and high economic sustainability coincide? Social and economic?)
- Critically: what does divergence in national sustainability profiles suggest about universal claims regarding scale or technology? (It suggests that priorities and institutional design matter more than production characteristics.)

*What it connects to:* Demonstrates that sustainability is not a unidimensional property that you optimize or fail to optimize. Different systems optimize different dimensions, reflecting explicit or implicit value choices.

---

**Mechanization and sustainable agri-food system transformation in the Global South. A review — T. Daum (2023)**

*Why you read it at this tier:* This is the most recent synthesis documenting mechanization's complex, non-linear sustainability implications. It shows simultaneously that mechanization increases productivity, reduces labor, enables environmental management practices (reduced tillage), and creates labor displacement, increases dependency on external inputs, and can degrade soils. This is where the field documents that technology effects are contingent on how technology is implemented, not inherent to the technology.

*What to look for while reading:*
- What are the documented synergies between mechanization and sustainability goals? (Labor reduction, conservation agriculture enablement, scale flexibility.)
- What are the documented trade-offs? (Labor displacement, input dependency, soil degradation in some contexts.)
- Critically: can you predict mechanization effects from knowing the technology? What determines whether effects are positive or negative? (Context-dependency on crops, soils, labor markets, training, credit access.)

*What it connects to:* Integrates findings from Van Loon et al. (2020) with broader sustainability assessment; demonstrates that mechanization effects cannot be predicted from scale or technology alone but only from configuration of technology + context + institutional support.

---

**A Systematic Review of Agricultural Sustainability Indicators — A. Bathaei et al. (2023)**

*Why you read it at this tier:* This is the most comprehensive recent synthesis of sustainability indicator research. It documents the current state of measurement science for agricultural sustainability across all three pillars, reveals systematic gaps in social dimension measurement, and identifies persistent methodological inconsistencies that prevent meta-analysis or synthesis across studies.

*What to look for while reading:*
- What gaps does Bathaei identify in existing sustainability measurement? (Which dimensions and sub-dimensions are well-measured vs. neglected?)
- How do indicator definitions vary across studies for apparently the same construct? (Trace specific examples: soil health, livelihood security, food security.)
- What does the heterogeneity in measurement suggest about the state of the field? (Can you yet conduct valid comparative assessment? Why or why not?)

*What it connects to:* Brings you to the current frontier—where the field has sophisticated tools for measuring individual indicators but no integrated frameworks for simultaneous multi-dimensional assessment, and no consensus on what indicators are essential.

## 4. The Conceptual Map — How the Ideas Connect

The intellectual architecture organizing this field rests on four contested conceptual relationships, each containing hidden assumptions that shape what research findings mean:

**Scale and Causation.** The field treats farm size as a causal variable—assuming larger farms are more mechanized, more market-integrated, more specialized because of size itself. But the reading curriculum reveals this may invert the causal arrow: farms may be larger because they are mechanized, not mechanized because they are large. Or both may be effects of institutional structures that favor large operations (credit availability, land titling, extension services). Scale appears causally determinative when it may be merely correlational with the actual causal mechanisms (technology access, market integration, capital availability, institutional support). This distinction is not trivial: if scale is causal, scale-neutral interventions cannot change outcomes; if scale is correlational with institutional arrangements, institutional reform could alter scale-sustainability relationships entirely.

**Sustainability as Farm-Level Property.** The field measures sustainability as an attribute of farms—soil health, biodiversity, labor conditions, profitability at the farm gate. But Lanjouw (2001) and Lawrence & Almas (2002) established that farms exist within nested systems: households with diversified livelihoods, communities with social institutions, regions with ecological systems, value chains with multiple actors. Farm-level sustainability metrics miss household-level resilience (a farm may be unproductive but households viable through income diversification), community-level food security (locally productive farms may not be accessible), and value-chain-level sustainability (a farm may be profitable because environmental costs are externalized to downstream water users). This creates a persistent measurement problem: you can assess farm-level sustainability robustly, but you cannot infer system-level sustainability from farm-level metrics.

**Dimensionality and Commensurability.** The three-pillar framework (environmental, economic, social) treats sustainability as multi-dimensional, but this obscures a deeper question: are these dimensions genuinely independent or are they expressions of underlying value choices? Zhang et al. (2021) showed that nations optimize different dimensions—some prioritize environmental metrics, others economic, others social—suggesting that "sustainability" is not a natural state to optimize but a contested value space where trade-offs are inevitable. This means comparative assessments claiming to measure "overall sustainability" are actually making hidden value choices about which dimensions to weight and how to trade off conflicts. A production system with high environmental sustainability but low social sustainability is not "more sustainable overall"—it is sustainable in dimensions you value and unsustainable in those you do not.

**Technology, Institutions, and Scale as Entangled Variables.** Van Loon et al. (2020) and Daum (2023) reveal that technology adoption, institutional arrangements (credit, extension, markets), and farm scale are empirically entangled but analytically separable. In many contexts, large farms have access to mechanization because they have access to credit and markets, not because size itself enables mechanization. Once institutional barriers are removed (through cooperatives, hire services, appropriate-scale technology development), mechanization becomes scale-neutral. This means observed correlations between scale and technology adoption, or scale and sustainability outcomes, may reflect institutional biases toward large operations rather than inherent properties of scale. Mechanization can be scale-appropriate or scale-inappropriate depending on institutional configuration; the same applies to other technologies and practices.

Underlying these conceptual relationships is an unresolved tension: **the field attempts to compare production systems as if they were isolable units, when they are actually embedded in multi-scale contexts (ecological, economic, social, institutional) that fundamentally shape their performance.** A small farm in a region with poor market access, insecure land tenure, and limited extension services will perform differently than an identical small farm in a region with good infrastructure. An agro-industrial operation optimized for export-commodity production in commodity-favorable global markets will perform differently in a context of price volatility or trade restrictions. The apparent variability in findings regarding scale and sustainability may not reflect genuine disagreement about small vs. large farms, but rather the fact that researchers are studying non-equivalent combinations of production systems + contexts + institutions. Resolution requires not choosing between small and large production but developing context-contingent frameworks that specify which production systems perform best under which combinations of ecological, market, and institutional conditions.

## 5. The Unresolved Core

### The Question

**What are the causal mechanisms through which farm scale correlates with sustainability outcomes, and to what extent are these mechanisms inherent to scale itself versus contingent on institutional, technological, and market contexts that are empirically correlated with scale but theoretically independent?**

This question remains radically unresolved despite decades of comparative research because the field has documented correlations (small farms in some contexts show higher environmental indicators; large farms show higher productivity) without isolating causal mechanisms. Three empirical facts remain simultaneously true and apparently contradictory: (1) small farms show higher biodiversity per hectare; (2) large farms show higher productivity per hectare; (3) neither correlation is strong enough or consistent enough to claim universal causation. These facts are only contradictory if you assume scale itself is causal. If scale is a proxy for management intensity, technology adoption, market integration, or institutional access, the apparent contradictions resolve—different contexts allow different scale-technology-institution combinations to thrive.

### Why This Question Remains Open

**Empirically:** No studies have been conducted that compare small-scale and agro-industrial production units while (a) controlling for agroecological context, (b) using standardized sustainability metrics across all three pillars simultaneously, (c) isolating technology adoption as an independent variable from scale, (d) accounting for value-chain position and market integration, and (e) tracking farms longitudinally as they transition between scales or mechanization levels. The research designs required to validate causal claims have not been executed.

**Methodologically:** The field lacks consensus frameworks for (a) defining production units in comparable ways across contexts, (b) measuring sustainability dimensions using equivalent metrics, (c) conducting true cost accounting that internalizes externalities, and (d) disaggregating scale effects from institutional effects. Without methodological standardization, findings from different studies cannot be synthetically compared.

**Conceptually:** The field treats scale as an independent causal variable when it may be only one dimension of a multi-dimensional configuration (scale + technology + market integration + institutional access). Scale-centric frameworks cannot accommodate findings that identical scale produces different outcomes in different contexts, or that different scales produce identical outcomes in different institutional environments. Context-contingent mechanism frameworks have been proposed but not operationalized at sufficient scale and rigor to generate validated comparative findings.

### What a Genuine Contribution to This Question Would Require

A genuine contribution would need to accomplish at least three of the following:

1. **Develop and operationalize a standardized multi-dimensional classification system for agricultural production units** that enables valid cross-context comparison. This requires moving beyond hectarage, mechanization level, or market integration as single dimensions, to capture typologically distinct production configurations (subsistence farms, market-oriented small farms, family enterprises with hired labor, agro-industrial operations, integrated value chains). The Guiomar typology provides a model; its extension and validation would be foundational.

2. **Conduct integrated multi-dimensional sustainability assessment using standardized metrics across environmental, economic, and social pillars simultaneously**, not separately. This requires developing equivalent social sustainability metrics to match the sophistication of existing environmental and economic metrics. Critically, it requires assessing sustainability at multiple scales—farm-level, household-level, community-level, value-chain-level—and documenting how results differ depending on analytical scale.

3. **Design quasi-experimental or longitudinal studies that isolate mechanism by tracking farms as they transition between scales, technology levels, or market integration status**, documenting what actually changes and what does not. This requires following specific farms over time through transitions (small to larger, low to higher mechanization) and documenting changes in sustainability outcomes while controlling for contextual shifts.

4. **Develop a true cost accounting framework that monetizes environmental and social externalities** and compares total production costs across systems, not just farm-gate productivity. This requires integrating ecological assessment (soil health, water quality, biodiversity) and social assessment (labor conditions, community impacts, food security) into economic calculations, revealing whether apparent economic advantages of large-scale operations persist when externalities are internalized.

5. **Explicitly map the mediating and moderating variables** that determine whether scale correlates positively, negatively, or not at all with sustainability outcomes. This requires identifying specific combinations of (agroecological context × technology × institutional support × market conditions) that explain observed variation in scale-sustainability relationships, enabling prediction of which systems will perform best under which circumstances.

### What Distinguishes Superficial from Deep Engagement

A superficial engagement would produce findings like: "Small farms are more environmentally sustainable," or "Large farms are more economically viable," supported by selected metrics from existing literature. This would not engage with the unresolved question because it would not address the causal mechanism problem or the methodological gaps.

A deep engagement would:
- Explicitly acknowledge what cannot be concluded from available evidence and why (what methodological designs are missing, what metrics are absent, what causal claims rest on unvalidated assumptions)
- Develop new measurement or analytical capacity that fills a specific gap rather than applying existing frameworks to new data
- Disaggregate scale effects from institutional effects by deliberately varying one while controlling the other
- Test the assumption that scale is causal by identifying contexts where scale fails to predict outcomes, and explain why
- Address the sustainability-at-what-scale question explicitly, documenting how findings differ depending on analytical level
- Map the heterogeneity within size categories and demonstrate that variation within "small farms" or within "large farms" is meaningful and predictively important

## 6. Self-Assessment Questions — Test Your Understanding

### Question 1: Scale as Causal Variable vs. Proxy Variable

**The Question:** Explain why the field has treated farm scale as a causal variable driving sustainability outcomes, what evidence Ebel (2020) and Van Loon et al. (2020) provide that scale may be only correlational with actual causal mechanisms, and what specific research design would be required to determine whether scale effects are real or artifacts of institutional bias.

**The Answer:** The field inherited a productivity-centric framework from early development economics (Oluwasanmi 1961) and agribusiness theory (Davis & Goldberg 1957) that asked "which scale produces more food per input?" This framing made scale appear causally determinative: larger farms were mechanized, market-integrated, and productive; smaller farms were labor-intensive, subsistence-oriented, and less productive. But Ebel (2020) demonstrates empirically that this correlation is not universal—some small farms are highly mechanized and market-integrated; some large farms remain labor-intensive and degrading. Van Loon et al. (2020) show that mechanization—long assumed scale-dependent—became scale-neutral once institutional barriers were removed through cooperatives and hire services. This suggests scale correlates with productivity because scale correlates with access to credit, markets, and extension services, not because size itself is causally productive. A genuine research design would randomly assign farms to different mechanization or technology interventions while controlling for institutional access, comparing farms of similar size but different technology adoption, or comparing farms of different sizes in identical institutional contexts, to isolate scale effects from institutional effects.

**Why This Matters for Your Work:** If scale is causal, your interventions must target scale itself (consolidation or fragmentation). If scale is correlational with institutions, your interventions can be scale-neutral but institution-focused, potentially achieving sustainability improvements across all farm sizes through institutional reform rather than scale change.

### Question 2: Why Definitional Frameworks Matter for Comparative Analysis

**The Question:** Kirsten et al. (1998) identifies multiple incompatible ways to define "small-scale" farms (hectarage, labor, mechanization, market integration, income). Explain why this definitional problem is not merely a technical issue, and what consequences follow if researchers use different definitions across studies claiming to compare the same phenomenon.

**The Answer:** Definitional choice is not technical but ontological—it determines what empirical object you are actually studying. A 5-hectare mechanized farm exporting vegetables is "small" by hectarage but "large" by mechanization level and market integration; it would be classified completely differently under various definitional schemes. If study A defines small farms by hectarage and study B by labor intensity, they are comparing non-equivalent categories, making synthesis impossible. Moreover, definitional choice shapes conclusions: if you define by size and compare to large farms, you find small farms are labor-intensive; if you define by market integration and compare, you find small farms are less market-connected. The same farms appear in multiple categories, producing apparent contradictions that reflect definitional differences rather than empirical disagreement. Guiomar et al. (2018) addresses this by developing a multidimensional typology that captures heterogeneity within size categories, enabling comparison across types rather than claiming small vs. large monolithic categories exist.

**Why This Matters for Your Work:** You cannot compare production systems without explicitly defining what you are comparing. Any comparative analysis requires transparent classification criteria that enable readers to understand what empirical categories are actually being studied and what variation is hidden within those categories. Inherited categories ("small farm," "agro-industrial unit") must be either operationalized explicitly or replaced with typologically grounded classification systems.

### Question 3: The Measurement Infrastructure Problem

**The Question:** Latruffe et al. (2016) and Bathaei et al. (2023) document that environmental and economic sustainability dimensions have relatively well-developed measurement frameworks while social sustainability lags significantly. What does this asymmetry in measurement sophistication suggest about the current knowledge base regarding scale and sustainability, and what types of false conclusions might result from this asymmetry?

**The Answer:** If environmental metrics are well-developed but social metrics are underdeveloped, the field can robustly claim small farms have high biodiversity or large farms have high productivity, but cannot robustly assess small farms' labor conditions, gender equity, livelihood quality, or community impacts. This produces a distorted knowledge base where environmental and economic dimensions appear more important than social dimensions simply because they are more measurable, not because they are more important. It enables researchers to claim "small farms are more sustainable" (based on biodiversity data) or "large farms are more sustainable" (based on productivity data) while ignoring social outcomes entirely. The solution is not to abandon environmental or economic measurement but to develop social metrics to equivalent sophistication, enabling simultaneous assessment rather than selective measurement. Zhang et al. (2021) addresses this by developing independent quantitative metrics for all three pillars, but such integrated approaches remain uncommon.

**Why This Matters for Your Work:** Any comparative sustainability assessment you conduct must be transparent about measurement asymmetries—which dimensions you can measure robustly and which remain incomplete. Incomplete measurement means incomplete conclusions. You should either develop new social sustainability metrics, or explicitly acknowledge that your comparison is environmental-economic but not comprehensive, rather than claiming broad sustainability conclusions from partial assessment.

### Question 4: The Hidden Assumption in Farm-Level Sustainability Assessment

**The Question:** Lanjouw (2001) shows that rural households in developing countries derive income from multiple sources (farming, wage labor, enterprise), often with non-farm income exceeding farm income. What does this imply about the validity of farm-level sustainability assessments that measure agricultural productivity as a proxy for household viability, and how would household-level assessment differ from farm-level assessment?

**The Answer:** Farm-level sustainability metrics (productivity, soil health, inputs used) tell you nothing about whether households dependent on those farms are viable or food-secure. A household with a low-productivity small farm but multiple income sources may be more resilient and food-secure than a household with a high-productivity specialized farm vulnerable to commodity price volatility. Farm-level productivity is not linearly related to household-level sustainability. This means rural livelihood sustainability requires assessing not farm characteristics but household income portfolios, which are distributed across multiple activities. For a household-level assessment, you would measure income sources and their stability, asset accumulation capacity, food security outcomes, and shock-response capacity—entirely different metrics than farm productivity. A farm that appears "inefficient" at farm-level may be optimal at household-level because it requires lower input investment and leaves labor available for other income-generating activities.

**Why This Matters for Your Work:** You must be explicit about analytical scale—are you assessing farm-level sustainability or household/livelihood-level sustainability? These are different questions with different metrics and different answers. Small farms often appear less productive at farm-level but more viable at household-level due to livelihood diversification. Claims about sustainability at one scale cannot be extrapolated to other scales without explicit justification.

### Question 5: Technology Adoption as Decoupled from Scale

**The Question:** Van Loon et al. (2020) and Daum (2023) document that mechanization adoption can be decoupled from farm scale through institutional innovations (cooperatives, equipment hire services, custom hire arrangements) and appropriate-scale technology development. What does this imply about previous research claiming mechanization is scale-dependent, and what mechanisms explain why mechanization adoption typically appears correlated with scale even if it is theoretically independent?

**The Answer:** If mechanization is theoretically scale-neutral but empirically correlated with scale in many contexts, the correlation reflects institutional bias—credit systems, extension services, and input supply chains favoring large operations—rather than technological necessity. Previous research claiming mechanization requires large scale was documenting institutional reality, not technological constraint. Van Loon et al. show that once institutional barriers are removed through cooperatives (collective credit), appropriate-scale technologies (smaller tractors, draft animals, shared equipment), and hire services (custom hire for peak labor periods), smallholders can mechanize selectively. The mechanism is institutional access, not scale. This implies that observed correlations between scale and technology adoption in cross-sectional data reflect the institutional context, and changing that context (without changing farm scale) could enable technology adoption at any scale.

**Why This Matters for Your Work:** Do not conflate observed correlations between scale and technology with necessary relationships. If you observe that large farms are more mechanized, this could reflect technological necessity or institutional advantage—your research design must distinguish between these possibilities. You can do this by (a) examining whether mechanization works at small scale under favorable institutional conditions (it does), or (b) comparing farms in similar institutional contexts that differ only in scale.

### Question 6: Context-Contingency and Why Universal Claims Fail

**The Question:** Fischer & van Velthuizen (2002) established agroecological zones methodology, and Lawrence & Almas (2002) emphasized globalization's varying impacts. What does this imply about the validity of claims like "small farms are more sustainable" or "large farms are more economically viable" without explicit reference to agroecological and institutional context?

**The Answer:** Context-contingency means these claims are not false but incomplete—they may be true in some contexts and false in others. A small-scale subsistence farm in a high-rainfall, high-biodiversity tropical region has entirely different constraints and capabilities than a small-scale commercial farm in a semi-arid region with established export markets. Large mechanized farms optimize for uniform, flat terrain with commodity-scale production; they are economic disasters in mountainous terrain with small dispersed markets. Agroecological context (climate, soil, topography, water availability) and institutional context (market access, credit availability, land tenure security, policy environment) are not external factors that modify scale effects—they are constitutive of what scale effects actually are. The claim "small farms are more sustainable" is only valid with the complete statement: "in contexts where (ecological conditions + institutional arrangements) enable labor-intensive management and market access to premium-priced products." The apparent universality of previous claims reflected the authors' specific contexts, not universal laws.

**Why This Matters for Your Work:** Any comparison you conduct is context-specific. You must explicitly specify the agroecological zone, institutional environment, market conditions, and policy context, because your findings will only apply to contexts with similar characteristics. Avoid claiming general sustainability principles from particular cases; instead, develop theoretical understanding of which mechanisms operate under which conditions.

### Question 7: The Value Choices Hidden in Sustainability Assessment

**The Question:** Zhang et al. (2021) shows that nations prioritize different sustainability dimensions (environmental, social, economic) and that this reveals explicit or implicit value choices. What does this suggest about claims that one production system is "more sustainable" when it scores higher on some dimensions and lower on others, and what methodology could surface these hidden value choices?

**The Answer:** Claiming one system is "more sustainable overall" while ignoring trade-offs between dimensions is making hidden value choices about which sustainability dimensions matter more. A system with high environmental sustainability but low social sustainability (e.g., mechanized production using minimal labor but producing industrial monocultures) is not "more sustainable"—it is sustainable in environmental terms but unsustainable in social terms. These are not aggregable into a single "sustainability score" without explicit value weighting. To surface these choices, you would need to either (a) ask stakeholders directly what sustainability means to them and weight dimensions accordingly (participatory approaches like deliberative polling or Q-methodology), or (b) develop separate metrics for each dimension and present findings separately, enabling readers to weight dimensions according to their own values. The appearance of scientific objectivity in "overall sustainability" scores masks the underlying value choices.

**Why This Matters for Your Work:** Do not present sustainability as a unidimensional property. If you assess multiple dimensions, present findings for each separately and acknowledge that integration requires value choices. Be transparent about what you are optimizing for and what trade-offs this involves. If your findings show that a system is environmentally sustainable but economically fragile, report both—do not combine them into an overall score that obscures the trade-off.

### Question 8: The Institutional Mediation of Scale Effects

**The Question:** Lawrence & Almas (2002) situate agricultural systems within global institutional structures, and Lanjouw (2001) shows institutional access shapes livelihood viability. Combined with Ebel's (2020) demonstration that scale does not determine sustainability, what does this suggest about the possibility that institutional reform could alter scale-sustainability relationships entirely, making currently observed correlations contingent rather than inevitable?

**The Answer:** If observed scale-sustainability correlations reflect institutional context rather than inherent properties of scale, then institutional reform could produce radically different correlations. In a context where land tenure is insecure, credit is unavailable, and extension services are absent, small farms appear unviable; but in a context where tenure is secure, credit is accessible through cooperatives, and extension is community-based, the same small farms appear economically competitive. The same institutional structures that currently favor large operations (economies of scale in credit access, concentration of extension services, large-farm-friendly infrastructure) could be reformed to be scale-neutral or small-farm-favoring. This suggests that the question "which scale is more sustainable?" may be answerable only contextually—the right scale for a given context depends on institutional design, and institutional reform could change which scale is optimal. This is theoretically important because it moves the locus of intervention from trying to change farm scale to trying to change institutions that mediate scale effects.

**Why This Matters for Your Work:** Do not treat institutional context as background or control variable to factor out—treat it as a central variable that determines scale-sustainability relationships. Your comparative analysis could examine how the same production scales perform under different institutional arrangements, or how institutional reforms change the viability of different scales. This approach enables you to address not just "what works" but "what works for whom under what conditions."
