## Overview

The theoretical landscape reveals that the scale-sustainability question has been misframed as a comparison problem when it is actually a systems architecture problem. Current approaches treat 'small-scale' and 'agro-industrial' as discrete categories to be compared on performance metrics, but the evidence pattern suggests these are actually different organizational architectures embedded in distinct socio-technical-ecological systems. Four major theoretical directions emerge: (1) Multi-scale nested systems theories that reconceptualize sustainability as an emergent property of farm-household-landscape-institutional configurations rather than farm-level attributes; (2) Value chain and power structure theories that relocate the unit of analysis from production units to food system architectures, treating scale as an outcome of supply chain power rather than a cause of performance; (3) Context-contingent mechanism theories that abandon universal scale effects in favor of interaction-centered models where scale-sustainability relationships emerge from specific combinations of agroecological, institutional, and technological contexts; and (4) Temporal dynamics theories that treat sustainability as a time-variant process operating across multiple timescales simultaneously, where fast variables (annual yields) obscure slow variables (soil degradation, institutional erosion, climate adaptation capacity). These proposals share a rejection of the foundational assumption that scale itself drives outcomes, instead treating observed scale patterns as symptoms of deeper structural forces — market integration, mechanization trajectories, institutional arrangements, knowledge systems, and power asymmetries — that constitute the actual causal architecture determining sustainability performance.

#### [novel] Temporal dynamics theory treating sustainability as a time-variant emergent property operating across multiple nested timescales (seasonal-annual-decadal-generational), where fast-variable optimization in agricultural production systems (annual yields, immediate profits, short-term productivity gains) systematically depletes slow-variable capital stocks (soil organic matter, microbiome diversity, aquifer levels, institutional trust, adaptive capacity, traditional ecological knowledge, genetic diversity) through differential rates of accumulation and depletion that remain invisible to snapshot assessments. This framework posits that small-scale and agro-industrial systems operate on fundamentally different temporal signatures: agro-industrial systems maximize fast-variable extraction while externalizing slow-variable costs across time and space, whereas small-scale systems embed slow-variable maintenance within production practices but appear inefficient when measured on fast-variable metrics. The theory predicts that systems ranked as sustainable or productive under short-term assessment horizons will demonstrate divergent trajectories when evaluated across decadal or generational timescales, with current 'winners' revealed as depleting foundational capitals while current 'laggards' maintain or build resilience capacity.

The proposal deliberately excludes: (1) specific recommendations about optimal farm scales or production systems (focuses on assessment framework not prescriptive conclusions), (2) detailed technical specifications for every slow variable (identifies key categories but full operationalization requires domain-specific expertise), (3) resolution of normative questions about appropriate temporal discount rates (identifies that these are political choices but does not prescribe values), (4) comprehensive catalog of all possible fast and slow variables (provides exemplars and framework rather than exhaustive enumeration), (5) integration with spatial dynamics beyond recognizing that slow variables often operate at larger spatial scales (leaves detailed spatial-temporal integration to future work building on this foundation)
