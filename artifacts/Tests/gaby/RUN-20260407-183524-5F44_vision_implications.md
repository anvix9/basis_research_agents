## Overview

The logical landscape reveals a research field operating under profound methodological uncertainty masquerading as empirical knowledge. The strongest implications cluster around measurement validity: without integrated three-pillar assessment, experimental designs, or longitudinal tracking, current comparative claims rest on correlational patterns that conflate scale with its proxies (management, technology, market access, institutional support). The foundational assumption that scale itself drives sustainability has never been validated, suggesting decades of research may have analyzed the wrong variable. The systematic absence of sociology and incomplete externality accounting imply that 'sustainability' assessments are actually partial economic-environmental evaluations with social dimensions treated as unmeasured residuals. Definitional inconsistency across studies means apparent empirical contradictions may be artifacts of comparing non-equivalent categories rather than genuine disagreements. The field exhibits a streetlight effect: sophisticated measurement of easily-quantifiable variables (productivity, biodiversity counts) while avoiding hard-to-measure dimensions (resilience, power, adaptation, justice). Complexity systems thinking demands recognition that sustainability is an emergent property of nested multi-scale systems across multiple time horizons, rendering farm-level assessments fundamentally incomplete. The value-ladenness of trade-offs means technical optimization is impossible without prior resolution of political questions about whose sustainability matters. Climate adaptation capacity being unmeasured implies current rankings evaluate past-condition performance rather than future viability. The agribusiness concept enabling analysis may artificially fragment integrated systems, making farm-level assessment a category error. Second-order implications suggest scale may be analytically useless due to within-category heterogeneity exceeding between-category variation, mechanization may have threshold effects requiring safe operating space identification, and knowledge systems may be epistemologically incommensurable. The field faces a paradox: knowing production distributions empirically while lacking causal understanding of why they exist or how to intentionally shape them. The logical conclusion is that comparative sustainability assessment in its current form is methodologically premature — the infrastructure for valid comparison does not yet exist.
