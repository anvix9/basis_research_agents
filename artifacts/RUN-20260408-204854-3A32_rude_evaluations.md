## Feasibility Summary

The proposal landscape divides sharply into three tiers: (1) Genuinely empirical proposals leveraging neuroscience and comparative cognition with robust methodologies and massive existing evidence bases—these are highly feasible and would directly advance understanding of intelligence as natural phenomenon. (2) Hybrid proposals attempting to bridge philosophical categories and empirical findings—partially feasible but face fundamental commensurability challenges, as ancient normative/ethical categories may not map onto cognitive mechanisms discoverable by neuroscience. (3) Intellectual history and critical theory proposals—not empirically feasible as framed because they investigate the history and social functions of intelligence concepts rather than intelligence itself. The gap between what has been philosophically theorized and what has been empirically demonstrated is vast. Most philosophical proposals about intelligence categories (Aristotelian virtues, Platonic divisions, medieval intellect distinctions) have never been operationalized for empirical testing and may not be testable in principle. The genuinely empirical tradition (psychometrics, neuroscience, comparative cognition) largely developed independently of philosophical taxonomies rather than testing them. The strongest path forward is to pursue direct empirical investigation while treating historical-philosophical proposals as heuristics rather than hypotheses. What is most empirically accessible: neural correlates of cognitive functions, behavioral comparisons across species, psychometric structure, genetic contributions. What is least empirically accessible: whether ancient philosophical categories carve nature at its joints, whether AI is 'really' intelligent vs. simulating intelligence, whether consciousness is necessary for intelligence. The proposals attempting to empirically adjudicate the latter face operational definition problems that may be unsolvable with current methods.
