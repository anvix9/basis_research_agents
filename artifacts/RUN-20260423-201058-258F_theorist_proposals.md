## Overview

The complete absence of connexin 43 and gap junction investigation in Marfan syndrome, Loeys-Dietz syndrome, and related fibrillinopathies represents an extraordinary opportunity to test whether electrical coupling defects contribute to cardiovascular pathology in genetic connective tissue disorders. The convergence of known TGF-β dysregulation in these syndromes with established TGF-β regulation of connexin 43 in other contexts creates a mechanistic hypothesis that can be directly tested. Furthermore, the documented but mechanistically unexplained increased arrhythmic risk in these patients demands investigation of connexin 43 remodeling as a canonical arrhythmogenic mechanism.

The proposal landscape spans from foundational molecular characterization (establishing whether connexin 43 alterations exist) through mechanistic investigation (determining causal relationships with TGF-β signaling, mechanical stress, and disease progression) to translational exploration (testing connexin modulation as therapeutic strategy). Given the complete disciplinary silence, even basic descriptive studies represent novel contributions, while mechanistic and temporal investigations address critical questions about whether connexin changes are pathogenic drivers or downstream consequences. The therapeutic dimension remains entirely hypothetical but could open new intervention strategies if connexin alterations prove functionally significant.
