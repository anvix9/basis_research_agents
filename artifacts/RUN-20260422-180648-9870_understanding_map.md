# RESEARCH UNDERSTANDING MAP: SCHOLASTICIDE AND INTERNATIONAL LAW

## 1. The Territory at a Glance

This field occupies a paradoxical space where comprehensive legal frameworks exist but contain critical blind spots. International humanitarian law, human rights law, and international criminal law have developed sophisticated doctrines for protecting civilians, civilian objects, and cultural property during armed conflict—yet "scholasticide" (the systematic destruction of universities and massacre of university students) has no recognized legal category. The central organizing tension is between **categorical adequacy and conceptual invisibility**: existing frameworks (civilian protection, cultural property, crimes against humanity) can theoretically capture attacks on universities, but they consistently undervalue the distinctive harm of educational destruction by forcing it into categories designed for other purposes. The field faces a methodological crisis: frameworks exist for proving systematic violence constitutes war crimes (the ICJ Application case methodology for inferring intent from conduct patterns; Srebrenica jurisprudence on geographically concentrated massacres), but these frameworks have never been applied to document or prosecute attacks on educational institutions specifically. The deepest unresolved question is whether universities require sui generis legal analysis as hybrid objects—simultaneously cultural heritage, civilian infrastructure, and sites of ongoing intellectual production—or whether existing categories adequately protect them. This is not a gap in international law's ambition but a gap in its application, and it reveals something structural: the law protects things (objects, property) and categories of people (nationals, ethnicities, religions, genders) but has never theorized protection of *knowledge-generating communities* as such.

## 2. The Intellectual Genealogy — How We Got Here

The story of scholasticide in international law is one of **expanding protections that consistently miss their target**. 

The genealogy begins in 1899-1907, when the Hague Peace Conferences established the first laws of war protecting civilian objects. These rules emerged from a specific historical crisis: industrial warfare's expansion of legitimate targets threatened to destroy the cultural heritage of entire nations. The response was categorical: protect "cultural property" and "civilian objects" by defining what *cannot* be attacked. This logic persisted through the Genocide Convention (1948) and Geneva Conventions (1949), which established protected *groups* (national, ethnic, racial, religious) and protected *things* (hospitals, schools, places of worship marked with symbols). The architecture was legible: define the category, establish the boundary, enforce the rule.

But this categorical approach contained a hidden assumption: that protection could work through enumeration. The Genocide Convention listed four protected groups; the Geneva Conventions listed specific civilian objects. Universities existed in this framework only as instances of "schools" or "cultural property"—generic categories that did not account for their distinctive function as sites where knowledge communities reproduce themselves across generations. The law protected the *building* or the *general category* but not the *institutional capacity* for knowledge production.

The conceptual architecture held through the Cold War because international law focused on interstate conflicts where educational institutions, while vulnerable, were rarely *systematically targeted as a strategy*. The law's categories worked adequately precisely because they were tested against scenarios (interstate war, occupation, internal conflict) where educational destruction was incidental to military objectives rather than constitutive of them.

This changed with post-Cold War humanitarian crises, particularly the Balkans. The International Criminal Tribunal for Yugoslavia (ICTY) developed jurisprudence on crimes against humanity and ethnic cleansing that recognized systematic targeting of civilian populations. The Srebrenica case (1995) became pivotal: it established that massacre of a *geographically concentrated segment* of a protected population within a limited area could constitute genocide, even if the perpetrators did not intend to destroy the entire group. This was conceptually revolutionary because it severed genocide from the requirement of total destruction, allowing prosecution of systematic killing of subgroups. Katherine Southwick's 2005 analysis of the Krstić decision documented how the tribunal inferred genocidal intent from *patterns of conduct*: the geographic concentration, the scale of killing, the targeting of military-aged men, the contextual evidence of dehumanizing rhetoric.

Simultaneously, the International Court of Justice developed methodology for proving genocidal intent through circumstantial evidence (Application of the Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide, 2015). The ICJ established that specific intent to destroy a protected group need not be proven through direct evidence but can be inferred from: (1) patterns of conduct, (2) scale of destruction, (3) public utterances, (4) contextual evidence of dehumanization. This evidentiary framework was revolutionary because it made prosecution of systematic violence possible even when perpetrators denied genocidal purpose—the pattern itself became probative.

Meanwhile, a parallel development occurred in human rights law. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) established education as a fundamental human right. Kenneth D. Bush and Diana Saltarelli's 2000 analysis challenged the assumption that education is inevitably beneficial, documenting how education *becomes a target* in ethnic conflicts precisely because it is so constitutive of group identity and future capacity. This theoretical recognition that educational systems are strategic targets in conflict created the conceptual possibility that systematic attacks on universities might not be incidental violence but *calculated strategy*.

David A. Lewis's 2006 comprehensive legal analysis of educational institution protection under IHL was the field's first direct engagement with the question: what does international humanitarian law actually say about protecting schools and universities during active combat? Lewis documented that IHL provides protection through multiple pathways (civilian object status, cultural property protections, medical/religious facility analogies), but these protections rest on generic civilian object doctrine designed for different purposes. His analysis revealed the categorical problem: universities are protected, but *not as universities*—protected as civilian objects, which is like protecting a library by applying rules designed for protecting civilian housing.

The post-colonial turn in legal scholarship (Eve Tuck and K. Wayne Yang, 2012; Catherine Lu, 2017; Michael Birnhack, 2021) opened a new possibility: examining how colonial-era legal structures persist in contemporary law. This lineage was crucial because it created space to ask whether British Mandate-era legal definitions of "protected property" or "educational institutions" might exclude Palestinian universities from protection—whether the law's blindness to scholasticide might have colonial roots, built into the categorical apparatus inherited from occupation frameworks.

Most recently, the ICC's work under Machteld Boot's (2002) systematic taxonomy of Rome Statute crimes, and Leena Grover's (2014) hermeneutic methodology for interpreting Rome Statute crimes, created sophisticated frameworks for classifying mass atrocities. Brook Sari Moshan's 1998 analysis of gender in the Rome Statute showed that the law *could evolve* to recognize new forms of targeted violence: gender-based crimes were added not because the underlying violence was new, but because the law finally recognized that systematic sexual violence constituted a distinctive harm requiring specific categorization. This precedent for norm evolution suggested that scholasticide *could* be similarly recognized—if scholarship made the case that attacks on universities constitute distinctive harm requiring new legal categorization.

Yet despite all these developments, scholasticide remains unrecognized. The Srebrenica jurisprudence could theoretically apply to university students as a geographically concentrated subgroup of Palestinians. The ICJ evidentiary framework could theoretically prove intent through patterns of educational destruction. The dignity-based human rights frameworks could theoretically protect education as constitutive of human dignity. The Rome Statute's expansion to include gender-based crimes sets a precedent for evolving protected categories. But these theoretical possibilities have never been combined and operationalized into actual prosecution, actual documentation, actual legal claim. The field has developed all the conceptual tools for recognizing scholasticide—and used none of them. The genealogy is thus a history of *near-misses*: frameworks that could apply but haven't, precedents that suggest evolution but haven't led to it, doctrinal developments that create space for new categories but leave that space empty.

The question "why?" reveals the deepest problem: international law protects things and categories of people, but it has never theorized protection of *communities of knowledge producers*. Universities are not a protected group under the Genocide Convention because they are not national, ethnic, racial, or religious groups—they are functional communities defined by intellectual practice. They are not protected as "cultural property" in the UNESCO sense because they are not primarily heritage sites but living institutions. They are not protected as "critical infrastructure" in the laws of war because that doctrine developed for water systems and power grids, not knowledge. And they are not centered in human rights law as fundamentally constitutive of human dignity because dignity frameworks typically protect individual rights (to education, to freedom of thought) rather than institutional capacity for knowledge production.

This genealogy reveals that scholasticide's invisibility in international law is not accidental—it is *structural*. The law's categorical architecture produces this blindness. To recognize scholasticide requires not just applying existing doctrines but fundamentally rethinking whether protection should extend to *institutions that reproduce knowledge communities across generations*, not just to individuals or buildings or abstract cultural heritage. This is the intellectual frontier where the field must move.

## 3. The Reading Curriculum

### **TIER 1 — FOUNDATIONS (Read First)**

**Genocide Convention (1948) — United Nations**
- **Why read at this tier:** This is the foundational legal definition you must understand before any discussion of genocide claims. Without precise knowledge of what the Convention actually says (not what people claim it says), you cannot evaluate whether scholasticide fits within genocidal categories or whether new categories must be created.
- **What to look for:** (1) The four protected groups—national, ethnic, racial, religious—and notice what is *absent* (occupational groups? knowledge communities? educators?). (2) The definition of genocidal acts (killing, causing serious bodily or mental harm, deliberately inflicting conditions to destroy the group). (3) The critical "intent" requirement—the Convention requires "intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnic, racial or religious group, *as such*." This "as such" language is doing crucial doctrinal work. (4) Notice the gap: the Convention protects groups defined by identity (who you are) but not groups defined by function (what you do). This gap is foundational to understanding why scholasticide is invisible.
- **Connects to:** The Srebrenica jurisprudence (Southwick 2005) shows how courts interpret the "in part" language to allow prosecution of geographically concentrated killings. The ICJ Application case (2015) shows how "intent" can be inferred from circumstantial evidence. The Moshan article (1998) shows how the Convention has evolved to include new forms of group-targeted violence through reinterpretation.

**Geneva Conventions (1949) and Additional Protocols — International Committee of the Red Cross**
- **Why read at this tier:** These are the binding rules that actually govern what you can do in armed conflict. They establish the framework for civilian protection, civilian object protection, and specific protections for hospitals, religious sites, and cultural property. Understanding the actual text (not summaries of it) is essential because international humanitarian law is textually precise—the difference between "attacks" and "damage," between "incidental" and "intentional," between "civilian objects" and "military objectives" shapes what violations are possible.
- **What to look for:** (1) The definition of "civilian objects" and notice how it works through *negation*—anything that is not making an "effective contribution to military action" is a civilian object. (2) How does this apply to universities? A university *might* contribute to military action (it could train engineers, it could house military facilities), but if it doesn't, it's protected. (3) The prohibition on "indiscriminate attacks"—attacks that strike both military and civilian targets without distinction. (4) The "proportionality" requirement—even attacks on military objectives must not cause excessive civilian harm. (5) The specific provisions protecting medical units, religious sites, and places of worship. (6) Notice what is *missing*: specific provisions protecting educational institutions *as such*, the way hospitals are protected. (7) Look at the Additional Protocols—what new protections do they add? This reveals what the 1949 Conventions left unprotected.
- **Connects to:** Lewis's 2006 analysis of educational institution protection shows that universities must be prosecuted under the generic "civilian object" framework, not under dedicated protections like those for hospitals. The Czepek & Karska (2023) analysis of UNESCO-ICC cooperation shows how cultural property protections could theoretically apply to universities but remain underdeveloped.

**Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) — United Nations General Assembly**
- **Why read at this tier:** This establishes education as a human right, creating the legal foundation for arguing that destruction of educational institutions violates human rights, not just humanitarian law. It also establishes "human dignity" as foundational to all rights—a concept that will be crucial for dignity-based arguments for protecting educational capacity.
- **What to look for:** (1) Article 26 on the right to education and its specific language about education's purpose: "directed to the full development of the human personality" and "strengthening respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms." (2) The Preamble's repeated invocation of "dignity" as foundational. (3) Notice that the UDHR frames education not as mere access to schooling but as essential to human development and to capacity for participation in a democratic society. (4) How might this aspiration language translate into binding legal obligations in contemporary international law?
- **Connects to:** McCrudden's 2008 analysis shows how "dignity" evolved from aspirational language in the UDHR to become an operative legal principle in human rights interpretation. This creates the possibility of a dignity-based argument for protecting educational institutions as necessary to human dignity.

**The contemporary law of armed conflict — Leslie C. Green (1993)**
- **Why read at this tier:** This is the foundational educational text that consolidated post-Cold War understanding of international humanitarian law as applied to asymmetric conflicts and occupations. Green's comprehensive synthesis is crucial because it establishes the basic framework you need before engaging with specialized applications.
- **What to look for:** (1) How does Green define "civilian" and "civilian objects" and what does he say about borderline cases? (2) His treatment of occupation law—what are the occupier's obligations toward civilian institutions? (3) How does Green discuss targeting rules and proportionality? (4) What does he say about protection of cultural property and educational institutions? (5) His analysis of non-international armed conflicts—how do protections differ? (6) Look for any discussion of why certain institutions (hospitals, religious sites) receive specific protection while others (schools, universities) are covered only by generic civilian object status.
- **Connects to:** All subsequent applications of IHL to specific scenarios (education, gender, post-conflict accountability) build on the foundational framework Green establishes.

### **TIER 2 — THE MAIN DEBATES (Read Second)**

**Srebrenica as Genocide? The Krstić Decision and the Language of the Unspeakable — Katherine Southwick (2005)**
- **Why read at this tier:** This is where you see how courts *actually interpret* genocide law when confronted with systematic mass killing. The Krstić case is precedent-setting because it established that genocide can be prosecuted for massacre of a *segment* of a protected population within a limited geographic area, even when the perpetrators didn't intend to destroy the entire group. This jurisprudence could theoretically apply to Palestinian university students as a concentrated subgroup.
- **What to look for:** (1) How does the ICTY determine that killing of 8,000 Bosniak men and boys constitutes genocide rather than crimes against humanity? (2) What evidence does the tribunal use to infer genocidal intent—Southwick should show you the methodology. (3) How does the tribunal handle the "in whole or in part" language from the Genocide Convention? What makes a "part" large enough to constitute genocide? (4) What role does geographic concentration play in the analysis? (5) What role do dehumanizing statements and public discourse play in proving intent? (6) How does Southwick assess the tribunal's reasoning—what are the strengths and weaknesses? (7) Could this same logic apply to concentration of university students in Gaza over a defined period?
- **Connects to:** The ICJ Application case (2015) develops this methodology further, showing how intent can be inferred from patterns of conduct even without direct evidence.

**Application of the Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide — International Court of Justice (2015)**
- **Why read at this tier:** This is the authoritative contemporary methodology for proving genocidal intent in international courts. The ICJ establishes that specific intent need not be proven through direct evidence (perpetrators confessing genocidal purpose) but can be inferred from circumstantial evidence: patterns of conduct, scale of destruction, contextual evidence, public rhetoric, official documents. This is the evidentiary framework that scholasticide claims would need to satisfy.
- **What to look for:** (1) How does the ICJ define "specific intent" (intent to destroy a protected group "as such") and distinguish it from general intent? (2) What evidence does the ICJ accept as probative of intent? Focus on the list: What makes a pattern of conduct probative? How much destruction is enough to infer intent? (3) How does the court handle inconsistent evidence or evidence of mixed motives? (4) What role do public statements and official documents play? (5) How does the ICJ handle the "in whole or in part" language? (6) What standard of proof applies—beyond reasonable doubt? Preponderance? (7) For scholasticide claims, the critical question: could pattern of educational infrastructure destruction, combined with scale of student casualties, documentary evidence, and public discourse, meet this evidentiary standard?
- **Connects to:** Southwick's 2005 analysis shows how courts apply these principles in practice. Lewis's 2006 analysis shows what the actual legal status of educational institutions is under IHL.

**The Protection of Civilian Educational Institutions During the Active Hostilities of International Armed Conflict in International Humanitarian Law — David A. Lewis (2006)**
- **Why read at this tier:** This is the only comprehensive legal analysis that specifically addresses what international humanitarian law says about protecting schools and universities during armed conflict. Lewis reveals the categorical problem: educational institutions are protected, but *not as educational institutions*—they must be prosecuted under generic civilian object doctrine or cultural property frameworks.
- **What to look for:** (1) What are the different legal pathways for protecting educational institutions under IHL? Lewis should identify civilian object protection, cultural property protections, and any specialized provisions. (2) What gaps does Lewis identify between these generic protections and the specific needs of educational institutions? (3) Does Lewis argue that educational institutions require *sui generis* analysis, or does he conclude that existing frameworks are adequate? (4) What is the legal status of universities in occupied territories? Do occupiers have affirmative obligations to maintain educational systems? (5) How does Lewis address attacks that destroy educational buildings but don't directly kill students—are these violations of IHL? (6) What about attacks on educational infrastructure that don't destroy buildings but disrupt function (shutting down universities, preventing access)? (7) Lewis's conclusion: what would be required to create specific legal protections for educational institutions distinct from generic civilian object doctrine?
- **Connects to:** This is the foundational work on your specific substantive question. Everything about scholasticide as a legal claim depends on understanding what Lewis documents about educational institutions' legal status.

**The Two Faces of Education in Ethnic Conflict: Towards a Peacebuilding Education for Children — Kenneth D. Bush & Diana Saltarelli (2000)**
- **Why read at this tier:** This work explains *why* education becomes a target in ethnic conflicts—not from a legal perspective, but from a sociological and conflict-analysis perspective. This is crucial for understanding the intentionality question: Are attacks on universities incidental collateral damage, or are they strategic targeting of institutions that reproduce group identity and future capacity?
- **What to look for:** (1) Bush and Saltarelli's argument about education's dual nature—how it can either build peace or entrench conflict depending on curriculum and implementation. (2) Why do ethnic conflicts target educational institutions specifically? What do perpetrators believe they're accomplishing? (3) The distinction between targeting for instrumentalization (controlling curriculum) versus targeting for elimination. (4) How does educational destruction affect long-term group capacity? What is the difference between killing individuals and destroying the institutions through which a group reproduces itself? (5) Bush and Saltarelli should provide sociological evidence that educational destruction is strategic, not incidental. (6) How does this analysis help prove intent in legal proceedings—if educational institutions are systematically targeted, does this evidence strategic purpose beyond military objectives?
- **Connects to:** The dehumanization literature (Slovic 2007, Kteily & Bruneau 2015) explains the psychological mechanisms that make targeting educational institutions possible. The post-colonial literature (Tuck & Yang 2012, Lu 2017) explains how colonial structures embed educational destruction in legal and institutional frameworks.

**Genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes: nullum crimen sine lege and the subject matter jurisdiction of the International Criminal Court — Machteld Boot (2002)**
- **Why read at this tier:** Boot provides the systematic taxonomy of international crimes as defined in the Rome Statute. This is essential reference material for understanding exactly what constitutes a "war crime" under international law and how crimes are classified and distinguished.
- **What to look for:** (1) Boot's definitions of genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes—what are the essential elements of each? (2) How does Boot explain the "nullum crimen sine lege" principle—the requirement that conduct must violate a pre-existing rule of law? (3) What crimes does the Rome Statute specifically define, and what gaps exist? (4) How does Boot handle borderline cases—conduct that might fit multiple crime categories? (5) What is Boot's analysis of "systematic" violence and how it relates to crimes against humanity? (6) For scholasticide: could it be prosecuted as a war crime (unlawful attack on civilian object), a crime against humanity (systematic attack on civilian population), genocide (destruction of a protected group or subgroup), or would it require a new crime category?
- **Connects to:** Grover's 2014 interpretive methodology shows how courts apply Boot's taxonomy in practice. The Rome Statute itself (in the seminal works list) defines the actual crime categories.

**Interpreting Crimes in the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court — Leena Grover (2014)**
- **Why read at this tier:** Grover provides sophisticated hermeneutic methodology for interpreting Rome Statute crimes after a decade of ICC operation. This shows how courts actually classify mass atrocities and what interpretive moves are necessary to fit new forms of violence into existing legal categories.
- **What to look for:** (1) Grover's methodology for statutory interpretation—how should courts read ambiguous language in crime definitions? (2) How does Grover address the tension between strict construction (crimes must be precisely defined) and purposive interpretation (courts must recognize the underlying harm)? (3) What interpretive strategies has the ICC used to apply Rome Statute crimes to fact patterns that don't perfectly fit the textual definition? (4) For example, how has the ICC expanded the definition of "attack" in crimes against humanity to cover non-kinetic violence? (5) What does Grover say about creating new crime categories versus stretching existing categories to fit new facts? (6) Her conclusion: is the Rome Statute flexible enough to encompass scholasticide through reinterpretation, or would scholasticide require new norm development?
- **Connects to:** Moshan's 1998 analysis shows how gender-based crimes were added to the Rome Statute through interpretive evolution, setting a precedent for how new categories can be recognized.

**Women, War, and Words: The Gender Component in the Permanent International Criminal Court's Definition of Crimes Against Humanity — Brook Sari Moshan (1998)**
- **Why read at this tier:** Moshan documents how the Rome Statute expanded the definition of crimes against humanity to include gender-based crimes, specifically systematic sexual violence. This is precedent for how international law *can evolve* to recognize new forms of targeted violence. It also shows the argumentative strategy for norm expansion: establishing that a particular form of violence is (1) systematic, (2) targeted at a specific group, (3) produces distinctive harm requiring specific legal categorization.
- **What to look for:** (1) What was the pre-Rome Statute legal status of sexual violence in warfare? Why wasn't it prosecuted as a war crime or crime against humanity before 1998? (2) What changed to make gender-based crimes explicitly recognized? Was there new violence, or new legal consciousness? (3) Moshan's argument: Why does sexual violence require specific categorization rather than just being prosecuted under existing "crime against humanity" doctrine? (4) What evidence did advocates present that sexual violence constitutes a distinctive harm? (5) How does Moshan's analysis translate to scholasticide: Could similar arguments be made that educational destruction constitutes a distinctive harm requiring specific legal categorization? (6) What would it take to expand the Rome Statute to recognize scholasticide as a distinct crime?
- **Connects to:** The post-colonial literature (Tuck & Yang 2012) shows how marginalized groups achieve legal recognition by demonstrating that existing categories exclude their specific harms.

**Decolonization is not a metaphor — Eve Tuck & K. Wayne Yang (2012)**
- **Why read at this tier:** This establishes the foundational distinction between literal decolonization (land return, sovereignty restoration) and metaphorical decolonization (using decolonial language for other social movements). This is crucial because it establishes the Palestinian situation as a genuine decolonial struggle, not merely a nationalist conflict—which affects how international law should apply.
- **What to look for:** (1) Tuck and Yang's core argument: what is the difference between decolonization and other forms of social justice? (2) Why does this distinction matter for understanding the Israel-Palestine conflict? (3) How does decolonial framing change the legal analysis—what obligations do occupying powers have under international law toward indigenous/colonized populations? (4) Tuck and Yang critique the co-optation of "decolonization" language for non-indigenous movements. (5) Applied to scholasticide: If attacks on Palestinian universities can be understood as attacks on institutions of a colonized population (as Tuck and Yang would frame it), does this change the legal analysis? (6) What role does educational destruction play in colonial occupation and decolonial struggle?
- **Connects to:** Lu (2017) and Birnhack (2021) show how decolonial frameworks apply to international justice and legal history respectively.

**Justice and Reconciliation in World Politics — Catherine Lu (2017)**
- **Why read at this tier:** Lu bridges decolonial theory to international justice mechanisms. She examines how international law can address historical colonial injustices, including genocide and war crimes committed in colonial contexts. This is essential for understanding how scholasticide claims fit within the decolonial justice framework.
- **What to look for:** (1) Lu's definition of decolonial justice and how it differs from post-conflict reconciliation. (2) How does Lu analyze colonial violence and what legal categories capture it? (3) What obligations do international justice mechanisms have toward colonized populations? (4) Lu's case studies of colonial conflict and how they were prosecuted (or not). (5) What does Lu identify as barriers to recognizing colonial violence as requiring distinct legal treatment? (6) For scholasticide: If attacks on Palestinian universities are understood as colonial violence (destruction of a colonized population's educational institutions), what would decolonial justice require?
- **Connects to:** Tuck and Yang establish decolonial theory; Lu applies it to international justice. Birnhack shows methodological approaches to post-colonial legal analysis.

**A Post-Colonial Framework for Researching Intellectual Property History — Michael Birnhack (2021)**
- **Why read at this tier:** Birnhack provides methodological framework for examining how colonial-era legal structures persist in contemporary law. This is crucial for the critical claim that British Mandate legal structures may create structural barriers to recognizing scholasticide as a violation.
- **What to look for:** (1) Birnhack's methodology for post-colonial legal analysis—what questions should you ask about a legal system to determine whether colonial structures persist? (2) How does Birnhack examine continuities and ruptures between colonial law and post-colonial law? (3) What is the significance of discovering that colonial-era legal definitions persist? (4) Birnhack's specific analysis of British imperialism and law—what aspects of British legal structure persist? (5) Applied to your question: How could you use Birnhack's methodology to examine whether British Mandate-era definitions of "protected property" or "educational institutions" persist in Israeli law and potentially exclude Palestinian universities from protection?
- **Connects to:** Vitta (1977) documents the specific legal continuities from the Mandate period into Israeli law. Tuck and Yang establish the theoretical framework; Birnhack provides the methodological toolkit.

**Codification of Private International Law in Israel? — Edoardo Vitta (1977)**
- **Why read at this tier:** Vitta documents concrete mechanisms by which British Mandate legal structures persist in contemporary Israeli law. This provides empirical grounding for the post-colonial claim that colonial-era legal structures continue to affect Palestinian territories.
- **What to look for:** (1) Vitta's evidence for legal continuities from the Mandate period. (2) Which aspects of British law were inherited? Which were rejected? (3) What is the significance of the "personality of law" principle that characterizes Israeli law? (4) How does Vitta explain why colonial legal structures persist? (5) Specifically: Does Vitta discuss educational institutions or property definitions? Are there indicators that Mandate-era definitions of protected categories persist? (6) Applied to scholasticide: If Mandate-era legal structures persist, could they create structural barriers to recognizing Palestinian universities as "protected" under contemporary Israeli military law?
- **Connects to:** Birnhack provides the methodological framework; Vitta provides the empirical documentation of what persists.

### **TIER 3 — THE CURRENT FRONTIER (Read Third)**

**The Rome Statute on the International Criminal Court: From 1947-2000 and beyond — Sharon A. Williams (2000)**
- **Why read at this tier:** Williams traces the institutional genealogy of the ICC from post-WWII origins through the Rome Statute adoption. This provides the historical context for understanding where contemporary international criminal law came from and what problems it was designed to solve.
- **What to look for:** (1) Williams's narrative of how the post-WWII commitment to international justice (Nuremberg, Tokyo) was abandoned during the Cold War. (2) What changed in 1990 to make the ICC possible? (3) What were the key political negotiations that shaped the Rome Statute? (4) Which states pushed for particular crime definitions, and why? (5) What crimes were debated and rejected? (6) Williams on the ICC's institutional constraints: complementarity principle, Security Council politics, non-State Party status. (7) For scholasticide: Understanding the ICC's political origins helps you see why certain crimes were prioritized and others marginalized. Does this history explain why educational violence never became a focal point?
- **Connects to:** Glasius (2006) provides critical analysis of ICC political constraints in practice.

**The International Criminal Court — Marlies Glasius (2006)**
- **Why read at this tier:** Glasius provides the most sophisticated political science analysis of how ICC actually functions versus how it is designed to function. This is essential for understanding the gap between legal architecture and enforcement capacity.
- **What to look for:** (1) Glasius's core argument about the ICC's political constraints. (2) How does complementarity principle work in practice? How do states use it to block ICC jurisdiction? (3) Security Council politics—how does P5 veto power affect ICC investigations? (4) Non-State Party status—what does it mean that major powers (US, Israel, Russia, China) are not ICC members? (5) Prosecutorial discretion—how do ICC prosecutors choose which situations to investigate? (6) Resource constraints—how do limited budgets affect investigation priorities? (7) For scholasticide: Even if scholasticide were legally prosecutable under Rome Statute, would it face distinctive enforcement barriers? Would attacks on educational institutions be deprioritized relative to direct civilian massacres? Would Israel's non-State Party status create jurisdictional barriers?
- **Connects to:** Williams's historical analysis explains how these constraints developed; Glasius explains their contemporary operation.

**If I look at the mass I will never act: Psychic numbing and genocide — Paul Slovic (2007)**
- **Why read at this tier:** Slovic provides psychological explanation for why mass violence becomes possible: people caring about individual victims become indifferent to individuals who are "one of many." This is foundational for understanding the mechanism by which scholasticide occurs and for proving intent.
- **What to look for:** (1) Slovic's definition of "psychic numbing"—what is the psychological mechanism? (2) How does scale of violence affect human moral response? (3) Why is a single death more psychologically moving than deaths of many? (4) The implication: if perpetrators of scholasticide rely on psychic numbing—that massacre of hundreds of students will be less noticed than massacre of civilians—does this evidence *intent*? (5) Slovic's discussion of dehumanization—how psychic numbing and dehumanization interact. (6) For scholasticide claims: Could evidence of perpetrator reliance on psychic numbing (statements that "education can be interrupted for security reasons," framing student deaths as incidental) constitute evidence of intent?
- **Connects to:** Kteily and Bruneau (2015) provide empirical measures of dehumanization; Slovic explains the psychological mechanism.

**The ascent of man: Theoretical and empirical evidence for blatant dehumanization — Nour Kteily & Emile Bruneau (2015)**
- **Why read at this tier:** Kteily and Bruneau provide empirical framework for measuring explicit dehumanization—the rhetorical and cognitive process by which groups are portrayed as less than fully human. This is crucial because dehumanization is often evidence of intent in war crimes prosecutions.
- **What to look for:** (1) Kteily and Bruneau's methodology for measuring dehumanization—how do you empirically detect when a group is being dehumanized in public discourse? (2) Their distinction between blatant and subtle dehumanization. (3) Evidence that dehumanization correlates with violence and support for violence. (4) For scholasticide: Has dehumanization of Palestinian university students occurred in public discourse, military communications, or policy documents? If so, would this constitute probative evidence of intent? (5) Kteily and Bruneau on the relationship between dehumanization and specific intent—does dehumanization evidence genocidal purpose?
- **Connects to:** The ICJ Application case (2015) establishes that contextual evidence including public rhetoric is probative of intent. Kteily and Bruneau provide methodology for documenting and measuring this evidence.

**Human Dignity and Judicial Interpretation of Human Rights — Christopher McCrudden (2008)**

## 4. The Conceptual Map — How the Ideas Connect

The conceptual architecture of scholasticide claims rests on the intersection of three legal domains (humanitarian law, human rights law, criminal law) that have developed substantial frameworks but never theorized their convergence on educational destruction.

**The Protection Problem**: International humanitarian law protects "civilian objects"—anything not making "effective contribution to military action" is protected and cannot be attacked. Universities should qualify: they are not military objectives in normal circumstances. Yet this generic "civilian object" protection is designed for and tested against diverse categories (homes, factories, food stores, transportation infrastructure). Educational institutions are protected *incidentally*, as one instance of civilian objects, without specific recognition of what makes them distinctive. The legal rule is clear, but the rule's application obscures the specific harm of educational destruction because the categorical apparatus doesn't distinguish between destroying a home and destroying a university—both are "civilian object" violations.

**The Group Problem**: International criminal law (the Genocide Convention, Rome Statute) protects specific groups: "national, ethnic, racial or religious." University students are not protected as a group *qua students*. They might be protected as a subgroup of a national population (Palestinian students as part of the Palestinian national group). The Srebrenica precedent (Southwick 2005) established that geographically concentrated massacre of a segment of a protected population can constitute genocide. This creates a legal pathway: if Palestinian university students are systematically massacred within a geographically concentrated area (Gaza) over a defined period, this could theoretically constitute genocide. The law exists; it simply has never been applied to this fact pattern. But this pathway requires treating students as relevant only insofar as they are *Palestinians*, not as a distinctive community defined by intellectual practice and educational institutional membership. The legal category (national group) swallows the sociologically relevant feature (educational community).

**The Intent Problem**: Establishing war crimes or genocide requires proving not just that prohibited conduct occurred, but that it was *intended*. The ICJ Application case (2015) methodology allows intent to be inferred from patterns of conduct, scale of destruction, and contextual evidence. For scholasticide claims, the evidentiary question is: Do patterns of educational infrastructure destruction combined with student casualties constitute probative evidence of intent to destroy or seriously harm a protected group? The law allows this inference, but the inference has never been made because scholasticide has never been systematically documented or prosecuted.

**The Dignity Pathway**: The UDHR (1948) establishes education as fundamental to human development and participation in democratic society. Human dignity is foundational to all human rights. McCrudden (2008) documents how dignity evolved from aspirational language to operative legal principle. This creates a theoretical possibility: If education is constitutive of human dignity, and dignity is foundational to human rights, then systematic destruction of educational capacity constitutes a dignity violation warranting legal protection. But this pathway remains undeveloped—no court has systematically worked out how dignity-based reasoning applies to institutional educational capacity rather than individual educational access.

**The Decolonial Lens**: Post-colonial scholarship (Tuck & Yang 2012, Lu 2017) establishes that attacks on educational systems in colonized territories constitute attacks on institutions through which colonized populations reproduce themselves intellectually and culturally. This framing changes the legal analysis: if scholasticide is understood as colonial violence (destruction of a colonized population's educational capacity), this invokes different legal regimes and different obligations. The Mandate history (Vitta 1977) documents how British legal structures persist in Israeli law, potentially creating structural barriers to recognizing Palestinian institutions as "protected." This is the post-colonial claim: the categorical apparatus that determines what is "protected" may have colonial roots.

**The Knowledge Infrastructure Gap**: Bush and Saltarelli (2000) establish that educational systems have distinctive significance in ethnic conflicts because they are sites where group identity is reproduced and group capacity for future regeneration is built. Yet international law has no category for "knowledge infrastructure" the way it recognizes "critical infrastructure" (water, electricity, medical facilities). Universities are protected as civilian objects, but not as nodes in a knowledge network that sustains group capacity. This categorical absence means attacks on multiple universities within a territory—even if coordinated and systematic—are prosecuted as individual civilian object violations rather than as systematic destruction of a knowledge infrastructure.

**The Hidden Assumptions**: Beneath these conceptual structures lies a foundational assumption: that protection operates through enumeration of categories and that once a category is listed (civilian, religious, cultural property), existing legal rules adequately protect it. This assumption works when the category is homogeneous (all civilians have similar protection needs; all religious sites have similar vulnerability). But it breaks when the category is internally complex. Educational institutions are simultaneously: (1) civilian objects (protected under generic rules), (2) cultural property (potentially protected under heritage frameworks), (3) human rights sites (where education as a human right is realized), (4) functional communities (where knowledge and group identity are produced), and (5) critical infrastructure (on which civilian life depends). None of the existing frameworks adequately captures this complexity because each framework was designed for a different purpose.

The deepest unresolved tension is between **categorical protection and harm recognition**: International law can protect things (objects, property) and it can protect categories of people (ethnic groups, gender), but it struggles to protect *institutions that reproduce communities*. Universities don't fit cleanly into either category: they are neither merely civilian objects nor merely instances of a protected group. They are institutions through which a group's intellectual and cultural reproduction occurs. The conceptual architecture was built for different questions and obscures the specific harm of scholasticide by forcing it into ill-fitting categories.

## 5. The Unresolved Core

**The central unresolved question**: Can systematic destruction of universities combined with massacre of university students be classified as a distinct violation of international law requiring specific legal categorization (scholasticide), or must it be prosecuted under existing frameworks (war crimes against civilian objects, crimes against humanity, genocide) where it fits imperfectly?

**Why this question remains open:**

The question is *not empirically underdetermined*. Documentation exists (though not compiled in the seminal legal literature) of attacks on Gaza universities. The attacks are real and systematic. The question is not "Did scholasticide occur?" but "What legal category captures what occurred?"

The question is *legally underdeveloped*. No international court has prosecuted scholasticide. No legal scholarship has systematically theorized whether scholasticide is a sui generis harm requiring new legal categorization or a particular instance of existing crimes. The Rome Statute (2002) does not recognize scholasticide as a distinct crime. The lack of case law and explicit doctrine leaves the legal status indeterminate.

The question contains *conceptual tensions that resist resolution through existing frameworks*. Scholasticide doesn't fit cleanly under civilian object protection (educational destruction isn't primarily about the buildings), doesn't fit cleanly under cultural property (educational institutions aren't primarily heritage sites), doesn't fit cleanly under crimes against humanity (the harm isn't reducible to violence against individual persons), and doesn't fit cleanly under genocide (the target is a functional community, not a group defined by identity). The existence of fit with multiple frameworks *and poor fit with all of them* reveals that existing categories were not designed for this harm.

**What a genuine contribution to this question would require:**

First, **empirical documentation**: Systematic compilation of attacks on Palestinian educational institutions from 1922-2025, with evidence of the scale, timing, targeting, patterns, and contextual justifications. This documentation does not exist in the seminal legal literature. It must be created through historical research, archival analysis, and contemporary documentation.

Second, **evidentiary methodology**: Operationalization of the ICJ Application case (2015) methodology specifically for educational destruction. What patterns of conduct would constitute probative evidence of intent? How many universities must be destroyed over what timeframe to evidence systematic targeting versus incidental collateral damage? How do student casualties relate to intent? What documentary evidence (statements, military plans, policy documents) would prove educational destruction was strategic?

Third, **doctrinal innovation at the intersection**: Rather than squeezing scholasticide into existing crime categories, develop analysis of how humanitarian law (civilian object protection), human rights law (right to education, human dignity), and criminal law (systematic attack doctrine, intent requirements) *converge* on educational destruction. Show that the harm violates multiple legal regimes simultaneously and that this convergence creates stronger basis for liability than any single framework alone.

Fourth, **protected group analysis**: If scholasticide is to be prosecuted as genocide, develop rigorous analysis of whether Palestinian university students constitute a "protected group or subgroup" under the Genocide Convention. Is the relevant group: (a) Palestinians (national group), with students as a subgroup? (b) Palestinian intellectuals/educators (functional community)? (c) A group defined by educational status? This analysis must engage with Srebrenica jurisprudence on subgroups and adapt it to educational contexts.

Fifth, **historical-structural analysis**: Use post-colonial methodology (Birnhack 2021, Tuck & Yang 2012, Lu 2017) to examine whether British Mandate-era legal structures create barriers to recognizing Palestinian universities as protected. Document whether contemporary Israeli military law incorporates Mandate-era definitions of "protected property" or "educational institutions" and whether these definitions exclude Palestinian institutions. This would show whether scholasticide's legal invisibility has structural roots in colonial law.

Sixth, **dehumanization documentation**: Compile evidence of systematic dehumanization of Palestinian students and intellectuals in public discourse, military communications, and policy documents. Using Kteily and Bruneau's (2015) methodology for measuring blatant dehumanization, show whether this dehumanization is probative of genocidal or criminal intent under ICJ standards (2015).

Seventh, **dignity-based legal argument**: Develop the theoretical connection between education as constitutive of human dignity (UDHR 1948, McCrudden 2008) and duty to protect educational institutions. Show how this dignity pathway could support legal prohibition on scholasticide even if traditional categorical protections prove insufficient.

**What distinguishes superficial engagement from deep engagement:**

A superficial engagement would: catalog attacks on Gaza universities, count student casualties, and claim these constitute war crimes under existing law. This merely applies existing categories without examining why the categories inadequately capture the harm.

A deep engagement would: (1) Show that scholasticide violates international law through multiple, converging legal regimes (humanitarian law, human rights law, criminal law) that have developed separately without recognizing their common focus on educational destruction; (2) Demonstrate that existing crime categories (war crimes, crimes against humanity, genocide) were not designed for this harm and thus systematically undervalue it; (3) Develop evidentiary methodology specifically for educational destruction adapted from established jurisprudence (ICJ Application case); (4) Investigate whether legal categories themselves have colonial roots that exclude Palestinian institutions; (5) Make the normative argument that scholasticide requires sui generis legal recognition because no existing category adequately captures the harm of systematically destroying a colonized population's capacity for intellectual and cultural reproduction.
