# SCHOLASTICIDE AND INTERNATIONAL CRIMINAL LAW: RESEARCH BRIEF

## Problem Statement

The research examines whether documented attacks on Gaza's universities and massacre of Palestinian students (1922–2025) constitute "scholasticide"—a putative war crime under international law. The problem inverts through pipeline analysis: it begins asking what evidence supports this classification, but reveals the prior question is whether systematic documentation of such attacks exists. The frameworks for prosecution are established; the empirical foundation is absent.

## Current State

**Known:** International humanitarian law protects civilian objects including educational institutions (Geneva Conventions 1949, Rome Statute Articles 8(2)(b)(ix) and 8(2)(e)(iv)). University students are civilians protected unless directly participating in hostilities. The ICJ Application case (2015) established methodology for inferring genocidal intent from conduct patterns, scale of destruction, and contextual evidence. Srebrenica jurisprudence permits prosecution of geographically concentrated massacres as genocide. British Mandate legal structures persist in contemporary Israeli law (Birnhack 2021).

**Contested:** Gaza's legal classification (occupation/international armed conflict/non-international armed conflict) determines applicable protection regimes. Whether students constitute a protected group under genocide frameworks, or merely a subgroup of Palestinian national population. Whether "scholasticide" requires legal categorization distinct from existing civilian object protection frameworks. Whether attacks on educational institutions evidence genocidal intent versus war crimes under proportionality doctrine.

**Unknown:** Whether systematic documentation exists tracing attacks on Palestinian educational institutions from 1922–2025. Whether archived forensic evidence comparable to Srebrenica exists for student casualties. What evidentiary standards should govern NGO documentation of university destruction for ICC admissibility. Whether British Mandate legal definitions structurally exclude Palestinian universities from protection categories.

## Key Gaps

**1. Categorical void (GAP-61D696FE):** International criminal law lacks doctrinal framework for "scholasticide" as distinct war crime. Rome Statute protects buildings dedicated to education as civilian objects, but systematic destruction of universities combined with student massacre fits imperfectly within civilian protection, cultural property, and crimes against humanity frameworks. Scholasticide occupies unmapped intersection of three doctrines; no sui generis analysis exists.

**2. Methodological underdevelopment (GAP-137EF331):** ICJ methodology for inferring intent from conduct patterns is established jurisprudence but has not been applied to analyze educational infrastructure destruction. Framework exists; application protocols do not. No validated dataset documents Gaza university attacks with sufficient precision for evidentiary analysis.

**3. Protected group ambiguity (GAP-28CA809C):** Unresolved whether Palestinian university students constitute protected group (requiring Genocide Convention expansion) or subgroup of national population (fitting existing doctrine). This distinction determines legal pathway—the former requires norm evolution, the latter merely requires application of established law to this context.

**4. Colonial legal structures (GAP-FF933EF4):** Post-colonial frameworks establish that British Mandate legal architecture persists in Israeli law, but these have not been applied to examine whether Mandate-era definitions create structural barriers to accountability for educational violence. Theoretical tools exist; archival research does not.

**5. Empirical foundation gap (GAP-4B0AC21C):** The problem specifies 1922–2025 timeframe, but zero systematic documentation exists in accessible scholarly literature connecting British Mandate-era violence to contemporary Gaza university attacks. Claims of century-long "scholasticide" policy lack longitudinal evidentiary base.

## Viable Approaches

**Adapt ICJ Application framework to scholasticide (PROP-14729D57):** Apply established intent-inference methodology to compile documentation of Gaza university attacks, analyze for systematicity (concentration, targeting patterns, comprehensiveness of destruction), and examine contextual evidence (military communications, policy statements, public rhetoric). Framework is jurisprudentially sound. Critical constraint: no validated dataset exists documenting attacks with sufficient precision.

**Operationalize dehumanization documentation (PROP-B33885EC):** Use validated psychological frameworks (Kteily & Bruneau dehumanization scale, Slovic psychic numbing) to systematically code public statements and media discourse targeting Palestinian students. Establish dehumanization as contextual evidence of intent, following ICTR/ICTY precedent. Framework has evidentiary precedent. Constraint: no sources document student-specific dehumanization; line between political rhetoric and criminal intent evidence remains contested.

**Construct dignity-based doctrinal pathway (PROP-0A09507C):** Ground scholasticide analysis in human dignity as foundational to right to education (UDHR Article 26). Argue systematic destruction constitutes dignity violation warranting war crimes classification. Framework is doctrinally legitimate. Constraint: dignity functions as interpretive tool for existing prohibitions rather than independent basis for prosecution; precedent limited.

## Recommended Trajectory

**Problem reframing required.** The original question assumes an evidence base that does not exist in accessible literature. Three paths forward:

**Path 1 (Archival):** Conduct primary research in British Mandate archives, Israeli military/government records, Palestinian Authority documentation, UN files, and NGO field reports to establish whether systematic documentation exists across full timeframe. Multi-year empirical project, not legal analysis.

**Path 2 (Temporal narrowing):** Focus on specific, well-documented conflicts (2008–09 Cast Lead, 2014 Protective Edge, 2023–24 operations) where forensic evidence and casualty documentation exist. Abandon century-long framing but enable rigorous analysis using established ICJ/ICC methodologies.

**Path 3 (Framework development):** Accept empirical void and develop theoretical-legal framework for analyzing scholasticide claims IF evidence emerges. Viable: three partially feasible proposals offer sound legal-theoretical foundations. Appropriate if intent is building prosecutorial toolkit rather than documenting historical atrocities.

**Recommended path:** Temporal narrowing (Path 2) with framework development components (Path 3). This enables rigorous legal analysis of documented incidents using existing methodologies while developing doctrine for future application.

## Key Uncertainties

**Classification deadlock:** Gaza's legal status remains politically contested with no resolution mechanism. This classification determines applicable protection regime—identical conduct could be lawful, violation, or grave breach depending on classification. Legal analysis cannot proceed until this political question resolves.

**Enforcement discretion gap:** ICC complementarity principle combined with Israel's non-State Party status creates double-bind: Israel can block ICC jurisdiction by claiming domestic investigation while remaining free from Rome Statute obligations. Structural feature enabling impunity.

**Colonial legal persistence:** Post-colonial analysis is methodologically sound but requires primary archival sources on Mandate-era legal definitions. Unknown whether inherited frameworks create structural barriers to accountability requiring challenge to colonial legal foundations themselves.

**Evidentiary standards absence:** NGOs have historical legitimacy in international governance (Charnovitz 1997), but scholasticide-specific evidentiary standards for ICC admissibility do not exist. Most accessible evidence in active conflict zones faces highest admissibility barriers.

**Dignitary framework precedent:** Dignity-based arguments have limited success in war crimes prosecution. ICC cases rely on specific Rome Statute provisions rather than abstract dignity violations. Whether dignity could establish independent criminal liability remains judicially untested.
