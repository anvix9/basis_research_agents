## Phases

### Pre-psychological Identity: Metaphysical Foundations (-335 to 1690)
Identity conceived as logical-metaphysical problem (Aristotle's essence, substance ontology). Narrative recognized as fundamental to meaning-making but not yet connected to selfhood. Identity as abstract philosophical category, not psychological phenomenon.
*Transition driver: Locke's revolutionary move to ground identity in psychological continuity rather than substance ontology*

### Psychological Turn: Consciousness and Memory (1690-1850)
Identity relocated from metaphysics to psychology. Memory and consciousness established as criteria for personal identity. Self becomes psychological rather than metaphysical entity. Mid-19th century vocabulary reflects transition from philosophical to proto-psychological concepts of mind.
*Transition driver: Rise of experimental psychology and scientific method demanded measurable constructs*

### Psychometric Revolution: Intelligence as Measurable Construct (1904-1950)
Intelligence established as general, measurable factor (Spearman's g). Psychometrics founded as quantitative science of mental abilities. Identity and intelligence treated as separate domains—intelligence as cognitive capacity, identity not yet theorized. Individual differences paradigm dominates.
*Transition driver: Post-war developmental psychology emphasized identity formation as distinct psychological process*

### Identity Development Theory: Psychosocial Framework (1950-1979)
Identity established as developmental achievement through stages (Erikson). Identity becomes legitimate psychological construct distinct from personality. Focus on individual identity formation, crisis, and resolution. Intelligence and identity remain theoretically disconnected—parallel tracks in psychology.
*Transition driver: Social psychology's cognitive revolution and discovery that group membership shapes cognition*

### Social Identity Revolution: Cognitive Consequences of Categorization (1979-1990)
Social identity theory (Tajfel/Turner) and self-categorization theory (Turner) established that social identities have direct cognitive effects. First systematic evidence that identity categories influence perception, memory, judgment. Identity shown to shape what and how we think, not just who we are. Still focused on social identity, not personal identity.
*Transition driver: Narrative turn in psychology; critical theory's epistemological claims about identity and knowledge*

### Narrative and Standpoint Epistemology: Identity as Epistemic Position (1985-1996)
Two parallel developments: (1) Narrative identity (McAdams) established personal identity as storied self-construction; (2) Standpoint epistemology (Collins, Freire) argued identity categories constitute epistemic positions—what you know depends on who you are. Critical consciousness linked identity to cognitive development. Philosophy of mind renaissance (Searle, Dennett, Baars, Kim, Chalmers) produced sophisticated theories of consciousness, intentionality, mental causation—but remained disconnected from identity theory.
*Transition driver: Hierarchical intelligence models integrated multiple cognitive abilities; personal identity debates reached maturity; but no synthesis emerged*

### Theoretical Fragmentation: Parallel Tracks Without Integration (1996-present)
Multiple mature theoretical traditions exist in parallel: (1) Psychometric intelligence theory (Gf-Gc hierarchical models); (2) Philosophy of mind (consciousness, intentionality, mental causation); (3) Personal identity theory (psychological continuity debates); (4) Social identity theory (cognitive effects of categorization); (5) Narrative identity; (6) Standpoint epistemology. Each tradition has sophisticated internal development but minimal cross-talk. The question 'how does identity influence intelligence?' remains unasked because disciplines don't share vocabulary or methods.
*Transition driver: No clear transition yet—field awaits synthetic framework*

## Dead Ends

- **Pure psychometrics connecting identity to intelligence** (1904-present)
  Reason: Psychometrics treated intelligence as individual difference variable independent of identity; measured cognitive abilities without theorizing how identity might constitute or modulate them; focused on measurement rather than conceptual integration
  Lesson: Measurement sophistication does not automatically generate theoretical synthesis across domains; identity remained outside psychometric framework
- **Mind-brain identity theory as framework for personal identity** (1956-1980)
  Reason: Mind-brain identity theory addressed metaphysical relation between mental states and brain states (type/token identity) but had no resources to address personal identity over time or social identity; wrong kind of identity for the problem
  Lesson: Identity as metaphysical relation (mind=brain) is distinct from identity as persistence condition (person over time) and from identity as social category; using same word 'identity' masked conceptual non-connection
- **Eriksonian identity development connecting to intelligence** (1950-1980)
  Reason: Identity development theory focused on achieving stable sense of self through psychosocial stages but never theorized how identity achievement affects cognitive capacity or intelligence; identity and intelligence treated as orthogonal developmental lines
  Lesson: Developmental frameworks can mature without addressing cross-domain interactions; identity formation and cognitive development studied separately despite co-occurrence
- **Consciousness studies connecting to personal identity** (1988-2000)
  Reason: Consciousness theories (global workspace, hard problem) addressed phenomenal experience and functional architecture but did not connect to personal identity persistence or social identity effects; consciousness treated as synchronic phenomenon independent of diachronic identity
  Lesson: Consciousness and identity remained separate explananda despite both being core features of minded beings; synchronic vs. diachronic split prevented integration

## Recurring Patterns

- **Identity as relation vs. identity as category persistently conflated**
  Appearances: 1690-present, especially 1956-1980 with mind-brain identity
  Why: English word 'identity' covers: (1) persistence condition (identity over time), (2) metaphysical relation (A=B), (3) social category (racial/gender identity), (4) sense of self (identity achievement). These are different concepts that keep getting confused because of linguistic overlap.
- **Intelligence and identity studied in parallel without integration**
  Appearances: 1904-present, especially acute 1950-1998
  Why: Disciplinary structure: psychometrics studied intelligence, developmental/social psychology studied identity, philosophy studied personal identity. No institutional incentive or shared methodology for integration. Each tradition matured independently.
- **Social identity effects on cognition demonstrated but not connected to intelligence constructs**
  Appearances: 1979-present
  Why: Social identity theory showed categorization affects perception/judgment but used social psychology paradigms (stereotyping, intergroup bias). Intelligence psychology used psychometric paradigms (IQ tests, factor analysis). Different methods, different journals, different communities—no bridge.
- **Epistemological claims about identity and knowledge not tested empirically**
  Appearances: 1973-present with Freire/Collins
  Why: Critical theory and standpoint epistemology made theoretical claims about identity determining epistemic capacity but resided in humanities/social theory rather than empirical psychology. Claims remained at theoretical level without operationalization or measurement.
- **Philosophy of mind and psychology of identity non-communicating**
  Appearances: 1956-present
  Why: Philosophy focuses on conceptual analysis, thought experiments, and metaphysics. Psychology focuses on empirical measurement and mechanisms. Identity appears in both but with different methods and questions—philosophers ask 'what is identity?' while psychologists ask 'how does identity develop/function?'

## Methods Evolution

Methods evolved within separate traditions without cross-fertilization. (1) Psychometrics: from Spearman's g (1904) through factor analysis to hierarchical models (Gf-Gc by 1998)—increasingly sophisticated statistical methods but always treating intelligence as individual trait. (2) Social identity: from minimal group paradigm (Tajfel 1979) to self-categorization measures (Turner 1985)—experimental methods showing identity affects cognition but focused on intergroup processes. (3) Narrative identity: from life story interviews (McAdams 1985) to coding systems for narrative complexity—qualitative/interpretive methods incompatible with psychometric tradition. (4) Philosophy of mind: from thought experiments (Place 1956) to conceptual analysis (Kim 1996, Chalmers 1996)—armchair philosophy separate from empirical work. (5) Standpoint epistemology: from theoretical argument (Collins 1990, Freire 1973) to intersectional analysis—remained in critical theory without empirical operationalization. NO methodological tradition emerged capable of integrating across these domains. The problem requires simultaneously: measuring intelligence (psychometrics), manipulating/measuring identity (social psychology), theorizing persistence and selfhood (philosophy), and addressing epistemic standpoints (critical theory). No single method or discipline has tools for all of this.
