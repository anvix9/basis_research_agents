## Original Definitions

- **genocide** (United Nations Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide, 1948): Acts committed with intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnical, racial or religious group, as such: (a) Killing members of the group; (b) Causing serious bodily or mental harm to members of the group; (c) Deliberately inflicting on the group conditions of life calculated to bring about its physical destruction in whole or in part; (d) Imposing measures intended to prevent births within the group; (e) Forcibly transferring children of the group to another group.
- **crimes against humanity** (Charter of the International Military Tribunal at Nuremberg, 1945): Murder, extermination, enslavement, deportation, and other inhumane acts committed against any civilian population, before or during the war, or persecutions on political, racial or religious grounds in execution of or in connection with any crime within the jurisdiction of the Tribunal, whether or not in violation of the domestic law of the country where perpetrated.
- **war crimes** (Charter of the International Military Tribunal at Nuremberg, 1945): Violations of the laws or customs of war, including but not limited to murder, ill-treatment or deportation to slave labor of civilian population of or in occupied territory; murder or ill-treatment of prisoners of war or persons on the seas; killing of hostages; plunder of public or private property; wanton destruction of cities, towns or villages; or devastation not justified by military necessity.
- **grave breaches** (Geneva Conventions, 1949): Serious violations of Geneva Conventions including willful killing, torture or inhuman treatment, willfully causing great suffering or serious injury to body or health, extensive destruction and appropriation of property not justified by military necessity and carried out unlawfully and wantonly, compelling a prisoner of war or protected person to serve in the forces of a hostile power, and willfully depriving a protected person of rights of fair and regular trial.
- **protected persons** (Geneva Conventions, 1949): Persons protected in time of war, including wounded and sick members of armed forces, prisoners of war, and civilians in occupied territories and enemy territory, who are entitled to respect for their persons, honor, family rights, religious convictions and practices, and manners and customs.
- **scholasticide** (Avi Shlaim and concurrent scholars (Domínguez, Hajir et al., Giroux), 2024): The deliberate destruction of an educational system and its institutions, including systematic targeting of universities, schools, libraries, archives, scholars, students, and educational infrastructure as part of a broader project of cultural annihilation or settler-colonial erasure.
- **unchilding** (Nadera Shalhoub-Kevorkian, 2019): The systematic denial of childhood to Palestinian children through incarceration, surveillance, and state violence, involving destruction of developmental and educational spaces as a technology of settler-colonial control that prevents normal maturation and education.
- **distinction** (Additional Protocol I to the Geneva Conventions, 1977): The fundamental principle of international humanitarian law requiring parties to armed conflict to distinguish at all times between civilian population and combatants, and between civilian objects and military objectives, directing operations only against military objectives.
- **proportionality** (Additional Protocol I to the Geneva Conventions, 1977): The principle prohibiting attacks which may be expected to cause incidental loss of civilian life, injury to civilians, damage to civilian objects, or a combination thereof, which would be excessive in relation to the concrete and direct military advantage anticipated.
- **mandate system** (League of Nations Covenant, 1919): League of Nations system establishing international supervision over territories detached from defeated powers after World War I, wherein 'advanced nations' would administer territories deemed not yet able to govern themselves, with the mandate held as a 'sacred trust of civilization.'
