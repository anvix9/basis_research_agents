## Phases

### Pre-Computational Foundations (-400 BCE - 1945)
Philosophical establishment of fundamental categories: intelligence as spectrum vs. binary, consciousness as phenomenon, meaning-seeking as human essence, embodiment as condition for genuine experience. Non-Western and Western traditions diverge on intelligence ontology (attunement vs. problem-solving). Experimental psychology reduces intelligence to measurable, mechanistic processes.
*Transition driver: World War II computational breakthroughs and need for automated calculation created technical possibility of mechanical intelligence*

### Functionalist Inauguration (1946-1982)
Turing shifts debate from metaphysical essence to behavioral/functional criteria. Intelligence reconceived as information processing detachable from substrate. Psychometrics establishes measurement frameworks (g-factor) that AI must meet or challenge. Philosophy of mind dominated by functionalism enabling AI legitimacy.
*Transition driver: Symbolic AI limitations and cognitive science embodiment turn revealed gap between functional definitions and actual cognitive architecture*

### Consciousness Wars (1983-1999)
Searle vs. Dennett crystallizes irresolvable split: intrinsic vs. derived intentionality, phenomenal consciousness vs. functional stance. Gardner's multiple intelligences fragments unitary model, raising question of which intelligences AI can embody. Damasio establishes embodiment necessity for consciousness. Heidegger's situated existence critique gains traction against disembodied AI.
*Transition driver: Computational power increases and neural network revival shifted focus from symbolic reasoning to pattern recognition architectures*

### Neural Revolution (1997-2012)
LSTM solves fundamental limitation, enabling modern deep learning. Russell & Norvig's rational agent paradigm dominates AI research, sidelining consciousness questions. Hierarchical Bayesian models establish computational bridge between biological and artificial intelligence. Focus shifts from replicating mind to engineering capability.
*Transition driver: Big data availability and GPU acceleration enabled scaling of neural architectures to breakthrough performance levels*

### Predictive Processing Convergence (2013-2020)
Clark's predictive processing reveals structural similarity between biological and artificial pattern recognition while highlighting embodiment difference. Godfrey-Smith's comparative cognition challenges anthropocentric evaluation criteria. Critical consciousness tradition (Freire) reframes consciousness as social praxis, not computational property. Growing recognition that AI and biological intelligence may be incommensurable categories.
*Transition driver: Transformer architecture and large language models created AI systems whose behavior exceeded expectations of existing theoretical frameworks*

### Generative AI Reckoning (2020-present)
LLMs produce human-like output without clear theoretical account of what they are doing. Functionalist criteria (Turing-style) seemingly met but consciousness debates unresolved. Tension between engineering success and explanatory failure. Question shifts from 'can machines think?' to 'what is AI's proper role given uncertainty about its nature?'
*Transition driver: Ongoing - rapid capability scaling without theoretical convergence*

## Dead Ends

- **Symbolic/Logic-based AI (GOFAI)** (1956-1985)
  Reason: Brittleness, combinatorial explosion, inability to handle uncertainty and common sense reasoning
  Lesson: Intelligence requires statistical/probabilistic reasoning not just formal logic; embodied knowledge cannot be fully symbolized
- **Expert Systems for General Intelligence** (1970s-1990s)
  Reason: Knowledge engineering bottleneck - couldn't manually encode all common sense knowledge
  Lesson: Intelligence emerges from learning, not knowledge codification; tacit knowledge resists explicit formalization
- **Perceptrons (First Wave Neural Networks)** (1958-1969)
  Reason: Minsky & Papert proved limitations (XOR problem), computational constraints
  Lesson: Single-layer networks fundamentally limited; required multi-layer architectures and backpropagation (later solved)
- **Behaviorist Intelligence Models** (1920s-1960s)
  Reason: Couldn't account for language acquisition, internal representations, creative problem-solving
  Lesson: Intelligence requires internal representations and cognitive architecture, not just stimulus-response
- **Strong AI Consciousness Claims** (1960s-present (recurring))
  Reason: Philosophical objections (hard problem, Chinese Room) remain unanswered; no consensus criteria
  Lesson: Functional success ≠ phenomenal consciousness; may be category error to attribute consciousness based on behavior
- **Unitary Intelligence Measurement for AI** (1950s-present (recurring))
  Reason: Multiple intelligences reveal incommensurability; AI excels in narrow domains, fails in others
  Lesson: Intelligence is multi-dimensional; human-AI comparison requires domain-specific evaluation

## Recurring Patterns

- **Functionalism vs. Phenomenology Divide**
  Appearances: 1950s (Turing), 1980s (Searle vs. Dennett), 2020s (LLM consciousness debates)
  Why: Fundamental disagreement whether behavioral/functional criteria suffice or whether phenomenal experience is irreducible
- **Anthropocentric Evaluation Criteria**
  Appearances: 1904 (Spearman g-factor), 1983 (Gardner's multiple intelligences), 2016 (Godfrey-Smith's alien minds)
  Why: Human cognition as reference point despite evolutionary contingency; difficulty establishing substrate-neutral criteria
- **Embodiment vs. Abstraction**
  Appearances: 1927 (Heidegger), 2000 (Damasio), 2013 (Clark predictive processing)
  Why: Tension between computational abstraction and biological grounding; question whether intelligence requires physical situatedness
- **General vs. Narrow Intelligence**
  Appearances: 1904 (Spearman g-factor), 1983 (Gardner multiple intelligences), 1995 (Russell & Norvig rational agents), 2020s (AGI debates)
  Why: Persistent question whether intelligence is unitary capacity or collection of domain-specific competencies
- **Mechanism vs. Meaning**
  Appearances: 1898 (Thorndike mechanism), 1946 (Frankl meaning), 1973 (Freire praxis), 2020s (AI alignment debates)
  Why: Tension between reductive mechanistic explanations and irreducible human meaning-making
- **Eastern vs. Western Intelligence Ontologies**
  Appearances: -400 (Tao Te Ching), Modern mindfulness-AI debates
  Why: Attunement/harmony models incompatible with instrumental problem-solving paradigm; cultural contingency of AI conceptualization

## Methods Evolution

Pre-1950: Philosophical speculation and psychometric measurement (Spearman, Thorndike). 1950-1985: Symbolic logic and rule-based systems (GOFAI) dominant. 1985-1995: Expert systems and knowledge engineering attempted. 1986-2010: Neural networks revived via backpropagation, but computationally limited. 1997-2012: LSTM and architectural innovations enabled deep learning. 2010-present: Big data, GPU acceleration, transformer architectures. Methodological shift from deductive logic → inductive learning from data. Philosophical methods remained stable (thought experiments, conceptual analysis) but empirical neuroscience (Damasio) and comparative cognition (Godfrey-Smith) added biological constraints. Cross-disciplinary integration increased (computational neuroscience, predictive processing).
