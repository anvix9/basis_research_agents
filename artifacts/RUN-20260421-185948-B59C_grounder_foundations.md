## Fundamental Whys

The original motivation behind Greek philosophical inquiry into intelligence arose from profound cultural transformation during the Archaic-Classical transition (circa 600-400 BCE). The breakdown of traditional mythopoeic worldview—where divine caprice explained natural phenomena—created urgent need for new principles of intelligibility. The pre-Socratic physiologoi sought to explain kosmos (ordered universe) through physis (nature's own principles) rather than anthropomorphic deities. This intellectual revolution asked: What makes the world intelligible? What faculty in humans grasps this intelligibility? The inquiry into nous/psyche/logos emerged from need to explain how human mind could access rational structure of cosmos.

The competitive intellectual environment of Greek polis-culture, particularly democratic Athens, intensified focus on human distinctiveness and excellence (arete). What separates humans from animals? The answer—logos, rational speech and thought—became defining characteristic and normative standard. Intelligence was never purely descriptive but carried evaluative weight: to be human meant to actualize rational capacity. This explains why Greek philosophy developed hierarchical models positioning human intelligence between animal sensation below and divine pure thought above. The fundamental why was simultaneously ontological (understanding humanity's place in natural order) and ethical (defining proper human function and excellence).

Plato's elevation of theoretical contemplation over practical affairs reflected specific socio-political context: Athenian democracy's perceived failures (including executing Socrates) and aristocratic disdain for manual labor and commerce. Theoretical wisdom became refuge from corrupting politics and contingent affairs—eternal Forms offered stability against political chaos. The fundamental motivation was finding source of permanent truth and value transcending unstable human conventions. This explains Platonic subordination of body to soul, practical to theoretical, temporal to eternal.

Aristotle's modification—grounding intelligence more firmly in natural world while maintaining human distinctiveness—reflected different concerns. His biological investigations revealed graduated complexity in nature, yet he preserved anthropocentric hierarchy by making theoretical contemplation (theoria) highest human activity, approximating divine self-thinking thought. The fundamental why was reconciling empirical observation of natural continuum with normative commitment to human supremacy and philosophical life as ultimate fulfillment.

Medieval theological transformation arose from new fundamental why: reconciling Greek philosophical heritage with revealed monotheistic religions claiming God created world and revealed truth. How does human intelligence relate to divine omniscience? How does natural reason relate to faith? Islamic, Jewish, and Christian thinkers inherited Aristotelian and Neoplatonic frameworks but reoriented them toward theological ends. The agent intellect debates—whether divine, cosmic, or individual—addressed how human thinking participates in or receives from divine source. The fundamental motivation was preserving both human intellectual dignity (humans can know truth) and divine transcendence (all truth derives from God). This created enduring tensions between natural philosophy and theology, reason and revelation, that shaped medieval and early modern thought.

## Intellectual Genealogy

The intellectual genealogy of intelligence categories begins in archaic Greece with Homer's epic poetry (circa 750 BCE), where intelligence appears not as abstract faculty but as practical attributes: metis (cunning), menos (spirited force), and noos (perceiving awareness). The Homeric warrior-hero embodied intelligence as situational resourcefulness—Odysseus as polytropos, the 'man of many turns,' exemplifying adaptive cunning rather than contemplative wisdom. This pre-philosophical understanding treated intelligence as embedded in concrete action and social context.

The pre-Socratic physiologoi (circa 600-400 BCE) initiated the transition from mythos to logos by seeking natural rather than divine explanations. Heraclitus introduced logos as cosmic rational principle governing change while characterizing physis as 'loving to hide' (kryptesthai philei), establishing foundational tension between intelligibility and obscurity in nature. Anaxagoras elevated nous to cosmic status as unmixed principle ordering primordial mixture, separating intelligence from materiality. Contrarily, Empedocles grounded cognition in material sympathy ('like knows like'), while Democritus' atomism reduced all mentality to configurations of material atoms. These divergent accounts established enduring tension between transcendent and embodied models of intelligence.

Plato (428-348 BCE) revolutionized intelligence discourse by establishing theoretical contemplation of eternal Forms as supreme cognitive activity, subordinating practical affairs and bodily existence. The Republic's divided line hierarchically ordered cognition from lowest imagination through belief and mathematical reasoning to highest noesis (pure intellection of Forms). The Timaeus presented cosmos itself as intelligent organism ordered by Demiurge according to eternal patterns, establishing three-tiered hierarchy: divine intelligence (Demiurge), cosmic intelligence (World-Soul), and human intelligence (rational soul). This Platonic framework made theoretical wisdom supreme and positioned human intelligence between eternal divine realm and corrupting material world.

Aristotle (384-322 BCE) built upon but also challenged his teacher Plato, grounding intelligence more firmly in natural world while maintaining hierarchical structure. De Anima's tripartite soul—nutritive (shared with plants), sensitive (shared with animals), and rational (unique to humans)—embedded intelligence within biological continuum while preserving human distinctiveness through logos. The crucial Book III Chapter 5 distinction between agent intellect (nous poietikos) and potential intellect (nous pathetikos) created interpretive crux that dominated subsequent tradition. Nicomachean Ethics introduced definitive distinction between theoretical wisdom (sophia) directed toward eternal necessary truths and practical wisdom (phronesis) addressing contingent human affairs, establishing that intelligence operates differently across theoretical and practical domains. Aristotle's hylomorphism (form-matter) made human intelligence essentially embodied—requiring phantasms from sense experience—while Metaphysics Lambda's unmoved mover as pure self-thinking thought (noesis noeseos) established divine intelligence as absolute standard.

Alexander of Aphrodisias (circa 200 CE) interpreted Aristotle's agent intellect as divine and transcendent rather than individual human faculty, creating problem of how individual souls access universal intelligence. This interpretation profoundly influenced medieval developments. Plotinus' Enneads (circa 270 CE) synthesized Platonic and Aristotelian elements into Neoplatonic emanationist hierarchy: the ineffable One beyond being, Nous (Divine Intellect containing all Forms), World-Soul, and descending material realm. Human intelligence achieves perfection through ascent back to source via intellectual purification, transcending discursive reasoning for direct intuition.

Augustine (354-430 CE) Christianized Neoplatonism, establishing divine illumination theory: human intelligence grasps eternal truths not through abstraction from senses but through inner divine light. This made human knowing dependent on God while preserving soul's created spiritual nature. Islamic philosophy inherited and transformed this heritage: Avicenna (Ibn Sina, 980-1037) established Agent Intellect as tenth separate intelligence emanating from First Cause, providing intelligible forms to individual human souls. This Neoplatonic emanationism made human thinking participation in transcendent cosmic hierarchy. Averroes (Ibn Rushd, 1126-1198) argued for monopsychism—single Agent Intellect shared by all humans—making individual human intelligence merely temporary conjunction with eternal separate intellect.

Maimonides (1138-1204) synthesized Aristotelian philosophy with Jewish theology, establishing Active Intellect as intermediary through which prophecy and intellectual perfection occur. Thomas Aquinas (1225-1274) definitively established Christian Aristotelian synthesis: contra Averroes, he argued agent and possible intellect are distinct powers within each individual human soul. Contra pure Platonism, he grounded human intelligence in embodied soul requiring sense experience. Yet contra pure naturalism, human intellect participates in divine light. This synthesis preserved individual human intelligence while maintaining its subordination to divine source, establishing framework that dominated Western thought into modernity.

## Original Definitions

- **nous (νοῦς)** (Anaxagoras first established nous as cosmic ordering principle (fragments circa 450 BCE); Aristotle systematized psychological nous in De Anima, -450): Mind or intellect as faculty grasping first principles, Forms, or intelligible reality; in cosmology, unmixed principle ordering all things; in psychology, highest rational capacity distinguishing humans from animals
- **psyche (ψυχή)** (Pre-Socratic usage; systematically analyzed by Plato (Phaedo, Republic) and definitively categorized by Aristotle (De Anima), -400): Soul or animating principle of living beings, encompassing nutritive (plant), sensitive (animal), and rational (human) capacities; principle of life and self-motion
- **logos (λόγος)** (Heraclitus established logos as cosmic principle (fragments circa 500 BCE); Stoics developed as immanent divine reason, -500): Cosmic rational principle governing change and providing intelligibility; human rational discourse and capacity for speech; proportional relationship or mathematical ratio; definition or account giving essence of thing
- **physis (φύσις)** (Pre-Socratic physiologoi; definitively analyzed by Aristotle in Physics Book II as immanent principle of motion and rest, -550): Nature as self-generating, self-disclosing creative force; intrinsic principle of growth and change in natural beings; essence or constitution of a thing; totality of natural world as opposed to convention (nomos)
- **sophia (σοφία)** (Originally attributed to Seven Sages as practical-political expertise; redefined by Plato as theoretical; systematized by Aristotle in Metaphysics and Nicomachean Ethics Book VI, -350): Theoretical wisdom as knowledge of first causes and principles, highest science concerning what is universal, eternal, and divine; intellectual virtue grasping necessary truths
- **phronesis (φρόνησις)** (Aristotle in Nicomachean Ethics Book VI distinguished from sophia, establishing as irreducibly practical intelligence, -350): Practical wisdom as intellectual virtue concerning contingent human affairs, involving both universal principles and particular perception; capacity for deliberation about good action
- **nous poietikos / nous pathetikos** (Aristotle, De Anima Book III Chapter 5 (430a10-19), -350): Agent/active intellect that makes all things intelligible versus potential/passive intellect that becomes all things; distinction within human rational soul or between divine and human intelligence (interpretations vary)
- **intellectus agens / intellectus possibilis** (Medieval Latin translations of Aristotle; extensively debated by Alexander of Aphrodisias, Avicenna, Averroes, Aquinas, 200): Latin translations of Aristotle's active/potential intellect; agent intellect as actualizing principle making forms intelligible; possible intellect as receptive capacity
- **divine illumination** (Augustine (Confessions, De Magistro circa 400 CE); developed from Plotinian Neoplatonism, 400): Theory that human intelligence grasps eternal truths through direct divine light or participation in God's knowledge rather than through abstraction from sense experience
- **noesis noeseos (νόησις νοήσεως)** (Aristotle, Metaphysics Lambda (Book XII), -350): Thought thinking itself; pure self-reflexive intellectual activity characterizing divine unmoved mover as eternal actuality whose essence is thinking and whose object is itself

## Assumptions Flagged

- **Intelligence is unitary phenomenon across historical periods**: Greek nous, medieval intellectus, and modern 'intelligence' may be incommensurable concepts rather than continuous category. Each embedded in different metaphysical frameworks—Greek hylomorphism, medieval divine illumination, modern naturalism—making direct comparison problematic.
- **Hierarchical ordering of intelligence types is neutral description**: Greek and medieval hierarchies (plant-animal-human-divine) carried normative and theological commitments, not just empirical observation. Modern rejection or retention of hierarchy similarly involves evaluative choices about human distinctiveness, not purely descriptive claims.
- **Theoretical and practical intelligence are natural kinds**: Aristotelian distinction between sophia and phronesis may reflect Greek aristocratic values (elevating leisured contemplation over productive labor) rather than discovering natural division. Cross-cultural examination shows different societies categorize mental capacities differently.
- **Embodied versus transcendent intelligence are exclusive alternatives**: Historical sources show attempts to synthesize (Aristotle's hylomorphism, Aquinas' embodied but immortal soul) rather than forcing dichotomy. Modern phenomenology and enactivism similarly reject mind-body dualism while preserving irreducibility of mental to physical.
- **Greek philosophy provides universal starting point**: Privileging Greek origins as 'foundational' reflects Eurocentric bias. Chinese (Confucian-Daoist), Indian (Vedantic-Buddhist), and Indigenous traditions developed distinct intelligence concepts simultaneously. Problem's framing 'from old greek philosophy until now' assumes single genealogy rather than multiple intellectual traditions.
- **Empirical science resolves philosophical disputes about intelligence**: Contemporary neuroscience and psychology presuppose conceptual frameworks inheriting unresolved philosophical problems (hard problem of consciousness, nature of intentionality, continuity vs. discontinuity). Empirical findings require philosophical interpretation and cannot settle metaphysical questions about intelligence's essential nature.
