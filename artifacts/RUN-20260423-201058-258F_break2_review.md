II. WHAT IS ESTABLISHED

Marfan syndrome results from FBN1 mutations causing fibrillin-1 deficiency, leading to weakened extracellular matrix architecture and secondary TGF-β pathway dysregulation. Loeys-Dietz syndrome arises from TGFBR1/TGFBR2 mutations causing direct alterations in TGF-β signaling. Both manifest progressive aortic root aneurysm formation with life-threatening dissection risk, cardiac valve abnormalities (particularly mitral valve prolapse), and cardiomyopathy. Patients exhibit documented increased arrhythmic risk that exceeds what structural valve disease alone would predict.

Connexin 43 is the primary gap junction protein in cardiac myocytes and vascular smooth muscle cells, essential for electrical coupling that coordinates myocardial contraction and metabolic coordination that enables synchronized vascular responses. Connexin 43 remodeling—altered expression levels, abnormal subcellular localization, or altered phosphorylation status affecting channel gating—is an established arrhythmogenic mechanism in ischemic heart disease, heart failure, and various cardiomyopathies. Importantly, TGF-β signaling has been shown to regulate connexin 43 expression in multiple tissue contexts outside cardiovascular connective tissue disorders, and mechanical stress modulates connexin biology through mechanotransduction pathways in other systems. Validated animal models (Fbn1 C1039G/+, Fbn1 C1041G/+, Tgfbr1 M318R/+ mice) recapitulate key cardiovascular features of human syndromes.

If Cx43 deletion in vascular smooth muscle on Marfan background worsens aneurysm progression or accelerates mortality, connexin dysfunction is pathogenic. If deletion has no effect or paradoxically improves outcomes, connexin changes (even if present) are epiphenomenal or compensatory rather than causally contributing.

**Key uncertainties**:

4. **Whether therapeutic modulation is viable**: Depends on tool quality (current gap junction-modulating reagents have significant limitations) and therapeutic window (whether intervention must occur early in disease course or remains effective after significant pathology). 5. **Whether connexin changes are expression-based, localization-based, or phosphorylation-based**: Different molecular mechanisms demand different investigation and intervention strategies. Human tissue characterization must include all three dimensions.
