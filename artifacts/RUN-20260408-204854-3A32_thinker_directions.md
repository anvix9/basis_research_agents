Together, these directions suggest the field is ready for what might be called 'post-categorical intelligence studies' — not abandoning categories but understanding them as pragmatic tools rather than natural kinds, and developing richer frameworks that embrace temporal, situational, collective, and phenomenological dimensions that categorical approaches systematically miss. This doesn't solve the research problem but transforms it from 'What are the categories of intelligence?' to 'How do we understand a phenomenon too complex, contextual, and multidimensional for stable categorical capture?'

## Challenged Assumptions

- **Assumption:** Intelligence is fundamentally about accuracy, correctness, and truth-tracking — getting things right
  **Challenge:** What if intelligence is fundamentally about error-correction, recovery, and robustness — getting things less wrong over time?
  **If wrong:** This inverts evaluation criteria. Ancient nous was defined by infallible grasp of first principles; modern IQ by correct answers. But evolutionary and computational perspectives suggest intelligence is about handling uncertainty, recovering from mistakes, adapting to feedback. This would make phronesis (practical wisdom adjusting to circumstances) more fundamental than nous (immediate certainty), and would explain why artificial systems with high accuracy but no error-recovery feel unintelligent. Changes what counts as intelligence from static correctness to dynamic adaptation.

- **Assumption:** Human intelligence is the relevant frame of reference — other intelligences (animal, artificial, collective) are measured against human standard
  **Challenge:** What if human intelligence is evolutionary peculiarity, and comparative frame should be broader metacognitive space including all information-processing systems?
  **If wrong:** Ancient scala naturae and modern anthropocentrism both assume human intelligence is reference point. But comparative cognition (PROP-A039F8BA) shows convergent evolution of intelligence in phylogenetically distant taxa; AI demonstrates non-biological intelligence. Reframing humans as one intelligence type among many, with characteristic strengths/weaknesses rather than pinnacle position, would require completely different categorization — not 'human faculties' but 'intelligence dimensions' that humans, corvids, cephalopods, and computers occupy differently. Ancient hierarchical positioning and modern human-normed IQ both collapse.

- **Assumption:** Intelligence categories are discovered natural kinds — we're finding real joints in nature of cognition
  **Challenge:** What if intelligence categories are pragmatic instruments — we're building tools for different purposes (education, selection, understanding) that create rather than discover distinctions?
  **If wrong:** Synthesis notes social-constructivist critique (IMP-58DA3FE3, IMP-8C6C57BB) but treats it as competing with realism. What if neither realism nor constructivism is right — categories are real (have effects, constrain thought) but not natural kinds (don't carve nature at joints)? This would explain category proliferation (five virtues, g-factor, multiple intelligences) as different tools for different tasks, not competing theories of same thing. Makes question 'Which categorization is correct?' meaningless; right question becomes 'Which categorization serves which purpose?' Changes intellectual project from discovering truth to evaluating utility.

- **Assumption:** More intelligence is better — quantity and development of intelligence are inherently valuable
  **Challenge:** What if intelligence has optimal ranges and trade-offs — more isn't always better, and high intelligence comes with costs?
  **If wrong:** Ancient virtue framework suggested excellence had proper measure (Aristotle's doctrine of mean), but this wasn't applied to intelligence itself — more nous, sophia was assumed better. Modern enhancement culture assumes more intelligence is better. But if intelligence is metabolically expensive, creates social challenges, or trades off against other capacities, then optimal intelligence might be mid-range. Would explain why evolution hasn't maximized intelligence in all species, and might reveal ancient phronesis (practical wisdom, not just maximum theoretical wisdom) captured something modern frameworks miss.

- **Assumption:** Intelligence is sufficiently stable to sustain categorical frameworks — individual intelligence is relatively fixed, types are enduring
  **Challenge:** What if intelligence is radically context-dependent and fluid — individuals move between intelligence modes, categories are unstable?
  **If wrong:** Ancient categories assumed stable faculties, modern IQ assumes stable general intelligence. But developmental, situational, and cultural variations might be more fundamental than stability. Same person might be highly intelligent in familiar context, unintelligent in novel one; ancient categories might describe situations not persons. This would explain why intelligence is so hard to pin down — we're trying to categorize something inherently unstable. Makes question shift from 'What intelligence types exist?' to 'What contextual configurations elicit what cognitive performances?'
