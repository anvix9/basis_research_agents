## Overview

The proposals address the profound methodological and doctrinal gap between existing international humanitarian law frameworks and the specific phenomenon of systematic attacks on Palestinian universities and students from 1922-2025. Current law provides general civilian protection (Geneva Conventions, Rome Statute) and emerging cultural property frameworks (UNESCO-ICC cooperation), but lacks doctrine specifically addressing the destruction of higher education infrastructure combined with massacre of university students as a distinct category of violence. The proposals span three primary strategies: (1) applying existing legal frameworks through novel evidentiary methodologies that leverage documented patterns of educational destruction; (2) developing sui generis legal categories that recognize universities' hybrid character as simultaneously civilian objects, cultural heritage sites, and sites of ongoing intellectual production; and (3) employing post-colonial analytical frameworks to examine how British Mandate legal structures create structural barriers to accountability. Several proposals directly address the enforcement gap created by ICC political constraints and Gaza's contested legal classification, proposing procedural innovations and alternative jurisdictional pathways. The most promising approaches combine doctrinal innovation with rigorous historical pattern analysis across the 1922-2025 timeframe, recognizing that proving systematic scholasticide requires both demonstrating continuous policy across administrations and developing legal categories adequate to the specific harm of educational annihilation.
