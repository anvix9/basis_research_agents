## Phases

### Ancient Greek Foundations (~600 BCE - 300 CE)
Establishment of foundational distinctions between different forms of intelligence: nous (intellect/intuitive reason), dianoia (discursive reasoning), episteme (scientific knowledge), techne (craft knowledge), phronesis (practical wisdom). Human intelligence positioned hierarchically between divine nous and animal sensation. Major schools: Pre-Socratics, Plato, Aristotle, Stoics, Neoplatonists.
*Transition driver: Rise of Christianity and shift from cosmological to theological frameworks of intelligence*

### Medieval Theological Synthesis (300-1400 CE)
Integration of Greek philosophical categories with Abrahamic theological frameworks. Intelligence hierarchies reframed through divine illumination theories. Three-tier structure: divine intellect (uncreated), angelic intellect (created but immaterial), human intellect (created, embodied, dependent on sensation). Arabic philosophers preserved and expanded Aristotelian frameworks. Scholastics debated nature of intellect as substance vs. faculty.
*Transition driver: Recovery of Aristotelian texts, rise of empiricism, printing press democratizing knowledge*

### Early Modern Mechanization (1400-1700)
Mechanical philosophy challenged hierarchical intelligence models. Descartes' radical dualism separated res cogitans from res extensa, creating mind-body problem that dominated subsequent discourse. Animal intelligence reduced to mechanical automata. Human intelligence uniquely defined by rational soul. Emergence of sensationalist epistemologies (Locke, Hume) grounding intelligence in experience rather than innate forms.
*Transition driver: Enlightenment empiricism, natural history classification systems, evolutionary thinking*

### Enlightenment Naturalization (1700-1859)
Intelligence categories increasingly naturalized. Faculty psychology proliferated distinctions (memory, judgment, imagination, reason). Comparative anatomy and natural history placed human intelligence on continuum with animals. Emergence of psychology as distinct discipline. Phrenology attempted material basis for mental faculties. German idealism (Kant, Hegel) reasserted transcendental categories while acknowledging empirical psychology.
*Transition driver: Darwin's evolutionary theory forcing reconceptualization of human intelligence as continuous with nature*

### Evolutionary Framework Emergence (1859-1900)
Darwin's evolutionary theory fundamentally repositioned human intelligence as evolved capacity continuous with animal cognition. Debate between discontinuity theorists (humans uniquely rational) and continuity theorists (intelligence differs by degree). Comparative psychology emerged. Spencer's universal evolutionism applied to mental development. Instinct vs. intelligence debates. Galton initiated psychometric tradition, measuring individual differences.
*Transition driver: Experimental psychology, psychometrics, behaviorist rejection of mentalistic categories*

### Psychometric Reification (1900-1950)
Intelligence operationalized through testing (Binet, Terman, Wechsler). 'General intelligence' (g factor) identified through factor analysis (Spearman). Intelligence reified as measurable, heritable trait. Multiple intelligence theories emerged (Thurstone's primary mental abilities). Behaviorism rejected internal mental states, focused on observable learning. Gestalt psychology emphasized holistic cognition. Nature vs. nurture debates intensified, often intertwined with eugenics.
*Transition driver: Cognitive revolution rejecting behaviorism, computer metaphor for mind, information processing theories*

### Cognitive Revolution and Expansion (1950-1980)
Mind reconceived as information processor. Computational theory of mind (Turing, Newell, Simon). Chomsky's critique of behaviorism revived rationalist nativism. Artificial intelligence research forced operational definitions. Piaget's developmental stages. Gardner's multiple intelligences challenged g factor dominance. Ethology documented animal intelligence (tool use, communication). Intentionality debates (Searle, Dennett) questioned computational sufficiency.
*Transition driver: Neuroscience imaging technologies, evolutionary psychology, embodied cognition critiques*

### Neuroscientific and Evolutionary Integration (1980-2000)
Brain imaging (PET, fMRI) linked cognitive functions to neural substrates. Evolutionary psychology framed intelligence as adaptive mechanisms. Emotional intelligence (Goleman) expanded beyond cognitive measures. Consciousness studies emerged as distinct field (Chalmers' hard problem). Connectionism challenged symbolic AI. Animal cognition research demonstrated theory of mind, episodic memory, culture in non-humans. Social intelligence recognized as distinct category.
*Transition driver: AI deep learning breakthroughs, comparative cognition explosion, cultural intelligence research*

### Contemporary Pluralization and AI Challenge (2000-present)
Radical pluralization of intelligence categories. Cultural intelligence studies question universal standards. Plant intelligence, microbial intelligence, collective intelligence recognized. AI systems achieve superhuman performance in specific domains, forcing reconceptualization. Embodied, extended, enactive cognition theories challenge brain-centric models. Consciousness remains contested (Global Workspace, Integrated Information Theory). Human intelligence decentered: no longer apex but one form among many natural and artificial intelligences.
*Transition driver: Ongoing: AI capabilities expanding, astrobiology considering alien intelligence, biosemiotics expanding cognition concepts*

## Key Actors

- **Plato** [Ancient Greek Foundations]: Established tripartite soul (reason, spirit, appetite) and theory of Forms positioning intellectual knowledge as highest form
- **Aristotle** [Ancient Greek Foundations]: Systematized intelligence categories (nous, episteme, techne, phronesis, sophia); positioned humans as rational animals bridging material and divine
- **Thomas Aquinas** [Medieval Theological Synthesis]: Synthesized Aristotelian psychology with Christian theology; distinguished active from passive intellect
- **René Descartes** [Early Modern Mechanization]: Established radical mind-body dualism; denied intelligence to animals while attributing rational soul uniquely to humans
- **John Locke** [Early Modern Mechanization]: Empiricist account of mind as 'blank slate'; grounded intelligence in sensory experience rather than innate ideas
- **Immanuel Kant** [Enlightenment Naturalization]: Identified transcendental categories structuring experience; distinguished phenomena from noumena
- **Charles Darwin** [Evolutionary Framework Emergence]: Evolutionary theory forcing reconceptualization of human intelligence as continuous with animal cognition
- **Francis Galton** [Evolutionary Framework Emergence]: Founded psychometric tradition measuring individual intelligence differences; initiated hereditarian intelligence research
- **Alfred Binet** [Psychometric Reification]: Created first practical intelligence test; operationalized intelligence as measurable construct
- **Charles Spearman** [Psychometric Reification]: Identified general intelligence (g factor) through factor analysis; established dominant psychometric paradigm
- **B.F. Skinner** [Psychometric Reification]: Behaviorist rejection of internal mental states; operant conditioning as learning mechanism
- **Jean Piaget** [Cognitive Revolution and Expansion]: Developmental stage theory of intelligence; constructivist epistemology
- **Noam Chomsky** [Cognitive Revolution and Expansion]: Critique of behaviorism; revival of nativist theories through universal grammar
- **Howard Gardner** [Cognitive Revolution and Expansion]: Multiple intelligences theory challenging unitary g factor; expanded intelligence domains
- **Alan Turing** [Cognitive Revolution and Expansion]: Computational theory of mind; Turing Test as operational intelligence criterion

- **Daniel Goleman** [Neuroscientific and Evolutionary Integration]: Popularized emotional intelligence concept; expanded intelligence beyond cognitive domains

## Dead Ends

- **Phrenology - localizing mental faculties through skull measurement** (1800-1850)
  Reason: Crude materialization of faculty psychology; no valid correspondence between skull topography and mental capacities
  Lesson: Need for rigorous empirical validation; material basis of mind requires sophisticated neuroscience not surface anatomy
- **Behaviorist elimination of mental states** (1920-1960)
  Reason: Radical operationalism eliminated explanatory power for complex cognition; language acquisition, problem-solving, intentionality intractable without internal representations
  Lesson: Methodological rigor cannot eliminate explanatory constructs central to phenomena; mind cannot be reduced to stimulus-response associations
- **Purely symbolic AI (GOFAI - Good Old-Fashioned AI)** (1956-1990)
  Reason: Symbol grounding problem, frame problem, brittleness in real-world environments; intelligence requires embodiment and situated action
  Lesson: Abstract symbol manipulation insufficient for general intelligence; cognition cannot be separated from sensorimotor engagement with environment
- **Unitary g factor as complete intelligence model** (1904-present (contested))
  Reason: Cultural bias in tests, oversimplification of cognitive diversity, reification of statistical artifact as biological essence
  Lesson: Psychometric measures reflect test-specific competencies not universal intelligence essence; multiple forms of intelligence exist
- **Strong nativism - intelligence as entirely innate endowment** (1960s-1980s (linguistic nativism peak))
  Reason: Underestimated plasticity, developmental dynamics, cultural scaffolding; statistical learning and experience-dependent development demonstrated
  Lesson: Nature-nurture dichotomy false; development involves complex gene-environment interactions and cultural transmission
- **Consciousness as epiphenomenon irrelevant to intelligence** (1970s-1990s)
  Reason: Cannot explain phenomenal character of experience, binding problem, subjective perspective; intelligence may fundamentally require consciousness
  Lesson: Functional equivalence insufficient; first-person experience potentially constitutive of certain cognitive capacities

## Recurring Patterns

- **Human exceptionalism vs. continuity with nature**
  Appearances: Ancient Greek (rational soul vs. animal sensation), Medieval (imago dei vs. created beings), Modern (language, culture vs. instinct), Contemporary (consciousness, general intelligence vs. narrow AI)
  Why: Tension between empirical evidence of biological continuity and phenomenological sense of distinctive human capacities; constantly renegotiated as science extends capabilities to other systems
- **Unitary vs. plural intelligence**
  Appearances: Ancient (nous as single highest faculty vs. multiple intellectual virtues), Enlightenment (reason vs. multiple faculties), Psychometric (g factor vs. primary abilities), Contemporary (general intelligence vs. multiple intelligences, domain-specific adaptations)
  Why: Trade-off between theoretical parsimony and explanatory adequacy; empirical evidence supports both general factors and specialized capacities
- **Mind-body problem**
  Appearances: Ancient (soul-body dualism), Medieval (intellect-matter), Modern (res cogitans-res extensa), Contemporary (consciousness-neural correlates, hard problem)
  Why: Fundamental explanatory gap between subjective experience and objective description; third-person science cannot capture first-person phenomenology
- **Nature vs. nurture**
  Appearances: Ancient (innate ideas vs. sensory experience), Enlightenment (rationalism vs. empiricism), 19th century (instinct vs. learning), 20th century (heredity vs. environment), Contemporary (genetics vs. epigenetics, culture)
  Why: Dichotomy dissolves with each resolution but reappears at finer grain; development always involves complex interactions
- **Intelligence as computational vs. embodied**
  Appearances: Ancient (nous as separate substance vs. hylomorphic form), Modern (Cartesian dualism vs. sensationalism), Cognitive revolution (information processing vs. embodied cognition), AI (symbolic vs. connectionist, embodied robotics)
  Why: Tension between abstraction enabling generalization and specificity enabling effective action; both capture essential aspects
- **Universal vs. culturally situated intelligence**
  Appearances: Enlightenment (universal reason vs. cultural relativism), 19th century (racial hierarchies vs. cultural evolution), 20th century (IQ universality vs. cultural bias), Contemporary (cognitive universals vs. situated cognition)
  Why: Human cognition exhibits both universal constraints (evolutionary endowment) and cultural variation (developmental plasticity); impossible to fully separate

## Methods Evolution

Ancient Greeks employed dialectical philosophy and introspective phenomenology to categorize intelligence forms. Medieval scholastics added textual exegesis and systematic theology. Early modern period introduced mechanistic explanation and mathematical idealization (Galilean science). Enlightenment brought comparative anatomy and natural history classification. 19th century added evolutionary comparative method and experimental psychology. Psychometric era introduced statistical analysis, standardized testing, factor analysis. Cognitive revolution brought computational modeling, information theory, formal logic. Neuroscientific era added brain imaging (PET, fMRI), single-neuron recording, lesion studies. Contemporary period employs network analysis, machine learning, comparative cognition across species, developmental robotics, neurophenomenology bridging first-person and third-person data, cultural evolution modeling, astrobiology speculation.
