## Fundamental Whys

The original motivation behind international laws prohibiting genocide and war crimes was visceral horror at World War II atrocities and determination that 'never again' required legal mechanisms, not merely moral condemnation. Raphael Lemkin, who lost 49 family members in the Holocaust, coined 'genocide' because existing legal categories—murder, persecution—failed to capture the distinctive evil of attempting to destroy entire peoples. The 1948 Genocide Convention emerged from recognition that state sovereignty could no longer shield governments massacring their own populations or occupied peoples. The Geneva Conventions (1949) extended this logic to armed conflict: if civilians could be deliberately targeted or starved, if hospitals and schools could be bombed with impunity, then warfare itself destroyed the possibility of post-war peace. The fundamental 'why' was establishing that certain acts—even in war, even by victors—were beyond the pale of legal permissibility.

The specific protection of educational institutions emerged from understanding that destroying a people's schools destroys their future, not merely their present. The Universal Declaration's Article 26 establishing the right to education recognized that denying education denies human development and dignity. UNESCO's founding (1945) on the principle that 'wars begin in the minds of men' reflected understanding that education is both target and prevention of conflict. When David A. Lewis analyzed educational institution protections in 2006, he built on this foundation: schools matter not merely as buildings but as sites where collective identity, knowledge, and futurity are reproduced. The motivation to protect students specifically recognized that targeting the young destroys generational continuity—the essence of group destruction that defines genocide. The recent coinage 'scholasticide' (systematic destruction of educational systems) attempts to name what existing categories—civilian casualties, infrastructure damage—fail to capture: the deliberate erasure of intellectual and educational capacity as distinct from physical killing alone. This builds on century-old recognition that certain destructions are not incidental to war but constitute war crimes because they target the foundations of human collective life.

## Intellectual Genealogy

The intellectual foundations of this problem rest on three genealogical pillars constructed over the past century. The first pillar emerged from the ashes of World War II: the 1948 Genocide Convention and the 1949 Geneva Conventions established that certain acts—destruction of groups, targeting of civilians—were no longer simply moral wrongs but legal crimes prosecutable under international law. Raphael Lemkin coined 'genocide' in 1944 and successfully campaigned for its criminalization; the Geneva Conventions built on earlier Hague Conference foundations (1899, 1907) but fundamentally transformed the laws of war by extending civilian protections. These instruments created the legal vocabulary—'protected groups,' 'civilian objects,' 'specific intent'—that later scholars and tribunals would apply to new contexts. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) added human dignity as the philosophical foundation, establishing education as a fundamental right whose denial could constitute a rights violation.

The second pillar was constructed through jurisprudence: from the Nuremberg Trials through the ICTY (1993) and ICTR (1994) to the Rome Statute (1998) creating the permanent ICC. William A. Schabas traced this genealogy in his 1997 and subsequent works on international tribunals, showing how each institution refined definitions and evidentiary standards. The Krstić decision (2001, analyzed by Southwick 2005) established that massacres could constitute genocide when accompanied by specific intent to destroy a group—creating precedent for inferring intent from patterns of conduct. Machteld Boot's 2002 comprehensive treatise synthesized these developments, establishing how 'nullum crimen sine lege' (no crime without law) operated in international criminal law. Leena Grover's 2014 work on interpreting Rome Statute crimes built on Boot, establishing hermeneutic frameworks still used today. This jurisprudential line demonstrates progressive refinement: from defining crimes to proving them to interpreting them in new factual contexts.

The third pillar emerged from critical scholarship challenging who defines atrocities and how. Eve Tuck and K. Wayne Yang's 2012 'Decolonization is not a metaphor' insisted that colonial violence required understanding through indigenous frameworks, not Western humanitarian discourse. This built on decades of post-colonial legal scholarship examining how colonial legal structures persist (as Edoardo Vitta documented in 1977 regarding Israeli law's inheritance from the Mandate). Catherine Lu's 2017 work on colonial justice synthesized these threads, arguing that colonial atrocities like genocide required decolonial justice frameworks. Michael Birnhack's 2021 post-colonial IP framework demonstrated methodologies for examining how British Mandate legal structures continue to operate. Meanwhile, psychological research by Paul Slovic (2007) and Kteily & Bruneau (2015) on dehumanization and psychic numbing provided mechanisms explaining how mass violence becomes psychologically possible—showing that legal prohibition alone cannot prevent atrocities without addressing underlying dehumanization. These three pillars—legal prohibition, jurisprudential refinement, and critical decolonial analysis—converge in contemporary scholarship attempting to name and prosecute systematic violence against Palestinians, including the neologism 'scholasticide' building on this century-long intellectual foundation.

## Original Definitions

- **genocide** (United Nations Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide, 1948): Acts committed with intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnical, racial or religious group, including killing members, causing serious bodily or mental harm, deliberately inflicting conditions calculated to destroy the group, preventing births, and forcibly transferring children
- **war crimes** (Geneva Conventions (1949) and Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court, 1949): Grave breaches of the Geneva Conventions and other serious violations of the laws and customs applicable in international and non-international armed conflict, including willful killing, torture, willfully causing great suffering, unlawful deportation, extensive destruction not justified by military necessity, and attacking civilians or civilian objects
- **crimes against humanity** (Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court, 1998): Widespread or systematic attack directed against any civilian population, with knowledge of the attack, including murder, extermination, deportation, imprisonment, torture, sexual violence, persecution, and other inhumane acts
- **civilian objects** (Geneva Conventions Additional Protocol I, 1977): All objects which are not military objectives, including educational institutions, hospitals, and cultural property, which may not be made the object of attack or reprisals
- **right to education** (Universal Declaration of Human Rights, Article 26, 1948): Everyone has the right to education, which shall be free at least in elementary stages, directed to full development of human personality and strengthening of respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms
- **decolonization** (Eve Tuck and K. Wayne Yang, 2012): The repatriation of Indigenous land and life; it is not a metaphor for other things we want to do to improve our societies and schools
- **specific intent (genocidal intent)** (International Court of Justice in Application of the Genocide Convention, 2015): The mental element requiring that the perpetrator clearly sought to produce the act charged, with intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a protected group as such, which can be inferred from patterns of conduct
- **psychic numbing** (Paul Slovic, 2007): The psychological phenomenon whereby caring people who exert great effort to rescue individual victims become numbly indifferent to the plight of individuals who are 'one of many' in a much greater problem
- **scholasticide** (Contemporary Palestinian and solidarity scholars (term emerging in 2010s, formalized in 2024-2025 scholarship), 2024): The systematic destruction of educational infrastructure, including schools and universities, and the targeted killing of students and educators, as a form of structural violence against intellectual and educational capacity
- **educide** (Dalal Iriqat and Rula Alousi, 2025): The devastating effects of ongoing conflict on an education system through systematic destruction of educational infrastructure and targeted killing of students and educators
